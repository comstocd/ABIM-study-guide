1. Anaphylaxis ‚Äî Recognition

‚ñº

Definition

Severe, potentially life-threatening systemic allergic reaction with rapid onset (usually minutes to hours after exposure).

Diagnostic Criteria (Any ONE of the following)

üö® Anaphylaxis Criteria
  1. Acute onset with skin/mucosal involvement (hives, pruritus, flushing, lip/tongue swelling) PLUS respiratory compromise OR hypotension/end-organ dysfunction
  2. Two or more rapidly after exposure to likely allergen:
    • Skin-mucosal involvement
    • Respiratory compromise
    • Hypotension or symptoms of end-organ dysfunction
    • GI symptoms (cramping, vomiting)
  3. Hypotension after exposure to known allergen (SBP <90 or >30% decrease)

Common Triggers

CategoryExamples
FoodsPeanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, milk, eggs, wheat, soy
MedicationsPenicillins, NSAIDs, contrast media, anesthetics
Insect stingsHymenoptera (bees, wasps, hornets, fire ants)
LatexHealthcare workers, spina bifida patients
ExerciseMay be food-dependent
IdiopathicNo identifiable cause (~20%)

Clinical Features by System

SystemManifestationsFrequency

RespiratoryDyspnea, wheezing, stridor, throat tightness70%
CardiovascularHypotension, tachycardia, syncope45%

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Point

Skin findings may be ABSENT in 10-20% of anaphylaxis cases. Don't exclude anaphylaxis based solely on absence of hives.

2. Anaphylaxis ‚Äî Treatment

‚ñº

Immediate Management

‚úì First-Line Treatment: EPINEPHRINE
  • Route: IM (anterolateral thigh) ‚Äî NOT subcutaneous
  • Dose: 0.3-0.5 mg (1:1000 concentration = 1 mg/mL) = 0.3-0.5 mL
  • Pediatric: 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.5 mg)
  • May repeat every 5-15 minutes if inadequate response
  • No absolute contraindications in anaphylaxis

Additional Measures

  1. Position: Supine with legs elevated (if hypotensive); sitting if respiratory distress
  2. Remove trigger if possible (stop infusion, remove stinger)
  3. Call for help ‚Äî activate emergency response
  4. Oxygen: High-flow
  5. IV fluids: 1-2 L NS bolus for hypotension
  6. Airway management: Early intubation if airway compromise

Adjunctive Medications (NOT First-Line)

MedicationRoleNotes
H1 antihistamines Skin symptoms (hives, pruritus) Diphenhydramine 25-50mg; does NOT treat hypotension or airway
H2 antihistamines May add to H1 blockers Famotidine 20mg IV
Glucocorticoids May prevent biphasic reaction Methylprednisolone 125mg IV; NOT immediate benefit
Bronchodilators Bronchospasm Albuterol nebulizer
Glucagon Beta-blocker refractory 1-5mg IV; for patients on beta-blockers not responding to epinephrine
üö´ Common Errors
  • Giving antihistamines INSTEAD of epinephrine
  • Delaying epinephrine
  • Using subcutaneous instead of IM route
  • Using IV epinephrine inappropriately (can cause arrhythmias)

Observation & Biphasic Reactions

  • Observe 4-6 hours minimum (longer if severe or history of biphasic)
  • Biphasic reaction: Recurrence of symptoms after initial resolution (1-20% of cases)
  • Usually within 8-12 hours; can occur up to 72 hours
  • Risk factors: Severe initial reaction, delayed epinephrine, need for multiple doses

Discharge Planning

  • Prescribe epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen, Auvi-Q)
  • Educate on use and when to use
  • Referral to allergist for trigger identification
  • Medic-Alert bracelet
  • Anaphylaxis action plan

3. Drug Allergy ‚Äî Overview

‚ñº

Types of Drug Reactions

TypeMechanismTimingExamples
Type I (IgE) IgE-mediated mast cell activation Minutes to hours Anaphylaxis, urticaria, angioedema
Type II (Cytotoxic) IgG/IgM antibodies against cell surface Days Hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia
Type III (Immune complex) Antigen-antibody complexes 1-3 weeks Serum sickness, vasculitis
Type IV (Delayed/T-cell) T-cell mediated Days to weeks Contact dermatitis, DRESS, SJS/TEN

Important Distinctions

  • Drug allergy: Immune-mediated (requires prior sensitization)
  • Drug intolerance: Non-immune (e.g., GI upset from erythromycin)
  • Drug toxicity: Predictable based on pharmacology
  • Drug idiosyncrasy: Unpredictable, non-immune (e.g., G6PD hemolysis)

Drugs Most Commonly Causing Allergic Reactions

  • Beta-lactam antibiotics (penicillins, cephalosporins)
  • Sulfonamides
  • NSAIDs
  • Radiocontrast media
  • Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine)
  • Allopurinol
  • Chemotherapy agents

4. Penicillin Allergy

‚ñº

Epidemiology

  • ~10% of patients report penicillin allergy
  • <1% have true IgE-mediated allergy when tested
  • IgE sensitization wanes over time (~50% lose sensitivity after 5 years, ~80% after 10 years)

History Assessment

üìã Key Questions
  • What was the reaction? (rash, hives, anaphylaxis, GI symptoms)
  • How long ago?
  • How long after taking the drug?
  • Were other drugs given simultaneously?
  • Has patient tolerated penicillins since?

Risk Stratification

Risk LevelHistoryAction
Low Non-specific rash, GI symptoms, unknown/family history, remote (>10 years) Consider direct challenge or penicillin skin testing
Moderate Urticaria, pruritic rash within hours-days Penicillin skin testing before use
High Anaphylaxis, angioedema, bronchospasm, SJS/TEN, DRESS Avoid penicillins; use alternative; allergist referral

Cross-Reactivity

‚ö†Ô∏è Penicillin-Cephalosporin Cross-Reactivity
  • Overall cross-reactivity is ~2% (lower than historically thought)
  • Higher with similar R-group side chains (1st gen cephalosporins)
  • Very low cross-reactivity with 3rd/4th generation cephalosporins, carbapenems, aztreonam
  • Aztreonam: Safe in penicillin allergy EXCEPT cross-reacts with ceftazidime (identical side chain)

Penicillin Skin Testing

  • Negative predictive value ~99% for IgE-mediated reactions
  • If negative ‚Üí safe to give penicillin (with initial observation)
  • If positive ‚Üí avoid or consider desensitization
  • Does NOT predict non-IgE reactions (SJS, DRESS)

Penicillin Allergy Delabeling

‚úì Benefits of Delabeling
  • Access to first-line antibiotics
  • Reduced use of broad-spectrum alternatives
  • Lower cost
  • Reduced antimicrobial resistance
  • Better outcomes (e.g., surgical prophylaxis)

5. Severe Cutaneous Drug Reactions

‚ñº

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)

üö® Medical Emergency

Severe mucocutaneous reaction with epidermal necrosis and detachment

Definition by BSA Involvement

  • SJS: <10% body surface area detachment
  • SJS/TEN overlap: 10-30% BSA
  • TEN: >30% BSA

Clinical Features

  • Prodrome: Fever, malaise, upper respiratory symptoms
  • Painful skin with erythematous/purpuric macules
  • Mucosal involvement (‚â•2 sites): oral, ocular, genital
  • Nikolsky sign positive (epidermis separates with lateral pressure)
  • Atypical targets with necrotic centers

Common Culprits

  • Allopurinol (highest risk)
  • Sulfonamides (TMP-SMX)
  • Anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine, phenobarbital)
  • NSAIDs (piroxicam)
  • Nevirapine

Management

  • Immediately discontinue causative drug
  • Supportive care in burn unit or ICU
  • Fluid/electrolyte management
  • Wound care
  • Ophthalmology consult (ocular complications)
  • Role of systemic steroids, IVIG, cyclosporine controversial

DRESS Syndrome

Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms

Clinical Features

  • Onset: 2-8 weeks after drug initiation
  • Fever, diffuse morbilliform rash, facial edema
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Eosinophilia and/or atypical lymphocytes
  • Internal organ involvement: Liver (most common), kidney, lung, heart

Common Causes

  • Anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine)
  • Allopurinol
  • Sulfonamides
  • Vancomycin
  • Minocycline

Management

  • Stop offending drug
  • Systemic corticosteroids for severe cases
  • Monitor for organ involvement (LFTs, renal function, cardiac)
  • Prolonged course; may flare during steroid taper
‚ö†Ô∏è HLA Associations
  • HLA-B*5701: Abacavir hypersensitivity ‚Äî screen before use
  • HLA-B*1502: Carbamazepine-induced SJS/TEN in Asian populations ‚Äî screen before use
  • HLA-B*5801: Allopurinol-induced SJS/TEN/DRESS (Asians, African Americans)

6. NSAID & Aspirin Reactions

‚ñº

Types of NSAID Reactions

TypeMechanismFeaturesCross-Reactivity
NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD) COX-1 inhibition ‚Üí leukotriene overproduction Asthma + nasal polyps + ASA/NSAID sensitivity All COX-1 inhibitors
NSAID-induced urticaria/angioedema COX-1 inhibition Urticaria/angioedema in patients with chronic urticaria All COX-1 inhibitors
Single NSAID-induced IgE-mediated (true allergy) Anaphylaxis, urticaria to specific NSAID Same drug or structurally similar only

AERD (Samter's Triad)

üìã Classic Triad
  1. Asthma (often severe)
  2. Chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps
  3. Respiratory reaction to aspirin/NSAIDs

Management

  • Avoid all COX-1 inhibitors
  • COX-2 selective inhibitors (celecoxib) are usually safe
  • Acetaminophen safe at low doses (<1000mg)
  • Aspirin desensitization if aspirin needed (e.g., CAD)
  • Leukotriene modifiers (montelukast)
  • Intranasal steroids
  • Consider biologic therapy for severe asthma

Contrast Media Reactions

Types

  • Immediate: Within 1 hour; urticaria, bronchospasm, hypotension
  • Delayed: 1 hour to 1 week; usually maculopapular rash

Premedication for Previous Immediate Reaction

‚úì Premedication Regimen
  • Prednisone 50mg PO at 13h, 7h, and 1h before procedure
  • Diphenhydramine 50mg 1h before
  • Use low-osmolar or iso-osmolar contrast

Note: Premedication reduces but doesn't eliminate risk (~10% breakthrough)

7. Urticaria

‚ñº

Definition

Wheals (hives): Raised, erythematous, pruritic plaques that blanch with pressure and resolve within 24 hours

Classification

TypeDurationCause
Acute <6 weeks Often identifiable: infection, food, drug, insect sting
Chronic spontaneous ‚â•6 weeks Usually idiopathic; rarely autoimmune
Chronic inducible ‚â•6 weeks Physical trigger: cold, pressure, vibration, cholinergic, solar

Chronic Spontaneous Urticaria

  • Most common form of chronic urticaria
  • Cause identified in <10%
  • Not typically allergic; extensive allergy testing not indicated
  • May be associated with autoimmune thyroid disease
  • Consider checking: CBC, TSH, CRP/ESR

Treatment Algorithm

‚úì Stepwise Approach
  1. Step 1: Second-generation H1 antihistamine (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) ‚Äî standard dose daily
  2. Step 2: Increase to 2-4√ó standard dose (off-label but guideline-recommended)
  3. Step 3: Add H2 blocker OR leukotriene antagonist OR switch antihistamine
  4. Step 4: Add omalizumab (anti-IgE; FDA-approved for chronic urticaria)
  5. Step 5: Add cyclosporine (refractory cases)

Key Points

  • Avoid first-generation antihistamines (sedation, anticholinergic effects) ‚Äî especially in elderly
  • Corticosteroids for short-term flares only; NOT for chronic management
  • Avoid NSAIDs if they exacerbate urticaria

8. Angioedema

‚ñº

Definition

Deep swelling of dermis, subcutaneous, or submucosal tissue. Often affects face, lips, tongue, throat, extremities, genitalia, bowel.

Types

TypeMechanismKey Features
Allergic/Mast cell-mediated Histamine release Usually with urticaria; responds to antihistamines/epinephrine
ACE inhibitor-induced Bradykinin accumulation NO urticaria; doesn't respond to antihistamines/steroids
Hereditary (HAE) C1-INH deficiency/dysfunction NO urticaria; recurrent; family history; can be life-threatening
Acquired C1-INH deficiency Autoantibodies or consumption Associated with lymphoproliferative disorders; older onset

ACE Inhibitor Angioedema

üö® Important Facts
  • Occurs in 0.1-0.7% of patients on ACEi
  • 5√ó more common in Black patients
  • Can occur at any time (even after years of use)
  • Usually affects head/neck; can be life-threatening
  • NO urticaria
  • Does NOT respond to epinephrine, antihistamines, or steroids

Management

  • Stop ACE inhibitor permanently
  • Supportive care, airway management
  • Consider icatibant or C1-INH concentrate for severe cases
  • Do NOT substitute ARB (small cross-reactivity risk; avoid if severe reaction)

Hereditary Angioedema (HAE)

Types

  • Type 1 (85%): Low C1-INH level and function
  • Type 2 (15%): Normal level, low function
  • Type 3 (rare): Normal C1-INH; often Factor XII mutation

Diagnosis

üìã Laboratory Findings
  • Low C4 (screening test ‚Äî low during and between attacks)
  • Low C1-INH level (Type 1) or function (Type 2)
  • C3 is normal (unlike acquired deficiency)

Triggers

  • Trauma, surgery, dental procedures
  • Stress, infections
  • Estrogen (OCPs, pregnancy)
  • ACE inhibitors (contraindicated)

Treatment

SettingTreatment
Acute attack C1-INH concentrate (Berinert, Cinryze), icatibant (bradykinin B2 antagonist), ecallantide (kallikrein inhibitor), fresh frozen plasma
Prophylaxis (long-term) C1-INH concentrate, lanadelumab (anti-kallikrein), berotralstat (oral kallikrein inhibitor)
Short-term prophylaxis C1-INH concentrate before procedures
üö´ Ineffective in HAE/Bradykinin-Mediated

Epinephrine, antihistamines, and corticosteroids do NOT work for HAE or ACEi-induced angioedema

9. Primary Immunodeficiency ‚Äî Overview

‚ñº

Warning Signs (Adults)

‚ö†Ô∏è When to Suspect Immunodeficiency
  • ‚â•2 serious bacterial infections (sepsis, meningitis, osteomyelitis)
  • ‚â•3 sinus/ear/pneumonia infections per year requiring antibiotics
  • Infections with unusual organisms
  • Recurrent deep abscesses
  • Infections requiring prolonged or IV antibiotics
  • Family history of immunodeficiency
  • Autoimmune disease with infections

Categories of Primary Immunodeficiency

DefectTypical InfectionsExamples
B-cell (antibody) Encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae), Giardia CVID, X-linked agammaglobulinemia, IgA deficiency
T-cell Viruses, fungi, Pneumocystis, mycobacteria DiGeorge, SCID
Phagocyte Bacteria (Staph, Serratia, Aspergillus) Chronic granulomatous disease (CGD)
Complement Neisseria (meningococcus), encapsulated bacteria, autoimmune disease Terminal complement deficiency

Initial Workup

  • CBC with differential (lymphopenia, neutropenia)
  • Quantitative immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM)
  • Specific antibody responses (tetanus, pneumococcal titers)
  • Complement: CH50 (total), C3, C4
  • HIV testing

10. Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)

‚ñº

Overview

  • Most common symptomatic primary immunodeficiency in adults
  • Typically diagnosed in 2nd-4th decade
  • Heterogeneous group of disorders

Clinical Features

  • Recurrent sinopulmonary infections (pneumonia, sinusitis, bronchiectasis)
  • GI infections (Giardia, C. difficile)
  • Autoimmune manifestations (ITP, AIHA, thyroiditis)
  • Granulomatous disease (lungs, liver, spleen)
  • Increased risk of lymphoma and gastric cancer
  • Splenomegaly, lymphadenopathy

Diagnosis

üìã Diagnostic Criteria
  • Low IgG (at least 2 SD below mean)
  • Low IgA and/or IgM
  • Poor vaccine responses (to protein and/or polysaccharide antigens)
  • Exclusion of other causes of hypogammaglobulinemia

Treatment

  • Immunoglobulin replacement (IV or subcutaneous) ‚Äî lifelong
  • Goal: trough IgG >500-800 mg/dL
  • Reduces frequency and severity of infections
  • Prompt antibiotic treatment for infections
  • Avoid live vaccines

11. Selective IgA Deficiency

‚ñº

Overview

  • Most common primary immunodeficiency (~1:500 in Caucasians)
  • Most patients are asymptomatic

Definition

IgA <7 mg/dL with normal IgG and IgM in patient ‚â•4 years old, after exclusion of other causes

Clinical Features

  • Many asymptomatic
  • Increased sinopulmonary and GI infections
  • Associated with autoimmune diseases (celiac, thyroiditis, SLE, RA)
  • Increased risk of allergies

Important Considerations

üö® Transfusion Reactions

Some IgA-deficient patients develop anti-IgA antibodies and can have anaphylactic reactions to blood products containing IgA.

  • Use IgA-depleted blood products or washed RBCs
  • Not all patients develop anti-IgA antibodies

Management

  • Most don't require specific treatment
  • Antibiotics for infections
  • IgG replacement NOT indicated (unless concurrent IgG subclass deficiency with infections)
  • Can progress to CVID in some patients

13. Allergic Rhinitis

‚ñº

Classification

  • Seasonal: Pollens (trees, grasses, weeds)
  • Perennial: Dust mites, mold, animal dander, cockroach
  • Intermittent: <4 days/week or <4 weeks
  • Persistent: ‚â•4 days/week and ‚â•4 weeks

Symptoms

  • Nasal congestion, rhinorrhea (clear)
  • Sneezing, nasal pruritus
  • Postnasal drip
  • Ocular symptoms (itching, tearing, redness)
  • Allergic shiners (dark circles under eyes)
  • Allergic salute (transverse nasal crease)

Treatment

‚úì Stepwise Approach
  1. Allergen avoidance (if feasible)
  2. Intranasal corticosteroids ‚Äî MOST EFFECTIVE for moderate-severe
    • Fluticasone, mometasone, budesonide
  3. Second-generation antihistamines ‚Äî for mild or PRN
    • Cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine
  4. Combination: Intranasal steroid + antihistamine
  5. Add-ons: Leukotriene antagonist, intranasal antihistamine (azelastine)
  6. Allergen immunotherapy ‚Äî for refractory cases

Allergen Immunotherapy

  • Subcutaneous (SCIT): Weekly buildup ‚Üí monthly maintenance √ó 3-5 years
  • Sublingual (SLIT): Daily tablets; FDA-approved for grass, ragweed, dust mite
  • Only treatment that modifies disease course
  • Contraindicated in severe/uncontrolled asthma

14. Asthma ‚Äî Allergy Connection

‚ñº

Allergic Asthma

  • Most common asthma phenotype
  • IgE-mediated response to aeroallergens
  • Often associated with allergic rhinitis, atopic dermatitis
  • Elevated total IgE, specific IgE to allergens

Allergy Testing in Asthma

  • Skin prick testing: Preferred; rapid, sensitive
  • Specific IgE (ImmunoCAP): When skin testing not possible
  • Identify triggers for avoidance
  • Assess candidacy for immunotherapy

Biologic Therapies for Severe Asthma

DrugTargetIndication

‚ñº

Common Food Allergens ("Big 9")

  • Peanut
  • Tree nuts
  • Milk
  • Egg
  • Wheat
  • Soy
  • Fish
  • Shellfish
  • Sesame

Diagnosis

  • History is most important
  • Skin prick testing: High NPV; positive test doesn't confirm clinical allergy
  • Specific IgE: Same limitations as skin testing
  • Oral food challenge: Gold standard; performed under supervision

Management

  • Strict avoidance of allergen
  • Epinephrine auto-injector ‚Äî all patients with IgE-mediated food allergy
  • Education on label reading
  • Oral immunotherapy (OIT): FDA-approved for peanut (Palforzia) in children/adolescents
‚ö†Ô∏è Food-Dependent Exercise-Induced Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis occurs when exercise follows ingestion of specific food (often wheat, shellfish). Either alone doesn't cause symptoms.

  • Wasps, hornets, yellow jackets

  • Short course oral steroids if severe
  • Venom immunotherapy NOT routinely recommended (but can be considered)

17. Eosinophilic Disorders

‚ñº

Causes of Eosinophilia

üìã "NAACP" Mnemonic
  • Neoplasm (Hodgkin lymphoma, other malignancies)
  • Allergy/Atopy
  • Adison's disease (adrenal insufficiency)
  • Connective tissue disease (eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis)
  • Parasites (helminths)

Also: Drug reactions (DRESS), hypereosinophilic syndrome, eosinophilic GI disorders

Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)

  • Chronic immune-mediated esophageal disease
  • Symptoms: Dysphagia, food impaction (adults); feeding difficulties, vomiting (children)
  • Diagnosis: ‚â•15 eosinophils/hpf on esophageal biopsy (after PPI trial)
  • Treatment: PPI, topical steroids (swallowed fluticasone or budesonide), elimination diets, dilation

Hypereosinophilic Syndrome (HES)

  • Persistent eosinophilia >1500/ŒºL + organ damage
  • Affects heart, lungs, skin, nervous system, GI tract
  • Rule out secondary causes
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids, hydroxyurea, imatinib (if FIP1L1-PDGFRA+)

18. High-Yield Board Pearls

‚ñº

Anaphylaxis Pearls

  • üíé Epinephrine IM is FIRST-LINE ‚Äî no absolute contraindications in anaphylaxis
  • üíé Skin findings may be absent in 10-20% of anaphylaxis
  • üíé Antihistamines treat hives, NOT hypotension or airway compromise
  • üíé Biphasic reactions can occur up to 72 hours later; observe 4-6+ hours

Drug Allergy Pearls

  • üíé <1% of reported penicillin allergies are true IgE-mediated
  • üíé Penicillin-cephalosporin cross-reactivity is ~2% (not 10%)
  • üíé Aztreonam is safe in penicillin allergy (except cross-reacts with ceftazidime)
  • üíé SJS/TEN: Allopurinol, sulfonamides, anticonvulsants; Nikolsky sign positive
  • üíé DRESS: 2-8 weeks after drug start; eosinophilia + liver involvement
  • üíé Screen HLA-B*5701 before abacavir

Angioedema Pearls

  • üíé ACEi angioedema: No urticaria; doesn't respond to epinephrine/antihistamines/steroids
  • üíé ACEi angioedema 5√ó more common in Black patients
  • üíé HAE: Low C4 (even between attacks); treat with C1-INH concentrate or icatibant
  • üíé Recurrent angioedema WITHOUT urticaria ‚Üí think bradykinin-mediated (HAE or ACEi)

Immunodeficiency Pearls

  • üíé CVID: Most common symptomatic primary immunodeficiency in adults; low IgG + poor vaccine responses
  • üíé Selective IgA deficiency: Most common overall; usually asymptomatic; risk of transfusion reactions
  • üíé Recurrent Neisseria infections ‚Üí check terminal complement (CH50)
  • üíé Encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. flu) ‚Üí antibody or complement deficiency

Other Pearls

  • üíé Intranasal corticosteroids are most effective treatment for allergic rhinitis
  • üíé Chronic urticaria: Up-dose antihistamines 2-4√ó before adding other agents; omalizumab for refractory
  • üíé AERD (Samter's triad): Asthma + nasal polyps + ASA/NSAID sensitivity; COX-2 inhibitors usually safe
  • üíé Venom immunotherapy reduces anaphylaxis risk from 30-60% to <5%
  • üíé All patients with IgE-mediated food allergy should have epinephrine auto-injector

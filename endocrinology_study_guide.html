Initiating Basal Insulin in T2D

  • Starting dose: 10 units or 0.1-0.2 units/kg/day
  • Titration: Increase by 2-4 units every 3-7 days until fasting glucose at target
  • Target fasting glucose: 80-130 mg/dL

Basal-Bolus Regimen (Type 1 or Advanced T2D)

  • Total daily dose (TDD): 0.4-0.5 units/kg/day (T1D), higher in T2D
  • Basal: 40-50% of TDD
  • Bolus: Divide remaining 50-60% among meals
  • Correction factor: 1800 √∑ TDD = mg/dL drop per unit (rapid insulin)
  • Carb ratio: 500 √∑ TDD = grams of carbs covered per unit
Example: TDD = 50 units
Correction factor = 1800 √∑ 50 = 36 (1 unit lowers BG by 36 mg/dL)
Carb ratio = 500 √∑ 50 = 10 (1 unit covers 10g carbs)
NPH has an unpredictable peak ‚Äî higher hypoglycemia risk. Long-acting analogs (glargine, degludec) are preferred for basal coverage.

Precipitants (The 6 I's)

Infection (most common), Insulin (missed/insufficient), Infarction (MI), Intoxication, Infant (pregnancy), Iatrogenic (steroids, SGLT2i)

Treatment Protocol

1. Fluids (First Priority)

  • Initial: 1-1.5 L NS in first hour
  • Then: 250-500 mL/hr NS (or 0.45% if corrected Na normal/high)
  • When glucose <200: Switch to D5 0.45% NS

2. Insulin

  • Regular insulin: 0.1 units/kg bolus, then 0.1 units/kg/hr infusion
  • OR: 0.14 units/kg/hr infusion (no bolus)
  • Target: Glucose drop 50-70 mg/dL/hr
  • When glucose <200: Reduce infusion to 0.02-0.05 units/kg/hr, keep glucose 150-200 until anion gap closes

3. Potassium

Serum K+Action
<3.3 mEq/LHold insulin, give 20-30 mEq/hr K+ until >3.3

Initial serum K+ is often normal or high despite total body K+ depletion. Insulin drives K+ into cells ‚Äî can precipitate fatal hypokalemia. Check K+ before starting insulin.

4. Bicarbonate

  • Only if pH <6.9: Give 100 mEq NaHCO3 in 400 mL water with 20 mEq KCl over 2 hours
  • Repeat until pH >7.0

Resolution Criteria

  • Glucose <200 mg/dL
  • Anion gap ‚â§12 mEq/L
  • Bicarbonate ‚â•15 mEq/L
  • pH >7.3
Transition to subcutaneous insulin when DKA resolved AND patient can eat. Give long-acting insulin 2 hours before stopping IV insulin to prevent rebound hyperglycemia/ketosis.

Euglycemic DKA

  • Definition: DKA with glucose <250 mg/dL
  • Causes: SGLT2 inhibitors (most common), pregnancy, fasting, alcohol
  • Management: Same as DKA but start dextrose-containing fluids earlier

Key Differences from DKA

  • More common in T2D (vs T1D in DKA)
  • Typically older patients with limited access to water
  • Profound dehydration (may have 8-10 L fluid deficit)
  • Higher mortality than DKA (10-20% vs 1-5%)

7. Hypoglycemia High-Yield

Definition

  • Level 1: Glucose <70 mg/dL (alert value)
  • Level 2: Glucose <54 mg/dL (clinically significant)

Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

  • Most common cause of hypothyroidism in iodine-sufficient areas

Subclinical Hypothyroidism

  • Definition: Elevated TSH, normal free T4
  • Treat if:
    • TSH >10 mU/L
    • Symptoms attributable to hypothyroidism
    • Anti-TPO positive (higher progression risk)
    • Pregnancy or planning pregnancy

16. Pheochromocytoma & Paraganglioma High-Yield

Overview

  • Pheochromocytoma: Catecholamine-secreting tumor from adrenal medulla
  • Paraganglioma: Extra-adrenal location (sympathetic chain)
  • ~40% are hereditary: MEN2, VHL, NF1, SDH mutations

Clinical Features

5 P's: Pressure (HTN), Pain (headache), Perspiration, Palpitations, Pallor
  • Hypertension: Paroxysmal (50%) or sustained (50%)
  • Classic triad: Headache, diaphoresis, palpitations
  • Episodic symptoms triggered by exercise, anesthesia, certain drugs
  • Hyperglycemia (catecholamines oppose insulin)

When to Suspect

  • Resistant hypertension
  • Adrenal incidentaloma
  • Hypertensive crisis with anesthesia/surgery
  • Syndromic features (MEN2, VHL, NF1)
  • Family history of pheochromocytoma

Diagnosis

  • First-line: Plasma free metanephrines OR 24-hour urine metanephrines and catecholamines

  • If positive: CT or MRI of abdomen/pelvis
  • If imaging negative: MIBG scan or 68Ga-DOTATATE PET (for metastatic/paraganglioma)

  • Alpha-blockade first: Phenoxybenzamine (irreversible) or doxazosin (10-14 days pre-op)

  • Then beta-blockade: Add after adequate alpha-blockade (prevents unopposed alpha stimulation and hypertensive crisis)
  • Volume expansion: High-salt diet, IV fluids (catecholamines cause volume contraction)
  • Surgery: Laparoscopic adrenalectomy (definitive)

  • Recommend for all patients with pheochromocytoma/paraganglioma

  • Perimenopause: Transition period with irregular cycles
  • Premature ovarian insufficiency: Menopause before age 40

Symptoms

  • Vasomotor: Hot flashes, night sweats
  • Genitourinary: Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urgency
  • Other: Sleep disturbance, mood changes

Hormone Therapy Indications

  • Bothersome vasomotor symptoms
  • Prevention of bone loss (when other options unsuitable)
  • Premature menopause (until average age of menopause)

Contraindications to Systemic HRT

  • Breast cancer (current or history)
  • Endometrial cancer
  • VTE or thrombophilia
  • Active liver disease
  • CAD, stroke
  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding
Key Points:
‚Ä¢ Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration
‚Ä¢ Initiate within 10 years of menopause or age <60 (lower CVD risk)
‚Ä¢ With intact uterus: estrogen + progestogen (prevents endometrial hyperplasia)
‚Ä¢ After hysterectomy: estrogen alone

Non-Hormonal Options for Vasomotor Symptoms

  • Paroxetine (Brisdelle) ‚Äî FDA approved for hot flashes
  • Venlafaxine, SSRIs
  • Gabapentin
  • Fezolinetant (NK3 receptor antagonist ‚Äî 2023 FDA approved)

  • Most common sex chromosome aneuploidy in males (1:500-1000)
  • Features: Tall stature, small firm testes, gynecomastia, infertility, learning disabilities

37. Carcinoid Tumors & Syndrome Boards Favorite

Overview

  • Well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumors
  • Common sites: GI tract (small bowel, appendix, rectum), lungs
  • Carcinoid syndrome: Occurs with hepatic metastases (or primary bronchial/ovarian)

Carcinoid Syndrome Features

"F-D-B-W": Flushing, Diarrhea, Bronchoconstriction, Wheezing (right-sided heart disease)
  • Flushing: Episodic, triggered by alcohol, stress, catecholamines
  • Diarrhea: Secretory (from serotonin)
  • Carcinoid heart disease: Right-sided valve fibrosis (tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonic stenosis)
  • Bronchoconstriction

Diagnosis

  • 24-hour urine 5-HIAA (serotonin metabolite) ‚Äî elevated
  • Chromogranin A ‚Äî elevated (nonspecific)
  • Imaging: CT/MRI, somatostatin receptor scintigraphy (Octreoscan), 68Ga-DOTATATE PET

Treatment

  • Surgery: Curative if localized
  • Somatostatin analogs: Octreotide LAR, lanreotide ‚Äî control symptoms, slow tumor growth
  • PRRT: Lutetium-177 DOTATATE for progressive disease
  • Carcinoid crisis prevention: Octreotide before surgery/procedures
Carcinoid crisis: Life-threatening hypotension, bronchospasm during surgery. Give octreotide 250-500 mcg IV before and during procedure.

42. Myxedema Coma Emergency

Clinical Features

  • Hypothermia (may be profound)
  • Altered mental status (confusion to coma)
  • Hyponatremia
  • Hypoglycemia, hypotension, bradycardia
  • Hypoventilation (CO2 retention)

Precipitants

  • Infection, cold exposure, medications (sedatives, narcotics)
  • Noncompliance with levothyroxine
  • Surgery, trauma, MI

Treatment

  • IV levothyroxine: Loading dose 200-400 mcg, then 50-100 mcg/day
  • IV hydrocortisone: 50-100mg Q8h (until adrenal insufficiency ruled out)
  • Supportive: Passive rewarming, ventilatory support, treat precipitant
Always give hydrocortisone with thyroid hormone ‚Äî treating hypothyroidism can precipitate adrenal crisis if underlying adrenal insufficiency exists.

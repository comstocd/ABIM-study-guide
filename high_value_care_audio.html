1. Screening Recommendations‚úì
Cancer Screening - Do

Lung cancer screening with low-dose CT for adults 50 to 80 with 20 pack-year smoking history who currently smoke or quit within 15 years.

Colon cancer screening starts at age 45. Options include colonoscopy every 10 years, FIT testing annually, or flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years.

Breast cancer screening with mammography every 1 to 2 years starting at age 40 to 50, continuing until at least 75.

Cervical cancer screening with Pap smear starting at age 21. Co-testing with HPV after age 30. Stop at 65 if adequate prior screening.

Cancer Screening - Don't

Do not screen for prostate cancer without shared decision-making. PSA screening has potential harms including overdiagnosis and overtreatment.

Do not screen for ovarian cancer in average-risk women. CA-125 and ultrasound are not recommended for routine screening.

Do not screen for pancreatic cancer in average-risk adults. There is no proven effective screening method.

Never recommend whole-body CT screening. It has high false positive rate, radiation exposure, and leads to unnecessary procedures.

When to Stop Screening

Stop cancer screening when life expectancy is less than 10 years. Benefits take years to accrue.

Stop colonoscopy after age 75, or 85 at maximum.

Stop Pap smear after age 65 with adequate prior screening.

Individualize mammography after 75. Stop if limited life expectancy.

2. Diagnostic Imaging‚úì
Avoid Unnecessary Imaging

No imaging for acute low back pain under 6 weeks without red flags. Red flags include trauma, neurologic deficits, cancer history, fever, IV drug use, osteoporosis, and age over 50 with new symptoms.

No imaging for uncomplicated headache with stable pattern without red flags. Red flags include thunderclap onset, neurologic signs, papilledema, fever, immunocompromise, and new onset after age 50.

No head CT or carotid imaging for typical vasovagal syncope. Focus on cardiac workup if concerning features.

Use Canadian CT Head Rule to avoid unnecessary CT for minor head injury. Low-risk patients with GCS 15, no loss of consciousness, no amnesia, and no vomiting do not need imaging.

Preoperative chest X-ray is not routine. Only obtain if acute cardiopulmonary symptoms or needed for specific surgical planning.

Clinical Decision Rules

Use Wells score for PE probability. Low probability with negative D-dimer rules out PE without CT angiography.

Use age-adjusted D-dimer for patients over 50. The cutoff is age times 10 micrograms per liter.

Use Ottawa Ankle Rules for ankle injury. No imaging needed if can bear weight and no tenderness over malleoli or midfoot.

Use PERC rule for very low-risk PE patients. If all PERC criteria negative, no further testing needed.

Incidental Findings

Follow Fleischner Society guidelines for pulmonary nodules. Many small nodules need no follow-up based on size and risk factors.

Follow Bosniak classification for renal cysts. Simple cysts need no follow-up.

Follow ACR guidelines for adrenal incidentalomas. Less than 1 centimeter with benign features need no follow-up.

3. Laboratory Testing‚úì
Avoid Routine Testing

Do not order daily labs in stable hospitalized patients. Reassess need before each order.

Do not order routine preoperative labs in healthy patients. Only order if clinically indicated.

Do not order BMP daily to monitor electrolytes unless actively repleting or on diuretics.

Do not check TSH as part of routine health screening. Only test if symptoms suggest thyroid disease.

Do not check vitamin D levels routinely. Only test if at high risk for deficiency or treating osteoporosis.

Avoid Unnecessary Markers

Do not use ESR and CRP to monitor chronic inflammation. They are nonspecific and do not guide treatment.

Do not order homocysteine or lipoprotein(a) routinely. They rarely change management.

Do not repeat positive antibody tests. ANA, anti-CCP, and similar antibodies do not fluctuate meaningfully.

Do not order tumor markers for screening or diagnosis in most cases. CA-125, CEA, CA 19-9 have limited diagnostic accuracy.

Transfusion Thresholds

Use restrictive transfusion with hemoglobin threshold of 7 grams per deciliter for most stable hospitalized patients.

Use threshold of 8 grams per deciliter for patients with cardiovascular disease.

Do not transfuse to normalize hemoglobin. Higher targets do not improve outcomes.

4. Cardiology‚úì
Cardiac Testing

Do not stress test asymptomatic patients at low cardiovascular risk. Baseline risk predicts outcomes better than stress test.

Do not stress test before low-risk surgery in asymptomatic patients with good functional capacity.

Do not repeat echocardiogram annually for stable heart failure. Repeat only if symptoms change or to guide therapy.

Do not order troponin in asymptomatic patients or for non-cardiac conditions.

Overuse to Avoid

Do not start dual antiplatelet therapy without clear indication. DAPT increases bleeding risk.

Do not continue proton pump inhibitors indefinitely with DAPT. Stop when bleeding risk decreases.

Do not use telemetry for low-risk patients. Telemetry rarely changes management and delays discharge.

Do not do carotid screening in asymptomatic patients. Screening does not improve outcomes without symptoms.

5. Pulmonology‚úì
Respiratory Testing

Do not obtain chest X-ray daily in stable ICU patients. Only image when clinical change occurs.

Do not check ABG when pulse oximetry is sufficient. Use ABG for acid-base assessment, not just oxygenation.

Do not order CT PE for low-probability patients with negative D-dimer.

COPD and Asthma

Do not prescribe chronic oral steroids for COPD. They do not improve outcomes and cause harm.

Do not use long-acting beta-agonists as monotherapy for asthma. Always combine with inhaled corticosteroid.

Do not continue supplemental oxygen if PaO2 has improved above 60 or saturation above 88 percent.

6. GI & Infectious Disease‚úì
GI Recommendations

Do not continue PPI indefinitely without reassessing indication. Limit to shortest duration necessary.

Do not test for C. diff in patients without diarrhea. Test only if 3 or more unformed stools in 24 hours.

Do not repeat EGD for GERD without alarm symptoms if initial scope was normal.

Do not repeat colonoscopy within 10 years after normal exam in average-risk patients.

Infectious Disease

Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria except in pregnancy or before urologic procedures.

Do not order urine culture for asymptomatic pyuria. Pyuria alone does not indicate infection.

Do not continue antibiotics beyond recommended duration. Longer courses increase resistance without benefit.

Do not give antibiotics for viral upper respiratory infections. Most are viral and resolve spontaneously.

Do not use broad-spectrum antibiotics when narrow-spectrum would suffice. De-escalate based on culture results.

7. Other Specialties‚úì
Neurology

Do not image for uncomplicated headache meeting migraine criteria.

Do not order EEG for simple syncope without seizure features.

Do not order carotid ultrasound for dizziness without focal neurologic symptoms.

Rheumatology

Do not order ANA without clinical suspicion for autoimmune disease. Positive ANA is common in healthy people.

Do not repeat ANA or anti-dsDNA to monitor lupus activity. Use clinical parameters instead.

Do not check uric acid during acute gout flare to diagnose gout. It can be falsely normal.

Endocrinology

Do not order HbA1c more frequently than every 3 months. It reflects 3-month glucose average.

Do not overtreat diabetes in elderly patients. Target A1c less than 8 is appropriate for many older adults.

Do not treat subclinical hypothyroidism with TSH under 10 in asymptomatic patients.

Nephrology

Do not place Foley catheters routinely for urine output monitoring. Use bladder scanner or timed voids instead.

Remove Foley catheters as soon as clinically appropriate. CAUTIs are the most common hospital-acquired infection.

Do not give IV fluids to patients who can take oral fluids. Oral hydration is safer and cheaper.

8. Hospital & Perioperative‚úì
Hospital Medicine

Do not place patients on bedrest unless medically necessary. Early mobilization improves outcomes.

Do not order routine blood typing unless transfusion is anticipated.

Do not order daily weights for patients without heart failure or volume management issues.

Do not wake patients for routine vital signs if hemodynamically stable. Prioritize sleep for healing.

Perioperative Care

Do not order preoperative chest X-ray, ECG, or labs unless clinically indicated by history or planned surgery.

Do not order preoperative stress testing for low-risk surgery or patients with good functional capacity.

Do not bridge anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation without mechanical valve. BRIDGE trial showed no benefit and increased bleeding.

Do not delay surgery to optimize stable chronic conditions. Preoperative optimization rarely changes outcomes.

Geriatrics

Avoid potentially inappropriate medications in elderly using Beers Criteria. Avoid benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, and NSAIDs when possible.

Deprescribe medications that are no longer indicated. Polypharmacy increases adverse events.

Focus on patient goals and values when making treatment decisions in elderly patients with limited life expectancy.

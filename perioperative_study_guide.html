Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI / Lee Index)

1 point each (6 total possible):
  1. High-risk surgery (intraperitoneal, intrathoracic, suprainguinal vascular)
  2. History of ischemic heart disease (MI, positive stress test, angina, nitrate use, Q waves)
  3. History of heart failure
  4. History of cerebrovascular disease (stroke or TIA)
  5. Diabetes requiring insulin

Step 2: Does patient have ACS? ‚Üí Delay surgery, treat ACS

Preoperative Testing

When to Order ECG

  • Known CAD, arrhythmia, PAD, CVD, structural heart disease
  • Intermediate/high-risk surgery with ‚â•1 RCRI risk factors
  • NOT routinely needed for low-risk surgery

When to Order Stress Testing

‚ö†Ô∏è Consider stress testing ONLY if:
  • Elevated risk (RCRI ‚â•2)
  • Poor functional capacity (<4 METs)
  • High-risk surgery
  • Result will change management
If stress testing within past 2 years was negative with no new symptoms ‚Üí no need to repeat
‚ùå Do NOT order cardiac catheterization just because of upcoming surgery. Revascularization is indicated only if independently warranted (ACS, left main disease, etc.)

Consider if:
  • New/worsening dyspnea of unknown cause
  • Known HF with worsening symptoms
  • Known severe valvular disease (reassess if >1 year)
NOT routinely indicated for noncardiac surgery

  • For any indication (HTN, CAD, HF, arrhythmia)

Preoperative Pulmonary Testing

‚ö†Ô∏è Routine preoperative PFTs are NOT recommended
  • Do not predict postoperative complications better than clinical assessment
  • No absolute PFT value precludes surgery
When PFTs may be helpful:
  • Undiagnosed dyspnea or cough
  • Known COPD with unclear baseline
  • Lung resection surgery (need to predict post-op lung function)
‚ùå Chest X-ray: Not routinely indicated. Consider if new cardiopulmonary symptoms or known cardiopulmonary disease requiring reassessment.

Risk Reduction Strategies

‚úÖ Evidence-based interventions:
  • Smoking cessation ‚Äî ideally ‚â•8 weeks preop; even shorter duration helpful
  • Lung expansion techniques ‚Äî incentive spirometry, CPAP, deep breathing exercises
  • Optimize COPD/asthma ‚Äî continue inhalers perioperatively
  • Nasogastric decompression ‚Äî only with symptomatic distension (not prophylactic)
  • Neuraxial anesthesia ‚Äî consider when appropriate (lower risk than GA)
‚ö†Ô∏è Smoking cessation: Some concern about increased sputum production in first weeks after quitting. However, benefits outweigh risks at any time point.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea

STOP-BANG Screening:
  • Snoring loudly
  • Tired during day
  • Observed apneas
  • Pressure (HTN)
  • BMI >35
  • Age >50
  • Neck circumference >40 cm
  • Gender male
Score ‚â•3 = intermediate risk; ‚â•5 = high risk
‚úÖ Perioperative management:
  • Continue CPAP perioperatively (bring from home)
  • Minimize opioids, use multimodal analgesia
  • Avoid supine position if possible
  • Continuous pulse oximetry postoperatively
  • May require ICU monitoring after major surgery

Perioperative Glucose Targets

‚úÖ Target glucose: 140-180 mg/dL
  • Tight control (<140) ‚Üí ‚Üëhypoglycemia without benefit (NICE-SUGAR)
  • Avoid hyperglycemia >180 ‚Üí infection, poor wound healing
  • More liberal targets (180-250) acceptable in some patients
Intraoperative management:
  • Check glucose on arrival, then q1-2h
  • D5W + insulin drip for major surgery
  • Sliding scale for minor procedures

Euglycemic DKA with SGLT2 Inhibitors

‚ö†Ô∏è Key features:
  • DKA with normal or only mildly elevated glucose (<250)
  • Precipitated by surgery, fasting, dehydration, illness
  • SGLT2 inhibitors promote ketogenesis
  • May be missed because glucose not very high
Hold SGLT2i 3-4 days before surgery

  • NOT indicated for primary prophylaxis (even high-risk surgery)

üíé High-Yield Board Pearls

‚ñº
Cardiac Risk:
  1. RCRI: 6 factors (high-risk surgery, CAD, CHF, CVD, insulin DM, Cr >2.0)
  2. Functional capacity ‚â•4 METs ‚Üí proceed without testing (if asymptomatic)
  3. Do NOT start high-dose beta-blockers on day of surgery (POISE: ‚Üëstroke, ‚Üëdeath)
  4. Continue beta-blockers if already on them
  5. Recent DES: delay elective surgery ‚â•6 months (‚â•3 months if urgent)
Pulmonary Risk:
  1. Surgical site is the strongest predictor of pulmonary complications
  2. Routine preoperative PFTs are NOT recommended
  3. Smoking cessation at any time before surgery is beneficial
  4. Continue inhalers perioperatively (COPD, asthma)
  5. OSA patients: continue CPAP, minimize opioids, monitor postoperatively
Medications:
  1. CONTINUE: beta-blockers, statins, thyroid hormones, anticonvulsants
  2. HOLD: ACE-I/ARBs (controversial), diuretics morning of surgery
  3. HOLD SGLT2 inhibitors 3-4 days before (euglycemic DKA)
  4. HOLD GLP-1 agonists (aspiration risk from delayed gastric emptying)
  5. Stress-dose steroids only for major surgery in high-risk patients
Anticoagulation:
  1. BRIDGE trial: Bridging NOT needed for most AFib patients on warfarin
  2. Bridge only for HIGH-risk: mechanical mitral valve, recent stroke, recent VTE
  3. DOACs: No bridging needed, hold 2-5 days based on renal function
  4. Continue aspirin for CAD/stents if possible (except intracranial surgery)
  5. Stop clopidogrel 5 days, ticagrelor 3-5 days, prasugrel 7 days before
Glycemic Control & VTE:
  1. Target glucose 140-180 (not tighter ‚Äî NICE-SUGAR)
  2. Reduce basal insulin by 20-25% night before surgery
  3. Orthopedic surgery: Extended VTE prophylaxis 10-35 days
  4. Abdominal/pelvic cancer surgery: 4 weeks VTE prophylaxis
  5. IVC filters NOT for primary prophylaxis, only acute VTE with contraindication to anticoagulation

1. Iron Deficiency Anemia Very Common

Etiology

  • Blood loss: GI (most common in men/postmenopausal women), menstrual (premenopausal women)
  • Decreased absorption: Celiac, gastric bypass, H. pylori, PPI use
  • Increased demand: Pregnancy, growth
  • Dietary: Rare in developed countries

Ferritin is an acute phase reactant. May be falsely normal/elevated in inflammation. If ferritin 30-100 ng/mL with inflammation, still consider iron deficiency.

Workup

  • Men and postmenopausal women: GI evaluation mandatory (upper and lower endoscopy)
  • Premenopausal women: GI workup if heavy menses don't explain, or if >50 or GI symptoms
  • Celiac serology if malabsorption suspected

Treatment

  • Oral iron: Ferrous sulfate 325mg (65mg elemental) daily-TID; take on empty stomach with vitamin C
  • IV iron: If intolerant, malabsorption, ongoing blood loss, or need rapid repletion
  • Reticulocyte response in 1 week; Hgb increase in 2-4 weeks
  • Continue 3-6 months after Hgb normalizes to replete stores

B12 Deficiency

Causes

  • Pernicious anemia: Anti-intrinsic factor antibodies (most specific), anti-parietal cell Ab
  • Gastric surgery, atrophic gastritis
  • Ileal resection or disease (Crohn's)
  • Metformin (long-term use)
  • Strict veganism

Clinical Features

  • Hematologic: Megaloblastic anemia, pancytopenia, hypersegmented neutrophils
  • Neurologic: Subacute combined degeneration (posterior columns + lateral corticospinal tracts), peripheral neuropathy, cognitive changes
  • Glossitis
Neurologic symptoms can occur without anemia. Low-normal B12 (200-400 pg/mL) with symptoms ‚Üí check methylmalonic acid (elevated in B12 deficiency, normal in folate deficiency).

Folate Deficiency

  • Causes: Dietary (alcoholism), malabsorption, increased demand (pregnancy, hemolysis), drugs (phenytoin, TMP-SMX)
  • No neurologic symptoms (unlike B12)
  • Elevated homocysteine; normal MMA
Never give folate alone if B12 status unknown! Folate can correct anemia but worsen neurologic damage in B12 deficiency.

5. Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia Boards Favorite

Warm AIHA

  • Antibody: IgG (binds at 37¬∞C)
  • Site: Extravascular (spleen)
  • Smear: Spherocytes
  • Causes: Idiopathic, CLL, SLE, drugs (methyldopa, penicillin, cephalosporins)
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids (first-line), rituximab, splenectomy

Cold Agglutinin Disease

  • Antibody: IgM (binds at <37¬∞C)
  • Site: Complement-mediated, intravascular
  • Causes: Mycoplasma, EBV, lymphoproliferative disorders
  • Treatment: Avoid cold, rituximab (steroids less effective)
Drug-induced AIHA: Methyldopa causes warm AIHA via drug-induced autoantibodies. Penicillin causes hapten-mediated hemolysis (drug binds to RBC).

6. G6PD Deficiency Boards Favorite

Overview

  • X-linked recessive
  • Most common enzyme deficiency worldwide
  • Episodic hemolysis triggered by oxidative stress

Triggers

"Fava beans and drugs that cause havoc":
Fava beans, Sulfonamides, Dapsone, Primaquine, Nitrofurantoin, Rasburicase, Infections

Clinical Features

  • Acute hemolytic crisis 1-3 days after exposure
  • Jaundice, dark urine, back pain
  • Smear: Bite cells, Heinz bodies (require special stain)

Diagnosis

  • G6PD enzyme assay: Check when NOT in acute hemolysis (young RBCs have higher G6PD levels)

Treatment

  • Remove/avoid trigger
  • Supportive care, transfusion if severe
  • Self-limited once trigger removed

9. Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura Emergency

Pathophysiology

  • ADAMTS13 deficiency (<10% activity)
  • Acquired (autoantibody) or hereditary
  • Uncleaved ultra-large vWF multimers ‚Üí platelet aggregation ‚Üí microthrombi

Classic Pentad (rarely complete)

FAT RN: Fever, microangiopathic hemolytic Anemia, Thrombocytopenia, Renal dysfunction, Neurologic changes

Diagnosis

  • Thrombocytopenia + MAHA (schistocytes, elevated LDH, low haptoglobin)
  • ADAMTS13 activity <10% (send before plasma exchange)
  • PLASMIC score: Predicts ADAMTS13 deficiency
Do NOT wait for ADAMTS13 results to treat! Untreated TTP has >90% mortality. Start plasma exchange empirically.

Treatment

  • Plasma exchange (PEX): First-line, removes antibody and replenishes ADAMTS13
  • Corticosteroids: Prednisone 1 mg/kg
  • Caplacizumab: Anti-vWF nanobody; accelerates platelet recovery
  • Rituximab: For refractory/relapsing cases
TTP vs HUS: TTP = neuro predominant, ADAMTS13 <10%. HUS = renal predominant, often Shiga toxin (STEC-HUS).

Types

HIT (Type 2)

  • Antibodies to PF4-heparin complexes
  • Platelet activation ‚Üí thrombosis (50% if untreated)
  • Onset: 5-10 days after heparin start (or <24h if prior exposure)
  • Platelet drop ‚â•50% from baseline

0-3: Low probability; 4-5: Intermediate; 6-8: High

Diagnosis

  • PF4-heparin ELISA: High sensitivity, lower specificity
  • Serotonin release assay (SRA): Gold standard, high specificity

Management

  • Stop all heparin immediately (including flushes)
  • Start non-heparin anticoagulant: Argatroban, bivalirudin, fondaparinux
  • Do NOT give warfarin until platelets >150 (causes skin necrosis)
  • Transition to DOAC or warfarin when stable
HIT is a prothrombotic state despite low platelets. Thrombosis risk 50% if untreated. Always anticoagulate, never transfuse platelets.

11. Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura Common

Overview

  • Autoantibodies against platelet glycoproteins (GPIIb/IIIa)
  • Isolated thrombocytopenia; normal WBC and Hgb
  • Diagnosis of exclusion

Clinical Features

  • Mucocutaneous bleeding (petechiae, purpura, epistaxis, gingival)
  • No splenomegaly (vs hypersplenism)

Workup

  • CBC, peripheral smear (isolated thrombocytopenia, large platelets)
  • HIV, HCV testing (can cause ITP)
  • H. pylori (treat if positive)
  • Bone marrow biopsy: Only if atypical features or >60 years

14. von Willebrand Disease Common

Overview

  • Most common inherited bleeding disorder
  • vWF: Mediates platelet adhesion, carries factor VIII

Types

TypeDefectLabs
Type 1 (75%)Partial quantitative deficiency‚Üì vWF:Ag, ‚Üì vWF:RCo
Type 2Qualitative defectVariable; 2A, 2B, 2M, 2N subtypes
Type 3Complete deficiency (severe)Absent vWF, very low factor VIII

Clinical Features

  • Mucocutaneous bleeding (menorrhagia, epistaxis, easy bruising)
  • Bleeding after dental procedures, surgery

Laboratory Findings

  • PTT: May be prolonged (if factor VIII low)
  • Bleeding time: Prolonged (if performed)
  • vWF:Ag (antigen) and vWF:RCo (activity) decreased

Treatment

  • DDAVP: First-line for Type 1 (releases vWF from endothelium)
  • vWF-containing factor concentrates: For Type 2, Type 3, or major bleeding
  • Antifibrinolytics: Tranexamic acid for mucosal bleeding
DDAVP is contraindicated in Type 2B vWD ‚Äî can worsen thrombocytopenia (mutant vWF binds platelets excessively).

16. Acute Myeloid Leukemia High-Yield

Overview

  • Clonal expansion of myeloid blasts (‚â•20% in marrow)
  • Most common acute leukemia in adults

Clinical Features

  • Bone marrow failure: Anemia (fatigue), thrombocytopenia (bleeding), neutropenia (infections)
  • Leukostasis: WBC >100K ‚Üí pulmonary/CNS symptoms (emergency)
  • DIC: Especially APL
  • Gingival hyperplasia: Monocytic subtypes (M4, M5)
  • Auer rods: Pathognomonic for AML

17. Chronic Myeloid Leukemia Boards Favorite

Pathophysiology

  • t(9;22) Philadelphia chromosome ‚Üí BCR-ABL1 fusion
  • Constitutively active tyrosine kinase

Clinical Features

  • Often asymptomatic (incidental leukocytosis)
  • Fatigue, weight loss, night sweats
  • Massive splenomegaly
  • WBC very high (often >100K) with left shift, basophilia

Phases

PhaseFeatures
Chronic<10% blasts, most patients at diagnosis
Accelerated10-19% blasts, basophils >20%, other criteria
Blast crisis‚â•20% blasts (myeloid or lymphoid)

Diagnosis

  • CBC with differential showing leukocytosis, left shift, basophilia
  • Low LAP score (vs leukemoid reaction)
  • Confirm: BCR-ABL1 by FISH or PCR, cytogenetics for Ph chromosome

Treatment

  • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs): Imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib
  • Monitor BCR-ABL1 transcripts by PCR
  • Deep molecular response ‚Üí possible TKI discontinuation
  • Allogeneic HSCT for TKI-resistant or blast crisis
TKIs have transformed CML into a chronic manageable disease. Most patients achieve near-normal life expectancy with treatment.

18. Hodgkin Lymphoma High-Yield

Pathology

  • Reed-Sternberg cells: "Owl-eye" appearance, CD15+/CD30+
  • Bimodal age distribution (20s and >55)

Clinical Features

  • Painless lymphadenopathy (cervical most common)
  • B symptoms: Fever >38¬∞C, night sweats, >10% weight loss in 6 months
  • Alcohol-induced lymph node pain (rare but classic)
  • Contiguous spread (vs NHL)

19. Multiple Myeloma High-Yield

Overview

  • Clonal plasma cell neoplasm producing monoclonal protein
  • Median age 70 years; more common in Black patients

Clinical Features ‚Äî CRAB

Calcium elevation (>11)
Renal insufficiency (Cr >2 or CrCl <40)
Anemia (Hgb <10 or 2g below normal)
Bone lesions (lytic, osteoporosis, pathologic fractures)

Other Features

  • Infections: Impaired humoral immunity
  • Hyperviscosity: Visual changes, bleeding, neurologic symptoms
  • Amyloidosis: AL type (light chain)
  • Neuropathy

Diagnosis

  • SPEP/UPEP: M-spike (monoclonal protein)
  • Serum free light chains: Kappa/lambda ratio
  • Bone marrow biopsy: ‚â•10% clonal plasma cells
  • Skeletal survey or PET/CT: Lytic lesions

Diagnostic Criteria (‚â•1 CRAB or biomarker)

  • Clonal plasma cells ‚â•10% OR biopsy-proven plasmacytoma
  • PLUS ‚â•1 myeloma-defining event: CRAB or biomarker (‚â•60% plasma cells, FLC ratio ‚â•100, >1 MRI lesion)

Treatment

  • Transplant-eligible: Induction (VRd: bortezomib, lenalidomide, dexamethasone) ‚Üí autologous HSCT ‚Üí maintenance
  • Transplant-ineligible: VRd or Dara-VRd, continue until progression
  • Supportive: Bisphosphonates, pain control, infection prophylaxis
Avoid NSAIDs and nephrotoxins. Light chain cast nephropathy is a medical emergency ‚Äî treat myeloma and consider plasmapheresis.

50. High-Yield Board Pearls

Anemia

  • üíé Low ferritin = iron deficiency. Normal ferritin with inflammation doesn't rule it out.
  • üíé B12 deficiency can cause neuro symptoms WITHOUT anemia. Check MMA if borderline.
  • üíé Spherocytes + positive DAT = warm AIHA. Spherocytes + negative DAT = hereditary spherocytosis.
  • üíé G6PD assay is falsely normal during acute hemolysis ‚Äî retest later.

Bleeding Disorders

  • üíé Prolonged PTT + normal PT + mixing corrects = factor deficiency (VIII, IX, XI, XII).
  • üíé Prolonged PTT + doesn't correct = inhibitor (lupus anticoagulant or factor inhibitor).
  • üíé vWD: Most common inherited bleeding disorder. DDAVP for Type 1.
  • üíé DIC: Treat the underlying cause. Replace products only if bleeding.

Thrombosis

  • üíé HIT = prothrombotic! Stop heparin AND start non-heparin anticoagulant.
  • üíé TTP: Don't wait for ADAMTS13 ‚Äî start plasma exchange immediately.
  • üíé Warfarin skin necrosis = protein C/S deficiency. Bridge with heparin.

Leukemia

  • üíé APL [t(15;17)] = DIC. Start ATRA immediately on suspicion.
  • üíé CML = Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22), BCR-ABL1. TKIs are curative.
  • üíé CLL = CD5+/CD23+ B-cells. Watch and wait if asymptomatic.
  • üíé Auer rods = AML (not ALL).

Lymphoma

  • üíé Reed-Sternberg cells = Hodgkin. CD15+/CD30+.
  • üíé "Starry sky" pattern = Burkitt lymphoma. t(8;14) c-MYC.
  • üíé Hodgkin spreads contiguously; NHL spreads hematogenously.

Myeloma

  • üíé CRAB criteria: Calcium, Renal, Anemia, Bone lesions.
  • üíé Rouleaux formation on smear = myeloma or Waldenstr√∂m's.
  • üíé Lytic lesions on skeletal survey (not bone scan ‚Äî no blastic activity).

Transfusion

  • üíé TRALI = noncardiogenic pulmonary edema within 6h of transfusion.
  • üíé TACO = volume overload. Diuretics help (vs TRALI where they don't).
  • üíé Febrile non-hemolytic = most common reaction. Cytokines in product.

1. Anemia‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Approach to Anemia
Classification by MCV

Microcytic anemia with MCV less than 80 is caused by iron deficiency, thalassemia, anemia of chronic disease, sideroblastic anemia, and lead poisoning.

Normocytic anemia is caused by acute blood loss, hemolysis, anemia of chronic disease, bone marrow failure, and chronic kidney disease.

Macrocytic anemia with MCV greater than 100 is caused by B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, hypothyroidism, liver disease, myelodysplastic syndrome, and medications.

Iron Deficiency Anemia
Diagnosis

Classic iron studies show low ferritin, low iron, high TIBC, and low transferrin saturation.

Ferritin is the most sensitive test but is an acute phase reactant and may be falsely normal in inflammation.

In inflammatory states, ferritin less than 100 suggests iron deficiency. Transferrin saturation less than 20 percent also supports the diagnosis.

Always investigate for GI blood loss in men and postmenopausal women with iron deficiency.

Hemolytic Anemia
Workup

Hemolysis shows elevated LDH, elevated indirect bilirubin, low haptoglobin, elevated reticulocyte count, and spherocytes on smear.

Direct Coombs test, or DAT, distinguishes immune from non-immune causes. Positive DAT indicates autoimmune hemolytic anemia.

Warm AIHA is IgG-mediated and associated with lymphoproliferative disorders. Treat with steroids, then rituximab.

Cold agglutinin disease is IgM-mediated and associated with Mycoplasma and lymphoma. Avoid cold, treat underlying cause, rituximab for severe cases.

2. Coagulation Disorders‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Approach to Bleeding
PT vs PTT

Elevated PT with normal PTT suggests factor VII deficiency, warfarin effect, early liver disease, or early vitamin K deficiency.

Elevated PTT with normal PT suggests hemophilia A or B, von Willebrand disease, or heparin effect.

Both PT and PTT elevated suggests common pathway defects, DIC, severe liver disease, or supratherapeutic anticoagulation.

Von Willebrand Disease
Diagnosis and Treatment

VWD is the most common inherited bleeding disorder. Type 1 is most common with partial quantitative deficiency.

Presents with mucocutaneous bleeding: epistaxis, menorrhagia, easy bruising, and prolonged bleeding after procedures.

Labs show prolonged PTT, prolonged bleeding time, low VWF antigen and activity, and possibly low factor VIII.

Treatment for Type 1 is DDAVP which releases stored VWF. For Type 2 and 3 or major bleeding, give VWF concentrate.

DIC
Key Points

DIC shows elevated PT and PTT, low fibrinogen, elevated D-dimer, low platelets, and schistocytes on smear.

Common triggers are sepsis, trauma, malignancy, obstetric complications, and transfusion reactions.

Treatment is supportive and treating the underlying cause. Transfuse blood products for active bleeding.

3. Thrombocytopenia‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Approach
Differential

First rule out pseudothrombocytopenia from platelet clumping. Check smear and redraw in citrate tube.

Decreased production causes include bone marrow failure, infiltration, medications, infections, and nutritional deficiencies.

Increased destruction causes include ITP, TTP, HUS, DIC, HIT, and drug-induced.

Sequestration occurs with hypersplenism from portal hypertension or infiltrative diseases.

TTP and HIT
TTP

TTP presents with thrombocytopenia, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia with schistocytes, fever, neurologic changes, and renal dysfunction.

Caused by ADAMTS13 deficiency leading to uncleaved VWF multimers.

ADAMTS13 activity less than 10 percent confirms diagnosis. Do not wait for results to treat.

Treatment is emergent plasma exchange plus steroids plus rituximab. Caplacizumab is an adjunct.

Do not transfuse platelets in TTP as it can worsen thrombosis.

HIT

HIT typically occurs 5 to 10 days after starting heparin. Platelet drop greater than 50 percent or to less than 150,000.

Paradoxically causes thrombosis, not bleeding. Both arterial and venous clots can occur.

Calculate 4T score for pretest probability. If intermediate or high, send HIT antibody and start non-heparin anticoagulant.

Stop all heparin including flushes. Start argatroban, bivalirudin, or fondaparinux. Do not give warfarin until platelets recover.

4. VTE & Anticoagulation‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Anticoagulation Duration
Guidelines

Provoked VTE from surgery or transient risk factor: 3 months of anticoagulation.

Unprovoked VTE: 3 months minimum, then reassess. Extended therapy if bleeding risk is low.

Recurrent unprovoked VTE or active cancer: indefinite anticoagulation.

Cancer-associated VTE: LMWH or DOAC preferred over warfarin. Continue while cancer is active or being treated.

Anticoagulant Reversal
Reversal Agents

Warfarin: Vitamin K for non-urgent reversal. 4-factor PCC plus vitamin K for urgent reversal.

Heparin and LMWH: Protamine sulfate. Reverses UFH completely, LMWH partially.

Dabigatran: Idarucizumab, brand name Praxbind.

Factor Xa inhibitors apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban: Andexanet alfa. Use 4-factor PCC if unavailable.

5. Hematologic Malignancies‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Leukemias
AML vs ALL

AML is more common in adults. Auer rods are pathognomonic. APL subtype shows t(15;17) and is treated with ATRA plus arsenic.

ALL is more common in children. Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22) is a poor prognostic marker.

CLL is the most common leukemia in Western adults. Smudge cells on smear. Many patients need no immediate treatment.

CML shows Philadelphia chromosome. Treat with tyrosine kinase inhibitors like imatinib.

Lymphomas
Hodgkin vs Non-Hodgkin

Hodgkin lymphoma has Reed-Sternberg cells. Bimodal age distribution. Often presents with mediastinal mass and B symptoms.

Non-Hodgkin lymphomas are a heterogeneous group. Most common is diffuse large B-cell lymphoma, which is aggressive. Follicular lymphoma is indolent.

Multiple Myeloma
CRAB Criteria

Myeloma causes hypercalcemia, renal insufficiency, anemia, and bone lesions, remembered as CRAB.

Diagnosis requires clonal bone marrow plasma cells 10 percent or more plus CRAB features or biomarkers of malignancy.

Biomarkers include 60 percent or more plasma cells, free light chain ratio 100 or more, or more than one MRI lesion.

SPEP shows M-spike. Serum free light chains are more sensitive than urine protein electrophoresis.

6. Transfusion Medicine‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Transfusion Reactions
Types

Acute hemolytic reaction is due to ABO incompatibility. Presents with fever, flank pain, hemoglobinuria, and hypotension. Stop transfusion immediately.

Febrile non-hemolytic reaction is due to cytokines. Fever without hemolysis. Treat with acetaminophen. Use leukoreduced products.

Allergic reactions range from urticaria to anaphylaxis. Stop transfusion for severe reactions. Use washed products for recurrence.

TRALI presents with acute respiratory distress within 6 hours of transfusion. Bilateral infiltrates without volume overload. Supportive care.

TACO is volume overload presenting as pulmonary edema. Treat with diuretics.

Transfusion Thresholds
Guidelines

Restrictive strategy with hemoglobin threshold of 7 is appropriate for most stable hospitalized patients.

Threshold of 8 is used for cardiac surgery and symptomatic cardiovascular disease.

Platelet transfusion threshold is 10,000 for prophylaxis in stable patients. Higher thresholds for bleeding or procedures.

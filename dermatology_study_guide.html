Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)

Spectrum of Severity:
  • SJS: <10% body surface area (BSA) detachment
  • SJS/TEN overlap: 10-30% BSA
  • TEN: >30% BSA (mortality 30-50%)

Clinical Features

  • Prodrome: fever, malaise, URI symptoms 1-3 days before rash
  • Mucosal involvement (‚â•2 sites): oral, ocular, genital (90%+)
  • Skin: atypical targetoid lesions, bullae, positive Nikolsky sign
  • Full-thickness epidermal necrosis ‚Üí "sheet-like" skin sloughing

High-Risk Medications

  • Sulfonamides (TMP-SMX) ‚Äî most common
  • Allopurinol
  • Anticonvulsants: carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine, phenobarbital
  • NSAIDs (oxicam type)
  • Antibiotics: penicillins, cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones
  • Nevirapine

Management

‚úÖ Treatment:
  • STOP the offending drug immediately
  • Burn unit or ICU admission for wound care
  • Fluid/electrolyte management (massive insensible losses)
  • Wound care: non-adherent dressings, minimize debridement
  • Ophthalmology consult (prevent corneal scarring)
  • Systemic steroids: controversial (no proven benefit, may harm)
  • IVIG: conflicting evidence
  • Cyclosporine: some evidence of benefit
üíé HLA-B*58:01: Strongly associated with allopurinol-induced SJS/TEN. Screen in high-risk populations (Han Chinese, Thai, Korean) BEFORE starting allopurinol.

DRESS Syndrome

Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms
  • Onset: 2-8 weeks after drug initiation (LATER than other reactions)
  • Fever, facial edema, lymphadenopathy
  • Morbilliform rash ‚Üí can become edematous, purpuric
  • Eosinophilia (‚â•700) and/or atypical lymphocytosis
  • Organ involvement: hepatitis (most common), nephritis, pneumonitis, myocarditis

Causative Drugs (SPADE)

  • Sulfonamides
  • Phenytoin / Phenobarbital
  • Allopurinol
  • Dapsone
  • Enticonvulsants (carbamazepine, lamotrigine)
  • Also: vancomycin, minocycline, abacavir
‚ö†Ô∏è HHV-6 reactivation occurs in ~60% of DRESS cases and may contribute to severity. Associated with prolonged course and may cause "flares" even after drug discontinuation.
‚úÖ Management: Stop drug, supportive care, systemic corticosteroids (unlike SJS/TEN, steroids DO help in DRESS)

Acute Generalized Exanthematous Pustulosis (AGEP)

  • Onset: Rapid (within 48 hours)
  • Hundreds of small, non-follicular sterile pustules on erythematous base
  • Starts in flexures (axillae, groin)
  • Fever, neutrophilia
  • Resolves quickly (days) after drug cessation with superficial desquamation

Common Causes

  • Antibiotics: aminopenicillins (most common), macrolides, quinolones
  • Calcium channel blockers
  • Hydroxychloroquine
üíé Board Pearl: AGEP has the most RAPID onset of severe drug reactions (48 hours). Unlike pustular psoriasis, it resolves quickly once the drug is stopped.

Melanoma

ABCDEs of Melanoma

  • Asymmetry ‚Äî one half unlike the other
  • Border ‚Äî irregular, ragged, blurred
  • Color ‚Äî varied shades of brown, black, red, white, blue
  • Diameter ‚Äî >6 mm (pencil eraser), though can be smaller
  • Evolving ‚Äî changing in size, shape, color, or symptoms

Types of Melanoma

Type Frequency Features
Superficial spreading ~70% Most common; horizontal growth, then vertical invasion
Nodular ~15% Most aggressive; vertical growth from onset; raised, dark nodule
Lentigo maligna ~10% Sun-damaged skin of elderly; slow growing; face/neck
Acral lentiginous ~5% Palms, soles, nail beds; most common type in dark-skinned individuals

Staging (Breslow Thickness)

  • Breslow thickness (depth in mm) is the most important prognostic factor
  • Ulceration worsens prognosis
  • Sentinel lymph node biopsy if thickness ‚â•1 mm (or 0.8 mm with ulceration)

Surveillance After Treatment

‚úÖ Follow-up:
  • Full skin exams every 3-12 months (based on stage)
  • Patient self-exams monthly
  • Imaging not routine for early-stage; consider for stage IIB+
üíé Board Pearl: Acral lentiginous melanoma is the most common melanoma in African Americans, Asians, and Hispanics. Look on palms, soles, and under nails. "Hutchinson sign" (periungual pigmentation) suggests subungual melanoma.

Actinic Keratosis

  • Precursor to SCC ‚Äî "precancer"
  • Rough, scaly, erythematous macules on sun-exposed skin
  • "Sandpaper" texture
  • 1-10% progress to SCC
  • Treatment: cryotherapy, topical 5-FU, imiquimod, photodynamic therapy
‚ö†Ô∏è High-risk SCC features:
  • Size >2 cm
  • Poor differentiation
  • Perineural invasion
  • Location: lip, ear, scar, chronic wound
  • Immunocompromised patient

Herpes Zoster (Shingles)

  • Reactivation of VZV in dorsal root ganglion
  • Dermatomal distribution ‚Äî does not cross midline
  • Prodrome: pain, burning, tingling 1-5 days before rash
  • Vesicles on erythematous base ‚Üí pustules ‚Üí crusts

Treatment

‚úÖ Antivirals (within 72 hours of rash onset):
  • Valacyclovir 1g TID √ó 7 days (preferred)
  • Acyclovir 800mg 5√ó/day √ó 7 days
  • Famciclovir 500mg TID √ó 7 days

  • Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) ‚Äî pain persisting >90 days; risk ‚Üë with age
  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus ‚Äî V1 involvement; Hutchinson sign (nose tip) = ‚Üërisk of ocular involvement
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome ‚Äî facial palsy + ear vesicles (CN VII + VIII involvement)
  • Disseminated zoster (>2 dermatomes) ‚Äî immunocompromised

Psoriasis

Classic features:
  • Well-demarcated, erythematous plaques with silvery scale
  • Extensor surfaces: elbows, knees, scalp, sacrum
  • Nail changes: pitting, onycholysis, oil spots
  • Auspitz sign: pinpoint bleeding when scale removed
  • Koebner phenomenon: lesions at sites of trauma

Types

Type Features
Plaque (vulgaris) Most common (90%); chronic plaques
Guttate Small "drop-like" lesions; often triggered by strep pharyngitis
Inverse Intertriginous areas; smooth, erythematous (less scale)
Pustular Sterile pustules; localized (palms/soles) or generalized (von Zumbusch ‚Äî life-threatening)
Erythrodermic >90% BSA involvement; medical emergency

Treatment

  • Mild: Topical steroids, vitamin D analogs (calcipotriene), tazarotene
  • Moderate-severe: Phototherapy (UVB), methotrexate, cyclosporine
  • Biologics: TNF-Œ± inhibitors, IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab), IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab)
‚ö†Ô∏è Associated conditions:
  • Psoriatic arthritis (30%)
  • Metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease
  • Depression

Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema)

Features:
  • Pruritic, erythematous, weeping/crusting patches
  • Flexural involvement in older children/adults (antecubital, popliteal fossae)
  • Associated with "atopic triad": asthma, allergic rhinitis, eczema
  • Chronic: lichenification (thickened, leathery skin)

Treatment

  • Emollients: Foundation of treatment ‚Äî apply immediately after bathing
  • Topical steroids: For flares; low-potency for face/flexures
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors: Tacrolimus, pimecrolimus (steroid-sparing)
  • Severe: Dupilumab (IL-4/IL-13 inhibitor), phototherapy, cyclosporine
  • Avoid triggers: harsh soaps, fragrances, irritants
üíé Board Pearl: Eczema herpeticum = disseminated HSV in atopic dermatitis. Presents with "punched-out" erosions, fever, lymphadenopathy. Treat with IV acyclovir.

Urticaria and Angioedema

Urticaria (hives):
  • Transient, pruritic wheals
  • Individual lesions last <24 hours
  • Acute (<6 weeks) vs chronic (>6 weeks)
Angioedema:
  • Deep dermal/subcutaneous swelling
  • Lips, eyelids, tongue, larynx
  • Lasts 24-72 hours

Types of Angioedema

Type Mechanism Features
Allergic/Mast cell Histamine-mediated + urticaria, + pruritus; responds to antihistamines/epinephrine
ACE-I induced Bradykinin-mediated NO urticaria; lips/tongue; can occur years after starting; does NOT respond to antihistamines
Hereditary (HAE) C1-INH deficiency (bradykinin) NO urticaria; recurrent episodes; abdominal pain; family history; low C4
‚ùå ACE-I angioedema:
  • Most common cause of drug-induced angioedema
  • Can occur after years of use
  • More common in African Americans
  • Does NOT respond to epinephrine/antihistamines (bradykinin-mediated)
  • Treat with FFP, icatibant (bradykinin antagonist), C1-INH concentrate
  • Discontinue ACE-I permanently; ARBs generally safe but small cross-reactivity risk

üíé High-Yield Board Pearls

‚ñº
Drug Reactions:
  1. SJS/TEN: Mucosal involvement + skin detachment; mortality 30-50% in TEN
  2. DRESS: Late onset (2-8 weeks), eosinophilia, organ involvement; HHV-6 reactivation
  3. AGEP: Most rapid (48h), pustules in flexures, self-limited
  4. High-risk drugs: Allopurinol, TMP-SMX, anticonvulsants
  5. HLA-B*58:01 screening before allopurinol in Asian populations
Skin Cancer:
  1. Melanoma ABCDEs: Asymmetry, Border, Color, Diameter, Evolving
  2. Breslow thickness = most important prognostic factor
  3. Acral lentiginous melanoma most common in dark-skinned individuals
  4. BCC: pearly papule with telangiectasias; rarely metastasizes
  5. SCC: can metastasize; higher risk if immunocompromised
Infections:
  1. Necrotizing fasciitis: Pain out of proportion, rapid spread, crepitus ‚Üí emergent surgery
  2. Herpes zoster: Dermatomal, Hutchinson sign (nose tip) = eye involvement
  3. Shingrix vaccine: Safe in immunocompromised (recombinant, not live)
  4. Tinea versicolor: "Spaghetti and meatballs" on KOH; Malassezia
Inflammatory Conditions:
  1. Psoriasis: Silvery scale, extensor surfaces, Auspitz sign, nail pitting
  2. Guttate psoriasis: Triggered by strep pharyngitis
  3. Eczema herpeticum: Disseminated HSV in atopic dermatitis ‚Äî IV acyclovir
  4. ACE-I angioedema: NO urticaria, bradykinin-mediated, doesn't respond to epinephrine
  5. HAE: Low C4, recurrent episodes, family history
Skin Signs of Systemic Disease:
  1. Dermatomyositis: Heliotrope rash + Gottron papules; screen for malignancy
  2. Acanthosis nigricans (sudden onset): Think GI malignancy
  3. Sweet syndrome: Think AML
  4. Necrolytic migratory erythema: Think glucagonoma
  5. Koilonychia: Iron deficiency; Terry nails: Cirrhosis

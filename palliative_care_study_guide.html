When to Have Goals of Care Conversations

  • New diagnosis of serious illness
  • Disease progression or treatment failure
  • Hospital admission for serious illness
  • Functional decline
  • When patient asks "What should I expect?"
  • Before high-risk procedures
  • When current treatment is no longer aligned with goals

Framework for Goals of Care Discussion

SPIKES Protocol for Breaking Bad News:
  • Setting ‚Äî Private space, sit down, minimize interruptions
  • Perception ‚Äî "What do you understand about your illness?"
  • Invitation ‚Äî "How much information would you like?"
  • Knowledge ‚Äî Share information in small chunks
  • Empathy ‚Äî Acknowledge and validate emotions
  • Summary/Strategy ‚Äî Plan next steps together

Key Questions to Explore

‚úÖ Understanding values and preferences:
  • "What matters most to you right now?"
  • "What are you hoping for?"
  • "What are you worried about?"
  • "What gives your life meaning?"
  • "What would be unacceptable to you?"
  • "If time is limited, how would you want to spend it?"
üíé Board Pearl: Ask about understanding BEFORE giving information. This allows you to meet the patient where they are and correct misconceptions.

Responding to Emotion

NURSE Statements:
  • Name the emotion: "It sounds like you're feeling scared."
  • Understand: "I can understand why you would feel that way."
  • Respect: "I can see how hard you've been fighting this."
  • Support: "I will be with you through this."
  • Explore: "Tell me more about what you're worried about."
‚ö†Ô∏è When emotions arise: Pause and respond to the emotion BEFORE continuing with medical information. Patients cannot process information when emotionally overwhelmed.

Code Status Discussions

Framing code status in context of goals:
  • Discuss code status as part of larger goals conversation, not in isolation
  • Provide realistic information about outcomes of CPR
  • Frame recommendations in terms of what you WOULD do, not just what you wouldn't

CPR Outcomes by Setting

Population Survival to Discharge
In-hospital cardiac arrest (all) ~25%
Metastatic cancer ~6%
Sepsis ~10%
Renal failure on dialysis ~10-15%
Nursing home residents ~5%
‚úÖ Better framing: "Given what we know about your illness, if your heart were to stop, I would not recommend CPR because it would not help you achieve your goal of [stated goal]. Instead, I recommend we focus on [comfort, time with family, etc.]."

Fentanyl Patch Conversion

  • Fentanyl patch 25 mcg/hr ‚âà Morphine 60-90 mg PO/day
  • Takes 12-24 hours to reach steady state
  • Continue short-acting opioid for first 12-24 hours when starting patch
  • Avoid in opioid-naive patients
  • Heat increases absorption (fever, heating pads)

Breakthrough Pain Dosing

‚úÖ PRN breakthrough dose:
  • 10-20% of total daily opioid dose
  • Give same opioid as scheduled if possible
  • Can give q1-2h PRN for IV, q3-4h for PO
Example: Patient on morphine ER 60mg BID (120mg/day)
  • Breakthrough = 10-20% of 120mg = 12-24mg
  • Order: Morphine IR 15mg PO q4h PRN

‚ùå Always avoid meperidine for chronic pain ‚Äî metabolite normeperidine causes seizures, especially in renal impairment.

üíé Board Pearl: Gabapentinoids and SNRIs are first-line for neuropathic pain. Opioids alone are often insufficient for neuropathic pain.

Dyspnea

Causes to evaluate: Pleural effusion, pulmonary embolism, anemia, pneumonia, CHF exacerbation, airway obstruction

Management

‚úÖ Pharmacologic:
  • Opioids ‚Äî First-line for refractory dyspnea
    • Opioid-naive: Morphine 2.5-5mg PO q4h or 1-2mg IV q4h
    • On opioids: Increase dose by 25%
  • Benzodiazepines ‚Äî For anxiety component (lorazepam 0.5-1mg)
  • Corticosteroids ‚Äî If lymphangitic spread, airway obstruction, COPD
‚úÖ Non-pharmacologic:
  • Fan blowing on face (stimulates trigeminal nerve)
  • Positioning (upright, leaning forward)
  • Oxygen if hypoxic (less benefit if not hypoxic)
  • Cool environment
üíé Board Pearl: Opioids reduce the sensation of dyspnea without significantly suppressing respiratory drive at appropriate doses. They are first-line for refractory dyspnea in palliative care.

‚ö†Ô∏è Bowel obstruction: Avoid metoclopramide (prokinetic can worsen colic). Use octreotide to decrease secretions, dexamethasone to reduce edema, and antiemetics.

Delirium at End of Life

Common causes:
  • Medications (opioids, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics)
  • Metabolic (hypercalcemia, uremia, hepatic encephalopathy)
  • Infection
  • Hypoxia
  • Brain metastases
  • Terminal delirium (part of dying process)

Management

‚úÖ Non-pharmacologic (first):
  • Reorientation, familiar objects, family presence
  • Minimize unnecessary interventions
  • Optimize sleep-wake cycle
  • Correct reversible causes if consistent with goals
‚úÖ Pharmacologic (for agitation):
  • Haloperidol 0.5-2mg PO/IV/SC q4-6h PRN ‚Äî first-line
  • Olanzapine 2.5-5mg PO/SL daily
  • Quetiapine 12.5-25mg PO BID
  • Avoid benzodiazepines (worsen delirium) unless alcohol withdrawal or terminal sedation
üíé Terminal delirium: Common in last days of life. Goals shift to patient comfort. May require palliative sedation if refractory and distressing.

Death Rattle (Terminal Secretions)

Noisy respirations from secretions in airways in dying patient
  • Does NOT cause dyspnea or discomfort to patient
  • Distressing to family ‚Äî education is key

Management

  • Position patient on side
  • Gentle suctioning (avoid deep suctioning ‚Äî causes discomfort)
  • Anticholinergics:
    • Glycopyrrolate 0.2-0.4mg IV/SC q4-6h (does not cross BBB)
    • Scopolamine patch 1.5mg q72h
    • Hyoscine (scopolamine) 0.3-0.6mg SC q4h
    • Atropine 1% drops SL q4h
‚ö†Ô∏è Anticholinergics work best prophylactically ‚Äî they prevent NEW secretions but don't dry existing secretions.

Anorexia-Cachexia Syndrome

  • Metabolic syndrome with loss of muscle/fat despite nutrition
  • Driven by inflammation and tumor factors
  • Artificial nutrition does NOT reverse cancer cachexia

Management

  • Education: eating won't reverse weight loss in advanced cancer
  • Appetite stimulants (modest benefit):
    • Dexamethasone 2-4mg daily (short-term)
    • Megestrol acetate 400-800mg daily (VTE risk)
    • Dronabinol 2.5mg BID
  • Focus on pleasure of eating, small portions, favorite foods

Hospice Eligibility Criteria

Medicare Hospice Benefit Requirements:
  • Prognosis of ‚â§6 months if disease runs its expected course
  • Two physicians certify (attending + hospice medical director)
  • Patient elects comfort-focused care
  • Patient agrees to forgo curative treatment for terminal diagnosis
Key points:
  • Patients can continue treatments for OTHER diagnoses
  • Can revoke hospice at any time and return to curative care
  • Can re-enroll in hospice if still eligible
  • Hospice covers: medications, equipment, nursing, aide, social work, chaplain, bereavement

Disease-Specific Prognostic Criteria

Cancer

  • Metastatic or locally advanced disease
  • Declining performance status (KPS ‚â§50 or PPS ‚â§50%)
  • Declining despite or refusing further treatment

Heart Failure

  • NYHA Class IV despite optimal therapy
  • EF ‚â§20% or severe diastolic dysfunction
  • ‚â•1 hospitalization in past 6 months
  • Not a transplant/VAD candidate
  • Refractory symptoms, rising creatinine/BUN

COPD

  • Disabling dyspnea at rest despite optimal treatment
  • FEV1 <30% predicted
  • pO2 ‚â§55 or O2 sat ‚â§88% on room air
  • pCO2 ‚â•50
  • Progressive weight loss, cor pulmonale, ER visits/hospitalizations

Dementia

  • FAST Stage 7C or beyond (non-ambulatory, minimal speech, incontinent)
  • Unable to ambulate, dress, bathe independently
  • Urinary and fecal incontinence
  • ‚â§6 intelligible words per day
  • Complication in past 12 months: aspiration pneumonia, pyelonephritis, sepsis, decubitus ulcer, recurrent fever
üíé Board Pearl: Prognostication is difficult. If you would "not be surprised" if the patient died within 6 months, hospice referral is appropriate.

‚öñÔ∏è Ethics & Legal Issues

‚ñº

Advance Directives

Document Purpose
Living Will Specifies treatments patient does/doesn't want if incapacitated
Healthcare Power of Attorney (HCPOA) Designates surrogate decision-maker; broader than living will
POLST/MOLST Physician orders for specific treatments (CPR, intubation, etc.); portable across settings
POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment):
  • Signed by physician and patient/surrogate
  • Actionable medical orders (unlike advance directive)
  • Intended for seriously ill patients
  • Addresses: CPR, intubation, hospitalization, artificial nutrition
  • Bright color (usually pink) for easy identification

Surrogate Decision-Making Hierarchy

When patient lacks capacity (typical order):
  1. Healthcare Power of Attorney / Healthcare Proxy
  2. Court-appointed guardian
  3. Spouse/domestic partner
  4. Adult children
  5. Parents
  6. Adult siblings
  7. Close friend (varies by state)
Order varies by state law

Standards for Surrogate Decision-Making

  1. Substituted judgment (preferred): What would THIS patient want?
  2. Best interest (if wishes unknown): What would reasonable person want?

Withdrawing vs. Withholding Treatment

‚úÖ Ethically and legally equivalent:
  • Withdrawing treatment already started = withholding treatment never started
  • Both are permissible when treatment no longer benefits patient or aligns with goals
  • Psychological difference exists but not ethical distinction
‚ö†Ô∏è Artificial nutrition/hydration:
  • Legally considered medical treatment (can be withdrawn)
  • In dying patients, forced hydration may increase discomfort (edema, secretions)
  • Dehydration in dying is NOT uncomfortable ‚Äî may even be comfortable

Principle of Double Effect

Four conditions for ethical permissibility:
  1. Action itself is morally good or neutral (giving medication for symptom relief)
  2. Good effect is intended (relief of suffering)
  3. Bad effect is foreseen but NOT intended (possible hastening of death)
  4. Good effect is not achieved BY means of the bad effect
Example: Giving morphine to relieve dyspnea in a dying patient is ethical even if it might hasten death, as long as the intent is comfort and the dose is titrated to symptom relief.
üíé Board Pearl: Appropriate opioid dosing for symptom control does NOT hasten death in most cases. Fear of hastening death should not prevent adequate pain/dyspnea treatment.

Palliative Sedation

Definition: Intentionally reducing consciousness to relieve refractory symptoms at end of life
Criteria for palliative sedation:
  • Patient is imminently dying (hours to days)
  • Symptom is refractory to standard treatments
  • Symptom is intolerable to patient
  • Consent obtained (patient or surrogate)
  • Intent is symptom relief, not hastening death
‚úÖ Medications used: Midazolam, propofol, pentobarbital, phenobarbital

Medical Aid in Dying (MAID)

Legal in some US states: Oregon, Washington, California, Colorado, Vermont, Hawaii, New Jersey, Maine, New Mexico, and DC (as of 2024)
  • Patient must have terminal illness with ‚â§6 month prognosis
  • Must be mentally competent adult
  • Must make multiple requests
  • Patient self-administers lethal medication
Different from euthanasia (physician administers)

üíé High-Yield Board Pearls

‚ñº
Goals of Care:
  1. Ask about understanding BEFORE giving information (SPIKES)
  2. Respond to emotion before continuing with information (NURSE)
  3. Frame recommendations positively: what you WILL do, not just what you won't
  4. Code status should be discussed in context of overall goals, not in isolation
Pain Management:
  1. Morphine PO:IV ratio = 3:1; Hydromorphone PO:IV = 4:1
  2. Breakthrough dose = 10-20% of total daily opioid dose
  3. AVOID morphine in renal failure (active metabolites accumulate)
  4. AVOID meperidine for chronic pain (neurotoxic metabolite)
  5. Constipation does NOT develop tolerance ‚Äî ALWAYS prescribe bowel regimen
  6. When rotating opioids, reduce dose 25-50% for incomplete cross-tolerance
Symptom Management:
  1. Opioids are first-line for refractory dyspnea (safe at appropriate doses)
  2. Fan blowing on face helps dyspnea (trigeminal nerve stimulation)
  3. Death rattle: Anticholinergics work best prophylactically
  4. Terminal delirium: Haloperidol first-line; AVOID benzodiazepines
  5. Bowel obstruction: AVOID prokinetics (metoclopramide); use octreotide
Hospice & Ethics:
  1. Hospice eligibility: Prognosis ‚â§6 months + comfort-focused goals
  2. Withdrawing = withholding treatment (ethically equivalent)
  3. Artificial nutrition is medical treatment (can be withdrawn)
  4. Double effect: Intent matters ‚Äî comfort care is ethical even if death hastened
  5. Surrogate decision: Substituted judgment > best interest
  6. POLST = actionable physician orders; Advance directive = patient wishes document

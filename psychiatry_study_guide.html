1. Major Depressive Disorder ‚Äî Overview

‚ñº

DSM-5 Criteria

üìã Diagnosis: ‚â•5 symptoms for ‚â•2 weeks (must include #1 or #2)

SIG E CAPS mnemonic:

  1. Sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia)
  2. Interest loss (anhedonia)
  3. Guilt or worthlessness
  4. Energy loss / fatigue
  5. Concentration problems
  6. Appetite changes (increased or decreased)
  7. Psychomotor agitation or retardation
  8. Suicidal ideation

Required: Must include depressed mood OR anhedonia

Severity

  • Mild: 5-6 symptoms, minor impairment
  • Moderate: Between mild and severe
  • Severe: Most symptoms, marked impairment, may have psychotic features

Screening

ToolDescription
PHQ-2 2 questions: depressed mood + anhedonia. Score ‚â•3 = positive screen ‚Üí do PHQ-9
PHQ-9 9 questions = 9 DSM criteria. Score 5-9 mild, 10-14 moderate, 15-19 mod-severe, ‚â•20 severe

Differential Diagnosis

  • Medical causes: Hypothyroidism, anemia, B12/folate deficiency, Cushing's, Parkinson's, stroke, malignancy
  • Medications: Beta-blockers, corticosteroids, interferon, isotretinoin, opioids
  • Substance-induced: Alcohol, cannabis, sedatives
  • Bipolar disorder: Screen for prior manic/hypomanic episodes before starting antidepressant

‚ñº

Treatment Approach by Severity

Moderate Medication AND/OR psychotherapy
Severe Medication + psychotherapy; consider ECT if refractory/psychotic

‚úì First-Line Choices
  • Sertraline ‚Äî Good safety profile, fewer drug interactions
  • Escitalopram ‚Äî Well tolerated, once daily
  • Fluoxetine ‚Äî Long half-life, good for non-adherence; can be activating

Sexual dysfunction SSRIs, SNRIs Switch to bupropion or mirtazapine; add bupropion

Treatment Timeline

  • Response onset: 2-4 weeks for initial improvement
  • Full response: 6-8 weeks at therapeutic dose
  • If no response at 4-6 weeks: Optimize dose ‚Üí switch ‚Üí augment
  • Duration: Continue at least 6-9 months after remission; lifelong if recurrent
üö´ Black Box Warning

Antidepressants may increase suicidal thinking in patients <25 years, especially in first weeks. Monitor closely. Risk decreases with age; benefit exceeds risk in adults.

3. Suicide Risk Assessment

‚ñº

Risk Factors

üö® SAD PERSONS Mnemonic
  • Sex (male)
  • Age (elderly, young adults)
  • Depression
  • Previous attempt (strongest predictor)
  • Ethanol/substance abuse
  • Rational thinking loss (psychosis)
  • Social support lacking
  • Organized plan
  • No spouse/partner
  • Sickness (chronic illness)

Assessment Components

  • Suicidal ideation: Passive vs active; frequency, intensity
  • Plan: Specific method, access to means
  • Intent: Does patient intend to act?
  • Prior attempts: Number, lethality, what prevented completion
  • Protective factors: Social support, reasons for living, religious beliefs

Management

Risk LevelAction
Low (passive ideation, no plan, protective factors) Outpatient management with close follow-up
Moderate (ideation with plan, no intent) Safety planning, remove access to means, frequent contact
High (plan + intent, prior attempts, impaired judgment) Psychiatric hospitalization (voluntary or involuntary)

Safety Planning

  1. Recognize warning signs
  2. Internal coping strategies
  3. Social contacts for distraction
  4. Family/friends who can help
  5. Professional resources (crisis line, ED)
  6. Reduce access to lethal means

4. Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

‚ñº

DSM-5 Criteria

  • Excessive anxiety/worry about multiple events occurring more days than not for ‚â•6 months
  • Difficulty controlling worry
  • ‚â•3 of: Restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulty, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance
  • Causes significant distress or impairment
  • Not due to substance or medical condition

Treatment

‚úì First-Line Treatment
  • SSRIs: Escitalopram, sertraline, paroxetine
  • SNRIs: Venlafaxine XR, duloxetine
  • CBT: Equally effective as medication; combination often best

Other Options

  • Buspirone: Non-sedating, no dependence; takes 2-4 weeks; good adjunct
  • Benzodiazepines: Rapid relief but avoid long-term; risk of dependence
  • Pregabalin: Alternative for some patients
  • Hydroxyzine: PRN for acute anxiety
‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid
  • Long-term benzodiazepines (dependence, falls in elderly, cognitive impairment)
  • Beta-blockers (not effective for GAD; useful for performance anxiety only)

5. Panic Disorder

‚ñº

DSM-5 Criteria

  • Recurrent unexpected panic attacks
  • ‚â•1 attack followed by ‚â•1 month of:
    • Persistent concern about additional attacks, OR
    • Maladaptive behavior change (avoidance)

Panic Attack Symptoms (‚â•4)

üìã Panic Attack = Abrupt surge of intense fear with ‚â•4 of:
  • Palpitations / racing heart
  • Sweating
  • Trembling / shaking
  • Shortness of breath
  • Choking sensation
  • Chest pain
  • Nausea / abdominal distress
  • Dizziness / lightheadedness
  • Chills or hot flashes
  • Paresthesias
  • Derealization / depersonalization
  • Fear of losing control / "going crazy"
  • Fear of dying

Peak within minutes; typically resolves in 20-30 minutes

Treatment

  • First-line: SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine, fluoxetine) or SNRIs
  • CBT: Highly effective; includes exposure therapy
  • Acute attacks: Benzodiazepine PRN (lorazepam, alprazolam) ‚Äî limit use
  • Avoid caffeine, stimulants

Differential

  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Pheochromocytoma
  • Cardiac arrhythmia
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Stimulant/caffeine use
  • Medication side effects

6. Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

‚ñº

DSM-5 Criteria

Exposure to traumatic event (experienced, witnessed, learned about, or repeated exposure to details) PLUS symptoms from each category lasting >1 month:

CategorySymptoms
Intrusion Flashbacks, nightmares, distressing memories, psychological/physiological reactivity to cues
Avoidance Avoiding memories, thoughts, feelings; avoiding external reminders
Negative cognition/mood Amnesia, negative beliefs, distorted blame, negative emotional state, detachment, anhedonia
Arousal/reactivity Irritability, reckless behavior, hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, concentration problems, sleep disturbance

Related Diagnoses

  • Acute Stress Disorder: Same symptoms, 3 days to 1 month post-trauma
  • Adjustment Disorder: Symptoms within 3 months of stressor, don't meet PTSD criteria

Treatment

‚úì Evidence-Based Treatments
  • Trauma-focused psychotherapy: Prolonged Exposure (PE), Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), EMDR
  • SSRIs: Sertraline, paroxetine (FDA-approved for PTSD)
  • SNRIs: Venlafaxine
  • Prazosin: For PTSD-related nightmares (alpha-1 blocker)
üö´ NOT Recommended for PTSD
  • Benzodiazepines (may worsen outcomes)
  • Antipsychotics as monotherapy
  • Debriefing immediately after trauma

7. Bipolar Disorder

‚ñº

Types

TypeDefinition
Bipolar I ‚â•1 manic episode (may have depressive episodes)
Bipolar II ‚â•1 hypomanic episode + ‚â•1 major depressive episode (no full mania)

Manic Episode Criteria

üî• DIG FAST Mnemonic (‚â•3 symptoms, ‚â•1 week)
  • Distractibility
  • Indiscretion / impulsivity (risky behavior)
  • Grandiosity
  • Flight of ideas / racing thoughts
  • Activity/Agitation increased
  • Sleep decreased (feels rested with little sleep)
  • Talkativeness (pressured speech)

Duration: ‚â•1 week (or any duration if hospitalized)

Severity: Marked impairment or psychotic features

  • Hypomania: ‚â•4 days, change from baseline, observable by others, no marked impairment, no psychosis, no hospitalization
  • Mania: ‚â•7 days, marked impairment, may have psychosis, often hospitalized

Treatment ‚Äî Acute Mania

  • Mood stabilizers: Lithium, valproate
  • Atypical antipsychotics: Quetiapine, aripiprazole, olanzapine, risperidone
  • Benzodiazepines: Adjunct for agitation/insomnia

Treatment ‚Äî Bipolar Depression

‚úì First-Line Options
  • Quetiapine (monotherapy)
  • Lurasidone (monotherapy or with lithium/valproate)
  • Olanzapine-fluoxetine combination (OFC)
  • Lamotrigine (good for maintenance, depression prevention)
üö´ Avoid in Bipolar

Antidepressant monotherapy ‚Äî can trigger mania/rapid cycling. If antidepressant needed, use with mood stabilizer.

Lithium Monitoring

  • Therapeutic range: 0.6-1.2 mEq/L (higher end for acute mania)
  • Baseline: TSH, creatinine, calcium, ECG, pregnancy test
  • Monitoring: Lithium level, TSH, creatinine every 6 months
  • Toxicity signs: Tremor ‚Üí ataxia ‚Üí confusion ‚Üí seizures
‚ö†Ô∏è Lithium Toxicity Risk
  • Dehydration, diarrhea, vomiting
  • NSAIDs (increase lithium levels)
  • ACE inhibitors, ARBs, thiazide diuretics
  • Renal impairment

8. Schizophrenia & Psychotic Disorders

‚ñº

DSM-5 Criteria for Schizophrenia

  • ‚â•2 of the following for significant portion of 1 month (‚â•1 must be #1, 2, or 3):

    2. Hallucinations
    3. Disorganized speech
    4. Disorganized or catatonic behavior

9. Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome

‚ñº
üö® NMS ‚Äî Life-Threatening Emergency

Cause: Dopamine receptor blockade (antipsychotics, antiemetics, withdrawal of dopamine agonists)

Clinical Features ("FEVER")

  • Fever (hyperthermia, often >40¬∞C)
  • Encephalopathy (altered mental status, confusion)
  • Vitals unstable (autonomic instability ‚Äî tachycardia, labile BP, diaphoresis)
  • Elevated CK (often >1000 U/L)
  • Rigidity ("lead-pipe")

Labs

  • Elevated CK (may be >10,000)
  • Leukocytosis
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Myoglobinuria (risk of AKI)

Treatment

  1. Stop offending agent immediately
  2. Supportive care: IV fluids, cooling, ICU monitoring
  3. Dantrolene (muscle relaxant)
  4. Bromocriptine (dopamine agonist)
  5. Amantadine (alternative)
  6. Benzodiazepines for agitation
  7. Monitor for rhabdomyolysis ‚Üí AKI

Differential: Serotonin Syndrome

FeatureNMSSerotonin Syndrome
CauseDopamine blockadeSerotonin excess
OnsetDays to weeksHours (rapid)
MuscleRigidity ("lead-pipe")Hyperreflexia, clonus, myoclonus
TreatmentDantrolene, bromocriptineCyproheptadine, benzodiazepines

üö® Delirium Tremens Risk Factors
  • Prior DTs or withdrawal seizures
  • Heavy, prolonged drinking
  • Concurrent illness
  • Older age

Mortality: Up to 5% if untreated; requires ICU

Treatment of Withdrawal

  • Benzodiazepines: First-line (chlordiazepoxide, lorazepam, diazepam)
  • Use symptom-triggered dosing (CIWA-Ar protocol)
  • Thiamine: Before glucose to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy
  • Electrolyte repletion (magnesium, potassium)
  • IV fluids

12. Opioid Use Disorder

‚ñº

Opioid Intoxication

üìã Classic Triad
  • Miosis (pinpoint pupils)
  • Respiratory depression
  • Decreased consciousness

Treatment: Naloxone (Narcan) ‚Äî 0.4-2mg IV/IM/SC; may need repeated doses

Opioid Withdrawal

NOT life-threatening (unlike alcohol, benzodiazepine withdrawal)

  • Symptoms: Lacrimation, rhinorrhea, yawning, piloerection ("goose bumps"), diarrhea, muscle aches, dilated pupils, anxiety, insomnia
  • Onset: 8-24 hours after last use (short-acting); 36-72 hours (methadone)

Medications for Opioid Use Disorder (MOUD)

MedicationMechanismNotes
Buprenorphine (+ naloxone) Partial Œº-agonist X-waiver no longer required; can prescribe in office; ceiling effect (safer)
Methadone Full Œº-agonist Must dispense from licensed program (OTP); QTc prolongation
Naltrexone Opioid antagonist Monthly injection (Vivitrol); patient must be opioid-free 7-10 days
‚úì Key Points
  • MOUD significantly reduces mortality and overdose
  • Any physician can prescribe buprenorphine (DEA X-waiver eliminated 2023)
  • Long-term/indefinite treatment is appropriate ‚Äî not "just substitution"

Symptomatic Treatment of Withdrawal

  • Clonidine: Autonomic symptoms (monitor for hypotension)
  • Loperamide: Diarrhea
  • NSAIDs: Muscle aches
  • Ondansetron: Nausea/vomiting
  • Trazodone: Insomnia

13. Other Substance Use

‚ñº

Benzodiazepine Withdrawal

üö® Potentially Life-Threatening
  • Similar to alcohol withdrawal: anxiety, tremor, seizures
  • Can be delayed (days to weeks) with long-acting agents
  • Treatment: Gradual taper; may substitute long-acting for short-acting

Stimulants (Cocaine, Amphetamines)

  • Intoxication: Euphoria, tachycardia, HTN, hyperthermia, dilated pupils, paranoia, psychosis
  • Complications: MI (even in young), stroke, arrhythmia
  • Treatment: Supportive; benzodiazepines for agitation; avoid beta-blockers alone (unopposed alpha)
  • Withdrawal: "Crash" ‚Äî fatigue, depression, hypersomnia, hyperphagia (not dangerous)

Cannabis

  • Intoxication: Euphoria, impaired coordination, red eyes, increased appetite, tachycardia
  • Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome: Cyclic vomiting with hot showers for relief; treat with capsaicin cream, haloperidol
  • Withdrawal: Irritability, insomnia, decreased appetite (mild, not dangerous)

Tobacco Cessation

TreatmentDetails
VareniclineMost effective single agent; partial nicotine agonist
Bupropion SRStart 1-2 weeks before quit date; seizure risk
NRTPatch, gum, lozenge, inhaler, spray; can combine
CombinationNRT + varenicline or bupropion may be most effective

15. Eating Disorders

‚ñº

Anorexia Nervosa

üìã DSM-5 Criteria
  1. Restriction of energy intake ‚Üí significantly low body weight
  2. Intense fear of gaining weight despite being underweight
  3. Disturbance in body image perception

Subtypes: Restricting type vs Binge-eating/purging type

Medical Complications

  • Cardiac: Bradycardia, hypotension, arrhythmias, QTc prolongation
  • Electrolytes: Hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, hypomagnesemia
  • Endocrine: Amenorrhea, hypothyroidism, low estrogen/testosterone
  • GI: Constipation, delayed gastric emptying
  • Bone: Osteoporosis
  • Hematologic: Anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia
üö® Refeeding Syndrome
  • Occurs with nutritional rehabilitation in severely malnourished patients
  • Hypophosphatemia is the hallmark (also low K, Mg)
  • Can cause cardiac failure, arrhythmias, seizures, death
  • Prevention: Start feeding slowly ("start low, go slow"), monitor electrolytes, supplement phosphate

Bulimia Nervosa

  • Recurrent binge eating with compensatory behaviors (purging, laxatives, exercise, fasting)
  • At least weekly for 3 months
  • Self-evaluation unduly influenced by weight/shape
  • Complications: Dental erosion, parotid enlargement, Russell's sign (knuckle calluses), hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis, esophageal tears

Binge Eating Disorder

  • Recurrent binge eating WITHOUT compensatory behaviors
  • Often associated with obesity
  • Treatment: CBT, lisdexamfetamine (only FDA-approved medication)

17. Sleep Disorders

‚ñº

Insomnia

  • Difficulty initiating, maintaining, or early morning awakening
  • First-line: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)
  • Pharmacotherapy (short-term): Low-dose trazodone, melatonin, Z-drugs (zolpidem, eszopiclone)
  • Avoid in elderly: Benzodiazepines, antihistamines, anticholinergics

OSA

  • Diagnosis: Polysomnography (AHI ‚â•5 with symptoms or AHI ‚â•15)
  • Treatment: CPAP is first-line; weight loss; positional therapy; oral appliances; surgery for anatomical issues

Restless Legs Syndrome

  • Urge to move legs, worse at rest, worse at night, relieved by movement
  • Check: Iron studies (treat if ferritin <75)
  • Treatment: Gabapentin/pregabalin preferred; dopamine agonists (risk of augmentation)

18. High-Yield Board Pearls

‚ñº

Depression Pearls

  • üíé PHQ-9 ‚â•10 = screen positive, needs further evaluation
  • üíé Screen for bipolar before starting antidepressant (risk of triggering mania)
  • üíé Bupropion: No sexual side effects; contraindicated in seizures, eating disorders
  • üíé Antidepressants take 2-4 weeks for initial effect; 6-8 weeks for full response

Anxiety Pearls

  • üíé SSRIs/SNRIs are first-line for GAD, panic, PTSD
  • üíé Avoid long-term benzodiazepines ‚Äî use for acute/short-term only
  • üíé Prazosin for PTSD nightmares
  • üíé Beta-blockers only for performance anxiety ‚Äî not effective for generalized anxiety

Bipolar Pearls

  • üíé Antidepressant monotherapy is contraindicated in bipolar
  • üíé Lithium: Narrow therapeutic window; interacts with NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, thiazides
  • üíé Valproate: Teratogenic (neural tube defects) ‚Äî avoid in pregnancy
  • üíé Quetiapine is effective for both acute mania and bipolar depression

Substance Use Pearls

  • üíé Alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal can be life-threatening (seizures, DTs)
  • üíé Opioid withdrawal is NOT life-threatening ‚Äî treat symptoms, start MOUD
  • üíé Give thiamine BEFORE glucose in suspected Wernicke encephalopathy
  • üíé Naltrexone: Good for alcohol AND opioid use disorder; contraindicated if on opioids
  • üíé Any physician can prescribe buprenorphine (X-waiver eliminated)

Psychosis/NMS Pearls

  • üíé Clozapine is reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia; requires ANC monitoring
  • üíé NMS: Fever + rigidity + altered mental status + elevated CK; treat with dantrolene, bromocriptine
  • üíé Serotonin syndrome: Rapid onset, hyperreflexia, clonus; treat with cyproheptadine
  • üíé Tardive dyskinesia: Late-onset, treat with VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine)

Somatic/Capacity Pearls

  • üíé Conversion disorder: Neurologic symptoms incompatible with anatomy; positive signs on exam
  • üíé Factitious disorder: Intentional symptoms for sick role (internal motivation)
  • üíé Malingering: Intentional symptoms for external gain (not a disorder)
  • üíé Capacity: Understanding, Appreciation, Reasoning, Choice ‚Äî decision-specific

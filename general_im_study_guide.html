USPSTF Grading System

üìä Recommendation Grades
  • Grade A: High certainty of substantial benefit ‚Üí RECOMMEND
  • Grade B: High certainty of moderate benefit OR moderate certainty of substantial benefit ‚Üí RECOMMEND
  • Grade C: Moderate certainty of small benefit ‚Üí OFFER SELECTIVELY based on patient circumstances
  • Grade D: Evidence shows no benefit or harms outweigh benefits ‚Üí DO NOT OFFER
  • Grade I: Insufficient evidence ‚Üí Cannot make recommendation

Colorectal Cancer Screening Details

‚úì Screening Options (All Acceptable)
  • Colonoscopy: Every 10 years
  • FIT (fecal immunochemical test): Annual
  • FIT-DNA (Cologuard): Every 1-3 years
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy: Every 5 years
  • CT colonography: Every 5 years

When to Stop Screening

  • Age 75-85: Individualize based on health, prior screening, preferences
  • Age >85: Do not screen
  • Life expectancy <10 years: Generally stop screening

Breast Cancer Screening

  • Age 40-49: Decision to start should be individual (Grade B)
  • Age 50-74: Biennial mammography (Grade B)
  • Age ‚â•75: Insufficient evidence (Grade I)
  • BRCA carriers: Start screening at age 25-30 with MRI

Cervical Cancer Screening

üìã Current Recommendations
  • Age 21-29: Pap smear every 3 years (no HPV testing)
  • Age 30-65: Pap + HPV every 5 years (preferred) OR Pap every 3 years OR HPV alone every 5 years
  • Age >65: Stop if adequate prior screening (3 consecutive negative Paps or 2 negative cotests in 10 years, most recent within 5 years)
  • Post-hysterectomy: No screening if cervix removed for benign disease

Lung Cancer Screening

LDCT Criteria:

  • Age 50-80 years
  • ‚â•20 pack-year smoking history
  • Current smoker OR quit within past 15 years
  • No symptoms of lung cancer
üö´ Do NOT Screen (Grade D)
  • Ovarian cancer (CA-125, ultrasound)
  • Prostate cancer (PSA) ‚Äî routine screening; shared decision for ages 55-69
  • Pancreatic cancer
  • Thyroid cancer (neck palpation, ultrasound)
  • Testicular cancer

‚ñº

Hypertension Screening

  • Confirm elevated readings with out-of-office measurements (ABPM or home BP)

Lipid Screening

  • Age ‚â•60: Do NOT initiate aspirin for primary prevention (Grade D)

  • Men 65-75 never smoked: Selective screening (Grade C)
  • Women who never smoked: Do not screen (Grade D)

5. Other Important Screenings

‚ñº

Diabetes Screening

  • Adults 35-70 who are overweight/obese: Screen (Grade B)
  • Methods: Fasting glucose, HbA1c, or 2-hour OGTT
  • Screen earlier if additional risk factors (family history, GDM, PCOS, high-risk ethnicity)

Osteoporosis Screening

  • Women ‚â•65: DEXA scan (Grade B)
  • Postmenopausal women <65: Screen if 10-year fracture risk ‚â• that of 65-year-old white woman (use FRAX)
  • Men: No routine screening recommendation

Depression Screening

  • Screen all adults (Grade B)
  • Use validated tool: PHQ-2, PHQ-9, or equivalent
  • Must have systems in place for diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up

Unhealthy Alcohol Use

  • Screen all adults ‚â•18 (Grade B)
  • Use validated screening tool: AUDIT-C, single question
  • Provide brief behavioral counseling for those with risky drinking

Tobacco Use

  • Ask all adults about tobacco use (Grade A)
  • Provide cessation interventions for those who use tobacco
  • Interventions: behavioral counseling + pharmacotherapy (varenicline, NRT, bupropion)

Obesity

  • Screen all adults for obesity (Grade B)
  • Offer intensive behavioral interventions for BMI ‚â•30

7. Preoperative Cardiac Risk Assessment

‚ñº

ACC/AHA Stepwise Approach

  1. Emergency surgery? ‚Üí Proceed with risk stratification and modification perioperatively
  2. Active cardiac condition? (unstable angina, decompensated HF, significant arrhythmia, severe valvular disease) ‚Üí Evaluate and treat before elective surgery
  3. Low-risk surgery? ‚Üí Proceed without cardiac testing
  4. Functional capacity ‚â•4 METs? ‚Üí Proceed without further testing
  5. <4 METs or unknown + elevated risk? ‚Üí Consider stress testing if it will change management
üö´ When NOT to Order Preoperative Stress Testing
  • Low-risk surgery
  • Patient has good functional capacity (‚â•4 METs)
  • Result will not change management
  • Revascularization not indicated regardless of result

Preoperative ECG

  • Not routine ‚Äî obtain only if clinically indicated
  • Consider if: known CVD, arrhythmia, structural heart disease, or high-risk surgery in patients with risk factors
  • NOT recommended for low-risk surgery in asymptomatic patients

Preoperative Echocardiography

  • NOT routine
  • Indicated if: suspected severe valvular disease, new dyspnea of unknown cause, known HF with worsening symptoms
  • Recent echo (<1 year) with no clinical change = no repeat needed

8. Perioperative Medication Management

‚ñº

Beta-Blockers

‚úì Continue

Patients already taking beta-blockers should continue perioperatively

üö´ Do NOT Start De Novo

Routine initiation of beta-blockers before surgery is NOT recommended (POISE trial showed increased stroke and death)

Statins

  • Continue in patients already taking
  • Reasonable to start preoperatively in vascular surgery patients

ACE Inhibitors / ARBs

  • May hold on morning of surgery ‚Äî risk of intraoperative hypotension
  • Resume when hemodynamically stable postoperatively
  • If held, ensure restart before discharge

Antiplatelet Agents

ScenarioManagement
Primary prevention aspirin Hold 7-10 days before surgery
Secondary prevention aspirin Continue perioperatively (except high-bleed-risk surgery)
Recent BMS (<30 days) Delay elective surgery; DAPT required
Recent DES (<6 months) Delay elective surgery if possible
DES >6 months, stable May hold P2Y12 inhibitor 5-7 days; continue aspirin

Anticoagulants

DrugHold Before SurgeryWhen to Resume
Warfarin5 days12-24 hours post-op (low bleed risk)
Dabigatran2-4 days (based on CrCl)24-48 hours post-op
Rivaroxaban/Apixaban2-3 days24-48 hours post-op

Bridging Anticoagulation

‚ö†Ô∏è When to Bridge (BRIDGE Trial)
  • High-risk for thrombosis: Mechanical mitral valve, recent VTE (<3 months), high-risk thrombophilia ‚Üí Bridge with LMWH
  • Moderate/Low risk Afib: Do NOT bridge ‚Äî similar thrombotic outcomes, more bleeding

Diabetes Medications

  • Metformin: Hold on day of surgery (lactic acidosis risk with contrast/hypoperfusion)
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: Hold 3-4 days before surgery (euglycemic DKA risk)
  • Sulfonylureas: Hold on day of surgery
  • Insulin: Reduce long-acting by 25-50%; hold short-acting

Preoperative Pulmonary Function Tests

üö´ PFTs NOT Routinely Recommended
  • PFTs do not predict postoperative pulmonary complications better than clinical assessment
  • Indications: Lung resection surgery (to assess resectability), unexplained dyspnea
  • Patients with known COPD/asthma should be optimized clinically (not with PFTs)

Preoperative Chest X-ray

  • NOT routine
  • Obtain if: acute cardiopulmonary symptoms or signs, new/unexplained findings on exam

Risk Reduction Strategies

  • Smoking cessation: Ideally ‚â•8 weeks before surgery; any duration helps
  • Optimize COPD/asthma: Ensure on appropriate maintenance therapy
  • Incentive spirometry: Pre- and postoperatively
  • Lung expansion maneuvers
  • Early mobilization
  • Avoid long-acting neuromuscular blockers

11. Medical Ethics Principles

‚ñº

Four Core Principles (Beauchamp & Childress)

PrincipleDefinitionExample
Autonomy Respect patient's right to make decisions Informed consent, respecting refusal
Beneficence Act in patient's best interest Recommending appropriate treatment
Non-maleficence Do no harm Avoiding unnecessary procedures
Justice Fair distribution of resources Triage, allocation decisions

Informed Consent

üìã Elements of Valid Informed Consent
  1. Disclosure: Nature of procedure, risks, benefits, alternatives
  2. Understanding: Patient comprehends information
  3. Voluntariness: Decision made without coercion
  4. Competence: Patient has capacity to decide
  5. Consent: Patient agrees to proceed

Exceptions to Informed Consent

  • Emergency: Immediate threat to life, patient unable to consent, no surrogate available
  • Waiver: Patient voluntarily waives right to information
  • Therapeutic privilege: Disclosure would harm patient (rarely justified)
  • Incapacity: Surrogate decision-maker provides consent

12. Decision-Making Capacity

‚ñº

Capacity vs. Competence

  • Capacity: Clinical determination by physician; decision-specific
  • Competence: Legal determination by court; global

Four Elements of Capacity

üß† Assess Capacity
  1. Understanding: Can patient state the medical problem and proposed treatment?
  2. Appreciation: Does patient acknowledge how this applies to their situation?
  3. Reasoning: Can patient weigh risks/benefits and consider alternatives?
  4. Choice: Can patient clearly communicate a decision?

Key Principles

  • Capacity is decision-specific ‚Äî patient may have capacity for some decisions but not others
  • Capacity is time-specific ‚Äî may fluctuate (delirium, medications)
  • Disagreement with physician does NOT equal lack of capacity
  • Capacity may need reassessment if circumstances change
‚ö†Ô∏è Board Pearl

A patient who refuses recommended treatment is NOT automatically lacking capacity. You must assess the four elements. A patient with capacity has the right to refuse any treatment, even life-saving therapy.

When Patient Lacks Capacity

  1. Identify surrogate decision-maker (healthcare proxy > spouse > adult children > parents > other family)
  2. Surrogate should use substituted judgment (what patient would want)
  3. If patient's wishes unknown, use best interest standard

13. Advance Directives

‚ñº

Types of Advance Directives

TypeDescription
Living Will Written document specifying treatment preferences in specific situations (e.g., terminal illness, PVS)
Healthcare Proxy/Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare Designates a person to make healthcare decisions if patient loses capacity
POLST/MOLST Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment ‚Äî actionable medical orders signed by physician

Key Points

  • Advance directives only take effect when patient lacks capacity
  • Patient can revoke or change at any time while having capacity
  • Oral statements to family/physicians are valid (though written is preferred)
  • Healthcare proxy can override written directive if in patient's best interest

DNR/DNI Orders

  • DNR: No CPR (chest compressions, defibrillation)
  • DNI: No endotracheal intubation
  • DNR/DNI does NOT mean "do not treat" ‚Äî other interventions can continue
  • DNR must be addressed preoperatively ‚Äî may be suspended during surgery with patient consent

14. Confidentiality & Privacy

‚ñº

HIPAA Basics

  • Protected Health Information (PHI) cannot be disclosed without patient authorization
  • Applies to all healthcare providers and their business associates
  • Patients have right to access and amend their records

Exceptions to Confidentiality

‚ö†Ô∏è When Disclosure is Permitted/Required
  • Mandatory reporting: Suspected abuse (child, elder, domestic), gunshot wounds, certain infectious diseases
  • Imminent harm to identifiable third party: Duty to warn/protect (Tarasoff)
  • Public health: Reportable diseases to health departments
  • Court order/subpoena
  • Workers' compensation claims
  • When patient consents

Adolescent Confidentiality

  • Minors may consent to care for: STIs, substance abuse, contraception, mental health (varies by state)
  • Emancipated minors can consent to all care
  • Mature minor doctrine may apply

15. End-of-Life Care

‚ñº

Withholding vs. Withdrawing Treatment

üìã Key Concept

Ethically and legally, withholding and withdrawing treatment are equivalent. There is no ethical obligation to continue a treatment that is no longer beneficial or desired.

Palliative Care vs. Hospice

FeaturePalliative CareHospice
PrognosisAny serious illness<6 months life expectancy
Curative treatmentMay continueForegone (comfort focus)
SettingAny (hospital, clinic, home)Usually home or facility
InsuranceStandard billingMedicare Hospice Benefit

Medical Aid in Dying (MAID)

  • Legal in some US states (Oregon, Washington, California, etc.)
  • Requirements: terminal illness (<6 months), decision-making capacity, multiple requests, waiting periods
  • Physician prescribes lethal medication; patient self-administers
  • Distinguished from euthanasia (physician administers ‚Äî illegal in US)

Palliative Sedation

  • Use of sedatives to reduce consciousness in dying patients with refractory symptoms
  • Ethically acceptable when: symptoms refractory, death imminent, patient/surrogate consent
  • Intent is comfort, not hastening death (principle of double effect)

16. Patient Safety

‚ñº

Types of Medical Errors

TypeDefinitionExample
Adverse Event Harm from medical care (not disease) Drug reaction, surgical complication
Preventable Adverse Event Adverse event due to error Wrong-site surgery, medication error
Near Miss Error caught before reaching patient Wrong medication caught at pharmacy
Never Event Serious, preventable event that should never occur Wrong-site surgery, retained foreign body

Swiss Cheese Model

Errors occur when multiple system failures align (like holes in swiss cheese slices lining up). Improving safety requires multiple layers of defense.

Error Disclosure

‚úì When to Disclose
  • All harmful errors should be disclosed to patient/family
  • Disclosure should be: prompt, honest, empathetic, include plan to prevent recurrence
  • Near misses: disclose to patient if appropriate; report internally

Malpractice

Four elements required ("Four D's"):

  1. Duty: Physician-patient relationship exists
  2. Dereliction: Breach of standard of care
  3. Direct causation: Breach directly caused harm
  4. Damages: Patient suffered harm

PDSA Cycle

üìä Plan-Do-Study-Act
  1. Plan: Identify problem, develop hypothesis, plan intervention
  2. Do: Implement intervention on small scale
  3. Study: Analyze results, compare to predictions
  4. Act: If successful, implement broadly; if not, revise and repeat

Root Cause Analysis

  • Systematic analysis of adverse event to identify contributing factors
  • Focus on system failures, not individual blame
  • Uses tools like: fishbone diagram, 5 Whys, failure mode analysis

High-Reliability Organizations

  • Preoccupation with failure
  • Reluctance to simplify
  • Sensitivity to operations
  • Commitment to resilience
  • Deference to expertise

18. Interpreting Diagnostic Tests

‚ñº

Key Definitions

TermDefinitionMnemonic
Sensitivity TP / (TP + FN) ‚Äî ability to detect disease SnNOut: Sensitive test, Negative result rules OUT
Specificity TN / (TN + FP) ‚Äî ability to confirm absence SpPIn: Specific test, Positive result rules IN
PPV TP / (TP + FP) ‚Äî probability disease present if positive Affected by prevalence
NPV TN / (TN + FN) ‚Äî probability no disease if negative Affected by prevalence

Likelihood Ratios

  • LR+ = Sensitivity / (1 - Specificity) ‚Äî how much more likely positive result in diseased
  • LR- = (1 - Sensitivity) / Specificity ‚Äî how much more likely negative result in diseased
  • LR+ >10 or LR- <0.1 are most useful

Effect of Prevalence

  • Sensitivity and specificity are constant regardless of prevalence
  • PPV increases with higher prevalence
  • NPV decreases with higher prevalence
‚ö†Ô∏è Board Pearl

For a screening test in low-prevalence population, even with high sensitivity/specificity, PPV will be low (many false positives). This is why screening is targeted to higher-risk populations.

19. Evidence-Based Medicine

‚ñº

Hierarchy of Evidence

  1. Systematic reviews / meta-analyses of RCTs
  2. Individual RCTs
  3. Cohort studies
  4. Case-control studies
  5. Case series / case reports
  6. Expert opinion

Study Types

TypeDescriptionMeasures
RCT Random assignment to intervention/control RR, ARR, NNT
Cohort Exposed vs unexposed followed over time Relative Risk (RR)
Case-Control Cases vs controls, look back at exposure Odds Ratio (OR)
Cross-Sectional Snapshot in time Prevalence

Key Statistics

  • Relative Risk (RR): Risk in exposed / Risk in unexposed
  • Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR): Risk in control - Risk in treatment
  • Number Needed to Treat (NNT): 1 / ARR ‚Äî patients to treat to prevent one event
  • Number Needed to Harm (NNH): 1 / ARI ‚Äî patients treated for one adverse event

Bias

TypeDescription
Selection biasNon-random selection of subjects
Recall biasDifferential recall of exposures (case-control)
Observer biasObserver interpretation affected by knowledge
Lead-time biasEarlier detection appears to prolong survival
Length-time biasScreening preferentially detects slower-growing disease
ConfoundingThird variable associated with both exposure and outcome

20. Professionalism

‚ñº

Core Elements

  • Primacy of patient welfare
  • Patient autonomy
  • Social justice
  • Honesty and integrity
  • Confidentiality
  • Commitment to competence

Conflicts of Interest

  • Financial relationships with industry
  • Self-referral
  • Gifts from pharmaceutical companies
  • Disclosure is key ‚Äî transparency to patients and institutions

Impaired Physicians

  • Obligation to report colleagues who may be impaired
  • Most states have physician health programs
  • Balance patient safety with support for colleague

Sexual Boundaries

  • Sexual contact with current patients is always unethical
  • Sexual contact with former patients may be unethical (depends on nature of prior relationship, power differential, time elapsed)
  • Sexual harassment: zero tolerance

Social Media

  • Never post PHI or patient-identifiable information
  • Maintain professional boundaries
  • Be aware of institutional policies

21. High-Yield Board Pearls

‚ñº

Preventive Care Pearls

  • üíé Grade D = Do not offer: PSA routine screening, ovarian cancer screening, thyroid cancer screening, carotid ultrasound in asymptomatic
  • üíé Aspirin primary prevention: Do NOT start in patients ‚â•60 (Grade D)
  • üíé AAA screening: Men 65-75 who ever smoked ‚Äî ONE TIME ultrasound
  • üíé Stop screening: When life expectancy <10 years (cancer screening unlikely to benefit)

  • üíé Functional capacity ‚â•4 METs (climb a flight of stairs) = low cardiac risk, no testing needed
  • üíé Continue beta-blockers perioperatively; do NOT start de novo (POISE)
  • üíé Bridging anticoagulation: Generally do NOT bridge Afib unless mechanical valve or very high risk
  • üíé Hold SGLT2i 3-4 days before surgery (euglycemic DKA risk)
  • üíé PFTs NOT routine before non-thoracic surgery

Ethics Pearls

  • üíé Capacity is decision-specific ‚Äî assess 4 elements: Understanding, Appreciation, Reasoning, Choice
  • üíé Disagreeing with physician ‚â† lack of capacity
  • üíé Withholding = Withdrawing treatment ethically and legally
  • üíé Break confidentiality: Imminent harm to identifiable third party, mandatory reporting (abuse, certain diseases)

Quality & Safety Pearls

  • üíé Always disclose harmful errors to patients
  • üíé PDSA cycle: Plan-Do-Study-Act for QI
  • üíé Root cause analysis: Focus on systems, not individuals
  • üíé SnNOut / SpPIn: Sensitive test + negative = rule out; Specific test + positive = rule in

Statistics Pearls

  • üíé NNT = 1/ARR
  • üíé Sensitivity/Specificity don't change with prevalence; PPV/NPV do
  • üíé Lead-time bias: Earlier detection appears to increase survival without actually prolonging life
  • üíé Intention-to-treat analysis preserves randomization, prevents attrition bias

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

HIGH YIELD
Diagnosis
  • Clinical diagnosis: Typical symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation) + response to PPI trial
  • EGD indications: Alarm symptoms, PPI non-response, long-standing symptoms (Barrett's screening)
  • pH monitoring: If diagnosis uncertain, refractory symptoms, pre-surgical evaluation
  • Alarm symptoms: Dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss, anemia, GI bleeding
üìò ACG GERD Guidelines (2022)
Treatment Approach:
  • Lifestyle: Weight loss (if overweight), avoid late meals, elevate head of bed
  • PPI therapy: Standard dose √ó 8 weeks; if responsive, attempt to step down
  • H2RA: For mild/intermittent symptoms or as add-on for nocturnal symptoms
  • Refractory GERD: Confirm diagnosis (pH testing), optimize PPI timing (30-60 min before meals)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: PPI Timing
PPIs work best when taken 30-60 minutes BEFORE meals (need active proton pumps to inhibit). Once-daily dosing should be before breakfast. Twice-daily = before breakfast and dinner.
Extraesophageal Manifestations
  • Chronic cough, asthma, laryngitis
  • Dental erosions
  • Note: Less responsive to PPI therapy; ensure adequate GERD diagnosis before attributing to reflux
üö® LONG-TERM PPI RISKS
  • C. difficile infection: Modest increased risk
  • Bone fractures: Minimal risk; calcium/vitamin D not routinely needed
  • Hypomagnesemia: Rare; check if on diuretics or symptoms develop
  • B12 deficiency: Consider monitoring in long-term users
  • CKD/AIN: Association reported; causality uncertain

Bottom line: Use lowest effective dose; benefits outweigh risks when indicated

Barrett's Esophagus

HIGH YIELD
Definition & Diagnosis
  • Definition: Intestinal metaplasia of esophageal squamous epithelium (salmon-colored mucosa + goblet cells on biopsy)
  • Risk factors: Chronic GERD, male sex, Caucasian, obesity, smoking, family history
  • Screening: Consider in patients with chronic GERD (>5 years) AND multiple risk factors
üìò ACG Barrett's Guidelines (2022)
Surveillance Intervals:
Dysplasia Grade Interval Management
No dysplasia Every 3-5 years Continue PPI, surveillance
Low-grade dysplasia Every 6-12 months OR ablation Endoscopic eradication therapy preferred
High-grade dysplasia Endoscopic eradication RFA, EMR, or esophagectomy
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Endoscopic Eradication
  • Radiofrequency ablation (RFA): First-line for flat dysplasia
  • Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR): For nodular/raised lesions
  • Goal: Complete eradication of intestinal metaplasia (CE-IM)
  • Continue surveillance after successful ablation (recurrence possible)
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: 58-year-old man with 15 years of GERD. EGD shows salmon-colored mucosa extending 4 cm above GE junction. Biopsy confirms intestinal metaplasia without dysplasia.

Answer: Barrett's esophagus without dysplasia. Continue PPI therapy. Repeat surveillance EGD in 3-5 years.

Achalasia

HIGH YIELD
Pathophysiology & Presentation
  • Mechanism: Loss of inhibitory neurons in myenteric plexus ‚Üí impaired LES relaxation + absent peristalsis
  • Symptoms: Progressive dysphagia to BOTH solids AND liquids, regurgitation of undigested food, chest pain, weight loss
  • Key clue: Dysphagia to liquids AND solids from onset suggests motility disorder (vs mechanical obstruction: solids ‚Üí liquids)
Diagnosis
  • Barium swallow: "Bird's beak" appearance at GE junction, dilated esophagus
  • High-resolution manometry: Gold standard ‚Äî absent peristalsis + impaired LES relaxation (IRP >15 mmHg)
  • EGD: To rule out pseudoachalasia (malignancy at GE junction)
Treatment
  • Pneumatic dilation: Effective; may require repeat; risk of perforation (2-5%)
  • Heller myotomy + fundoplication: Surgical; durable response; laparoscopic preferred
  • POEM (peroral endoscopic myotomy): Endoscopic; similar efficacy to surgery; higher GERD rates
  • Botox injection: Temporary effect (3-6 months); reserve for poor surgical candidates
üö® PSEUDOACHALASIA
Always consider malignancy (especially gastric adenocarcinoma at GE junction) in:
  • Age >60 with new-onset symptoms
  • Rapid symptom progression (<1 year)
  • Significant weight loss
EGD with careful inspection/biopsy of GE junction mandatory

Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Demographics: Young adults, males > females, often with atopy (asthma, eczema, allergies)
  • Symptoms: Dysphagia (solids > liquids), food impaction, chest pain, heartburn
  • Key clue: Young patient with recurrent food impactions
Diagnosis
  • EGD findings: Rings ("trachealization"), furrows, white exudates, strictures
  • Histology: ‚â•15 eosinophils/HPF in esophageal biopsies
  • Must exclude GERD: PPI trial or pH monitoring (note: some EoE is PPI-responsive)
üìò ACG EoE Guidelines (2020)
Treatment Options:
  • PPI: First-line therapy (PPI-responsive EoE is still EoE)
  • Topical steroids: Swallowed fluticasone or budesonide (oral viscous)
  • Dietary elimination: 6-food elimination or targeted elimination
  • Dupilumab: FDA-approved for EoE (IL-4/IL-13 inhibitor)
  • Dilation: For strictures; symptom relief, not disease-modifying
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: 28-year-old man with asthma presents with food impaction requiring emergent EGD. Scope shows esophageal rings and furrows. Biopsies show 45 eosinophils/HPF.

Answer: Eosinophilic esophagitis. Start PPI (first-line) or topical steroids.

Peptic Ulcer Disease

HIGH YIELD
Etiology
  • H. pylori: Most common cause globally; 70-90% of duodenal ulcers, 50-70% of gastric ulcers
  • NSAIDs: Second most common cause; direct mucosal injury + COX-1 inhibition
  • Stress ulcers: ICU patients (mechanical ventilation, coagulopathy)
  • Zollinger-Ellison: Gastrinoma ‚Üí gastric acid hypersecretion
H. pylori Testing
Test Advantages Limitations
Urea breath test High accuracy, non-invasive, confirms active infection Hold PPI 2 weeks, antibiotics 4 weeks before
Stool antigen High accuracy, non-invasive Same hold requirements
Serology No hold required Cannot confirm active infection; remains positive after eradication
Biopsy (histology, rapid urease) Can assess for pathology Invasive; patchy infection may cause false negative
üìò ACG H. pylori Guidelines (2017)
First-Line Regimens (14 days preferred):
  • Bismuth quadruple: PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline (preferred if prior macrolide exposure)
  • Concomitant therapy: PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin + metronidazole
  • Triple therapy (if local clarithromycin resistance <15%): PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin
Confirm eradication: Urea breath test or stool antigen ‚â•4 weeks after completing therapy
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Who to Test for H. pylori
  • Active PUD or history of PUD
  • Low-grade gastric MALT lymphoma
  • After resection of early gastric cancer
  • Uninvestigated dyspepsia (test-and-treat strategy)
  • Long-term NSAID use (test and treat before starting)
  • Unexplained iron deficiency anemia, ITP
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: Patient completes 14-day triple therapy for H. pylori-positive duodenal ulcer. How do you confirm eradication?

Answer: Urea breath test or stool antigen at least 4 weeks after treatment completion and 2 weeks after stopping PPI. Do NOT use serology (remains positive).

Gastritis & Gastropathy

CORE
Autoimmune Gastritis
  • Mechanism: Antibodies against parietal cells and intrinsic factor ‚Üí achlorhydria, B12 deficiency
  • Location: Body/fundus (parietal cell distribution)
  • Associations: Pernicious anemia, other autoimmune conditions (thyroiditis)
  • Cancer risk: Increased risk of gastric adenocarcinoma and carcinoid tumors
  • Labs: Elevated gastrin (due to loss of acid feedback), low B12, anti-parietal cell antibodies
H. pylori Gastritis
  • Location: Antrum predominantly (may progress to pangastritis)
  • Associations: PUD, gastric adenocarcinoma, MALT lymphoma
  • Treatment: Eradication therapy
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: MALT Lymphoma
  • Low-grade B-cell lymphoma associated with H. pylori
  • First-line treatment: H. pylori eradication (70-80% remission in localized disease)
  • Surveillance EGD after eradication
  • Chemo/radiation for persistent or advanced disease

Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Etiology: Gastrin-secreting tumor (gastrinoma), usually duodenum or pancreas
  • Presentation: Severe/refractory PUD, multiple ulcers, ulcers in unusual locations (distal duodenum, jejunum), diarrhea
  • Associations: MEN1 (20-30% of cases) ‚Äî check for hyperparathyroidism, pituitary tumors
Diagnosis
  • Fasting serum gastrin: Markedly elevated (>1000 pg/mL highly suggestive; >10√ó ULN virtually diagnostic)
  • Must be off PPI (PPIs elevate gastrin; hold 1-2 weeks if possible)
  • Gastric pH: Must be <2 (excludes achlorhydria as cause of elevated gastrin)
  • Secretin stimulation test: Paradoxical rise in gastrin (>120 pg/mL increase)
  • Tumor localization: CT, MRI, EUS, somatostatin receptor scintigraphy (Octreoscan) or Ga-68 DOTATATE PET
üö® CAUSES OF ELEVATED GASTRIN
  • PPI use: Most common cause (stop before testing)
  • H. pylori infection
  • Atrophic gastritis / pernicious anemia: (gastric pH will be high)
  • Renal failure: Decreased gastrin clearance
  • Retained antrum post-surgery
  • Gastrinoma (ZES)

Upper GI Bleeding

HIGH YIELD
Common Causes
  • Peptic ulcer disease: Most common (30-50%)
  • Erosive gastritis/esophagitis
  • Esophageal/gastric varices: In cirrhosis
  • Mallory-Weiss tear: Mucosal tear at GE junction after vomiting
  • Malignancy
  • Dieulafoy lesion: Submucosal artery, can cause massive bleeding
üìò ACG UGIB Guidelines (2021)
Initial Management:
  • Resuscitation: IV access, fluids, type and crossmatch
  • Transfusion threshold: Hemoglobin <7 g/dL (restrictive strategy preferred); higher threshold if CAD or hemodynamic instability
  • PPI: IV PPI infusion (no benefit for pre-endoscopy bolus in all patients)
  • Prokinetic: Erythromycin 250mg IV 30-90 min before EGD may improve visualization
  • Timing of EGD: Within 24 hours; urgent (<12h) if hemodynamic instability or high-risk features
üìä GLASGOW-BLATCHFORD SCORE (GBS)
  • BUN, hemoglobin, systolic BP, heart rate, melena, syncope, hepatic disease, cardiac failure
  • Score 0-1: Low risk; may consider outpatient management
  • Higher scores: Need intervention or transfusion more likely

‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Post-Endoscopy Management
  • High-risk stigmata (Ia, Ib, IIa, IIb): IV PPI infusion (80mg bolus then 8 mg/hr) √ó 72 hours
  • Low-risk stigmata (IIc, III): Oral PPI; can discharge early
  • Test and treat H. pylori in all PUD bleeding

Variceal Hemorrhage

HIGH YIELD
üö® VARICEAL BLEEDING IS A MEDICAL EMERGENCY
Mortality 10-20% per episode
üìò AASLD Variceal Bleeding Guidelines
Acute Management:
  • Resuscitation: Restrictive transfusion (Hgb target 7-8 g/dL); avoid over-transfusion (increases portal pressure)
  • Vasoactive drugs: Octreotide 50 mcg bolus ‚Üí 50 mcg/hr infusion (or terlipressin where available) √ó 2-5 days
  • Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily √ó 7 days (reduces mortality!)
  • EGD within 12 hours: Endoscopic band ligation (EBL) preferred over sclerotherapy
  • Balloon tamponade: Bridge to definitive therapy if uncontrolled bleeding
  • TIPS: For refractory bleeding or early TIPS in high-risk patients (Child C or B with active bleeding)
Secondary Prophylaxis
  • Non-selective beta-blocker: Propranolol or nadolol (target HR 55-60 or 25% reduction)
  • PLUS serial EBL until varices eradicated
  • Carvedilol: May be more effective than traditional NSBB
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Primary Prophylaxis
  • Screen for varices: All patients with cirrhosis at diagnosis
  • No varices: Repeat EGD in 2-3 years (or 1 year if decompensated)
  • Small varices: NSBB or surveillance
  • Medium/large varices: NSBB OR endoscopic band ligation

Lower GI Bleeding

HIGH YIELD
Common Causes
  • Diverticulosis: Most common cause of massive LGIB; painless, often right-sided
  • Angiodysplasia (AVMs): Especially in elderly, CKD, aortic stenosis
  • Hemorrhoids: Most common cause of minor LGIB
  • Colorectal cancer/polyps
  • Colitis: IBD, ischemic, infectious, radiation
  • Post-polypectomy bleeding
üìò ACG LGIB Guidelines (2016)
Evaluation:
  • Colonoscopy: Preferred first-line diagnostic and therapeutic modality
  • Timing: Within 24 hours after bowel prep (may be urgent if hemodynamically significant)
  • CTA: If ongoing bleeding and colonoscopy not feasible/non-diagnostic
  • Tagged RBC scan: Detects slower bleeds; localizes for angiography
  • Angiography: For ongoing brisk bleeding; allows embolization
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: 72-year-old with painless, large-volume maroon stool. Hemodynamically stable after resuscitation. No prior colonoscopy.

Answer: Likely diverticular bleed. Colonoscopy after bowel prep within 24 hours for diagnosis and potential therapy.

üíé HEYDE SYNDROME
GI bleeding from angiodysplasia + aortic stenosis. Mechanism: Acquired von Willebrand syndrome (high shear stress across stenotic valve destroys vWF multimers). Treatment: AVR can resolve bleeding.

Celiac Disease

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Presentation
  • Classic: Diarrhea, steatorrhea, weight loss, abdominal bloating
  • Non-classic: Iron deficiency anemia, osteoporosis, elevated transaminases, dermatitis herpetiformis
  • Silent: Positive serology + villous atrophy but no symptoms
  • Associations: Type 1 DM, autoimmune thyroiditis, Down syndrome, IgA deficiency
Diagnosis
  • First-line test: IgA tissue transglutaminase (TTG-IgA) ‚Äî high sensitivity and specificity
  • Check total IgA: IgA deficiency causes false negative TTG-IgA; use IgG-based tests if deficient
  • Must be on gluten-containing diet at time of testing
  • Confirmation: EGD with duodenal biopsies (‚â•4 samples from duodenum)
  • Histology: Villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, intraepithelial lymphocytosis
üìò ACG Celiac Guidelines (2023)
Treatment:
  • Strict lifelong gluten-free diet (avoid wheat, barley, rye)
  • Dietitian referral essential
  • Screen for nutritional deficiencies (iron, B12, folate, vitamin D, calcium)
  • Pneumococcal vaccination (functional hyposplenism)
  • DEXA scan for bone density
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Non-Responsive Celiac
If symptoms persist despite GFD:
  1. Ongoing gluten exposure (most common)
  2. Other diagnosis (IBS, lactose intolerance, microscopic colitis, SIBO)
  3. Refractory celiac disease
  4. Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL) ‚Äî rare but serious
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: 35-year-old woman with iron deficiency anemia unresponsive to oral iron, mild diarrhea, and a pruritic, vesicular rash on elbows and knees.

Answer: Celiac disease with dermatitis herpetiformis. Test TTG-IgA, confirm with duodenal biopsies. Treat with gluten-free diet (rash also responds).

Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO)

HIGH YIELD
Risk Factors
  • Anatomic: Blind loops, strictures, surgical anastomoses, diverticula
  • Motility disorders: Scleroderma, diabetic gastroparesis, chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction
  • Achlorhydria: PPI use, atrophic gastritis
  • Immune deficiency
  • Cirrhosis
Diagnosis
  • Glucose or lactulose breath test: Measures hydrogen/methane (positive = early rise)
  • Jejunal aspirate and culture: Gold standard (>10¬≥ CFU/mL) but invasive, rarely used
Treatment
  • Rifaximin 550mg TID √ó 14 days: Preferred; non-absorbed, well-tolerated
  • Alternatives: Ciprofloxacin, metronidazole, amoxicillin-clavulanate
  • Recurrence common: May need cyclical or long-term antibiotics
  • Address underlying cause when possible

Ulcerative Colitis

HIGH YIELD
Key Features
  • Distribution: Continuous inflammation, starts at rectum, extends proximally
  • Depth: Mucosal and submucosal only
  • Symptoms: Bloody diarrhea, urgency, tenesmus
  • Endoscopy: Erythema, friability, loss of vascular pattern, pseudopolyps
  • Histology: Crypt abscesses, crypt architectural distortion
üìò AGA/ACG UC Treatment Guidelines
Mild-Moderate (Distal):
  • Topical mesalamine (suppository for proctitis, enema for left-sided) ‚Äî most effective
  • Oral + topical mesalamine for extensive disease
Moderate-Severe:
  • Oral corticosteroids for induction (not maintenance)
  • Thiopurines (azathioprine, 6-MP) for maintenance
  • Biologics: Anti-TNF (infliximab, adalimumab), vedolizumab (gut-selective), ustekinumab, tofacitinib (JAK inhibitor)
Acute Severe UC (Fulminant):
  • IV corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 60mg/day)
  • If no response in 3-5 days: infliximab, cyclosporine, or colectomy
üö® TOXIC MEGACOLON
  • Definition: Colonic dilation >6 cm + systemic toxicity
  • Management: NPO, NG decompression, IV steroids, broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Avoid: Opioids, anticholinergics, colonoscopy
  • Surgery if: Perforation, no improvement in 24-72 hours
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: UC Cancer Surveillance
  • Start surveillance colonoscopy 8 years after diagnosis
  • Interval: Every 1-3 years depending on risk factors
  • Higher risk: Pancolitis, PSC, family history of CRC, prior dysplasia
  • Chromoendoscopy with targeted biopsies preferred over random biopsies

Crohn's Disease

HIGH YIELD
Key Features
  • Distribution: Any part of GI tract (mouth to anus); skip lesions; terminal ileum most common
  • Depth: Transmural inflammation
  • Symptoms: Abdominal pain, diarrhea (often non-bloody), weight loss
  • Endoscopy: Aphthous ulcers, cobblestoning, strictures
  • Histology: Non-caseating granulomas (present in ~30%)

Crohn's Complications
  • Strictures: Inflammatory (treat medically) vs fibrotic (may need dilation/surgery)
  • Fistulas: Perianal, enterocutaneous, enterovesical, enterovaginal
  • Abscesses: Percutaneous drainage + antibiotics; rule out before starting biologics
  • B12 deficiency: Terminal ileal disease/resection
  • Bile acid diarrhea: Ileal resection >100 cm
  • Oxalate kidney stones: Fat malabsorption ‚Üí increased oxalate absorption
üìò Treatment Approach
  • Mild: Budesonide (ileal/right colon), mesalamine less effective than in UC
  • Moderate-Severe: Biologics (anti-TNF, vedolizumab, ustekinumab) ¬± immunomodulators
  • Perianal disease: MRI for fistula mapping, antibiotics (ciprofloxacin + metronidazole), anti-TNF agents
  • Post-surgical prophylaxis: Anti-TNF or thiopurines to prevent recurrence
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Biologics Safety
  • Before starting anti-TNF: Screen for latent TB (PPD/IGRA), Hepatitis B (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs)
  • Avoid live vaccines on immunosuppression
  • Anti-TNF risks: Infections, reactivation of TB/HBV, demyelination, heart failure exacerbation, drug-induced lupus

Diverticular Disease

HIGH YIELD
Diverticulosis
  • Definition: Outpouchings of colonic mucosa through muscular wall
  • Location: Left-sided (sigmoid) in Western populations; right-sided in Asian populations
  • Risk factors: Age, low fiber diet, obesity, sedentary lifestyle
  • Usually asymptomatic; may cause painless bleeding
Acute Diverticulitis
  • Presentation: LLQ pain, fever, leukocytosis
  • Diagnosis: CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast (preferred)
üìò AGA Diverticulitis Guidelines (2015)
Uncomplicated Diverticulitis:
  • Mild: Outpatient management; antibiotics now selective (not always required)
  • If antibiotics used: Ciprofloxacin + metronidazole OR amoxicillin-clavulanate √ó 7-10 days
  • Clear liquid diet advancing as tolerated
Complicated Diverticulitis:
  • Abscess <3 cm: IV antibiotics alone
  • Abscess ‚â•3 cm: Percutaneous drainage + antibiotics
  • Perforation/peritonitis: Surgical intervention
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Colonoscopy After Diverticulitis
  • Perform colonoscopy 6-8 weeks after acute episode to rule out malignancy
  • Especially important if not recently screened
  • Elective sigmoid colectomy: consider after complicated diverticulitis or recurrent episodes in select patients

Microscopic Colitis

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Presentation: Chronic watery diarrhea (non-bloody), often nocturnal
  • Demographics: Middle-aged to elderly women
  • Colonoscopy: NORMAL appearing mucosa
  • Diagnosis: Random colonic biopsies (must biopsy even if mucosa looks normal)
Subtypes
  • Collagenous colitis: Thickened subepithelial collagen band (>10 Œºm)
  • Lymphocytic colitis: Increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (>20/100 epithelial cells)
Treatment
  • First: Stop offending medications (NSAIDs, PPIs, SSRIs)
  • First-line therapy: Budesonide 9mg daily √ó 6-8 weeks (highly effective)
  • Maintenance: May need low-dose budesonide for relapsing disease
  • Alternatives: Bismuth, cholestyramine, mesalamine
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: 62-year-old woman with 6 months of watery diarrhea. Colonoscopy shows normal mucosa. Random biopsies show thickened collagen band.

Answer: Collagenous colitis. Treat with budesonide.

Ischemic Colitis

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Demographics: Elderly with cardiovascular risk factors
  • Presentation: Sudden onset crampy abdominal pain + bloody diarrhea
  • Location: "Watershed" areas ‚Äî splenic flexure, rectosigmoid junction
  • Rectum usually spared (dual blood supply)
Diagnosis & Management
  • CT: Colonic wall thickening, thumbprinting
  • Colonoscopy: Segmental erythema, edema, ulcerations; "single stripe sign"
  • Treatment: Supportive (bowel rest, IV fluids, antibiotics if severe)
  • Most cases self-limited; surgery for perforation, gangrene, or stricture
üö® ACUTE MESENTERIC ISCHEMIA
Different from ischemic colitis!
  • Small bowel involvement; pain out of proportion to exam
  • Causes: Arterial embolism (AF), arterial thrombosis, venous thrombosis, NOMI
  • CT angiography for diagnosis
  • Emergency: Surgical exploration, revascularization, resection of necrotic bowel

Irritable Bowel Syndrome

CORE
üìò Rome IV Criteria
Recurrent abdominal pain ‚â•1 day/week (on average) for past 3 months, associated with ‚â•2 of:
  • Related to defecation
  • Change in stool frequency
  • Change in stool form/appearance
Subtypes: IBS-D (diarrhea), IBS-C (constipation), IBS-M (mixed)
Treatment by Subtype
  • General: Fiber supplementation, diet modification (low FODMAP)
  • IBS-D: Loperamide, rifaximin, eluxadoline, alosetron (women with severe IBS-D)
  • IBS-C: Linaclotide, lubiprostone, plecanatide, tegaserod (women <65)
  • Pain-predominant: TCAs (amitriptyline), SSRIs, antispasmodics (dicyclomine, hyoscyamine)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: When to Test Further
Alarm features requiring workup:
  • Age >50 without prior colonoscopy
  • Rectal bleeding, unintentional weight loss
  • Nocturnal symptoms (waking from sleep)
  • Family history of CRC, IBD, celiac
  • Anemia, elevated inflammatory markers

Piecemeal resection of adenoma ‚â•20mm 6 months (to confirm complete removal)
üíé SERRATED POLYP PATHWAY
  • Alternative pathway to CRC (15-30% of sporadic CRCs)
  • BRAF mutation, MLH1 methylation, MSI-high
  • Sessile serrated lesions often in right colon, flat, mucus-covered
  • Risk of interval cancers due to rapid progression and difficult detection

Hereditary CRC Syndromes

HIGH YIELD
Lynch Syndrome (HNPCC)
  • Genetics: Autosomal dominant; mismatch repair genes (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2)
  • Cancer risk: CRC (up to 80%), endometrial, ovarian, gastric, urinary tract, others
  • Features: Right-sided CRC, early onset, multiple primary cancers, MSI-high tumors
  • Screening: Colonoscopy every 1-2 years starting age 20-25 (or 2-5 years before youngest case in family)
üìù AMSTERDAM II CRITERIA (Lynch)
"3-2-1 Rule": ‚â•3 relatives with Lynch-associated cancer, across ‚â•2 generations, ‚â•1 diagnosed <50 years
Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)
  • Genetics: Autosomal dominant; APC gene mutation
  • Features: >100 adenomatous polyps; 100% CRC risk by age 40 if untreated
  • Extracolonic: Duodenal adenomas (leading cause of death after colectomy), desmoid tumors, osteomas, CHRPE
  • Management: Prophylactic colectomy (typically late teens/early 20s); upper GI surveillance
Attenuated FAP & MUTYH-Associated Polyposis
  • Attenuated FAP: Fewer polyps (10-100), later onset, still high CRC risk
  • MAP: Autosomal RECESSIVE; resembles attenuated FAP; screen siblings
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Universal Tumor Testing
All newly diagnosed CRCs should be tested for Lynch syndrome via:
  • Immunohistochemistry (IHC) for MMR proteins, AND/OR
  • MSI testing
If abnormal ‚Üí germline genetic testing

üü§ Liver Disease

üíä

Approach to Abnormal Liver Tests

HIGH YIELD
Hepatocellular vs Cholestatic Pattern
  • Hepatocellular: ALT/AST predominant elevation (ratio of ALT:ALP >5)
  • Cholestatic: ALP/bilirubin predominant (ratio of ALT:ALP <2)
  • Mixed: Ratio 2-5
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: AST:ALT Ratio
  • AST:ALT >2:1: Think alcoholic liver disease (AST rarely >500)
  • ALT > AST: Most other causes (NAFLD, viral hepatitis)
  • Very high transaminases (>1000): Acute viral hepatitis, ischemic hepatitis, drug/toxin (acetaminophen), autoimmune flare
Isolated Elevated ALP
  • First: Check GGT or 5'-nucleotidase to confirm hepatic source (vs bone)
  • If hepatic: RUQ ultrasound (biliary dilation?)
  • If no dilation: Consider PBC, PSC, infiltrative disease, medications

NAFLD / MASLD

HIGH YIELD
Terminology Update
  • MASLD: Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (new term)
  • MASH: Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (formerly NASH)
Diagnosis
  • Imaging: Hepatic steatosis on ultrasound, CT, or MRI
  • Exclusion: Rule out other causes (alcohol, viral, autoimmune, drugs)
  • Fibrosis staging: FIB-4 score, NAFLD fibrosis score, transient elastography (FibroScan), MR elastography
  • Liver biopsy: Gold standard to distinguish simple steatosis from NASH and stage fibrosis
üìò AASLD NAFLD Guidelines
Management:
  • Lifestyle: Weight loss (7-10% body weight improves NASH); exercise
  • Resmetirom: First FDA-approved drug for NASH with fibrosis (thyroid hormone receptor agonist)
  • Vitamin E: Consider in non-diabetic NASH (800 IU daily)
  • Pioglitazone: May use in NASH (with or without diabetes)
  • GLP-1 agonists: Benefit for NAFLD with diabetes/obesity (semaglutide promising)
  • Bariatric surgery: Can improve/resolve NASH in obesity
üìä FIB-4 SCORE
  • Formula: (Age √ó AST) / (Platelet √ó ‚àöALT)
  • <1.3: Low risk for advanced fibrosis
  • >2.67: High risk for advanced fibrosis
  • 1.3-2.67: Indeterminate; further testing (elastography, biopsy)

Autoimmune Hepatitis

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Demographics: Women > men; bimodal age (10-30 and 40-60)
  • Presentation: Variable ‚Äî asymptomatic, chronic hepatitis, acute severe/fulminant
  • Associations: Other autoimmune diseases (thyroiditis, RA, UC, Sj√∂gren's)
Diagnosis
  • Labs: Elevated transaminases, hypergammaglobulinemia (IgG)
  • Autoantibodies:
    • Type 1: ANA, anti-smooth muscle (ASMA) ‚Äî most common
    • Type 2: Anti-LKM1, anti-LC1 ‚Äî more common in children
  • Liver biopsy: Interface hepatitis, plasma cell infiltration
  • Exclude: Viral hepatitis, Wilson's, drug-induced
Treatment
  • Induction: Prednisone (¬± azathioprine) ‚Äî most respond well
  • Maintenance: Azathioprine monotherapy (often can wean steroids)
  • Treatment duration: Usually lifelong; relapse common if stopped
  • Check TPMT before azathioprine (toxicity risk if deficient)

PBC & PSC

HIGH YIELD
Feature Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC) Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)
Demographics Middle-aged women (90%) Young-middle aged men
Ducts affected Small intrahepatic bile ducts Medium/large intra- and extrahepatic ducts

Antibody Anti-mitochondrial (AMA) ‚Äî 95% p-ANCA (non-specific)

‚ö° BOARD PEARL: PBC Treatment
  • Ursodiol (UDCA) 13-15 mg/kg/day: First-line for all PBC patients
  • Inadequate response: Add obeticholic acid (FXR agonist)
  • Pruritus: Cholestyramine, rifampin, naltrexone, sertraline

Hereditary Hemochromatosis

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Classic triad: Cirrhosis, diabetes, bronze skin ("bronze diabetes")
  • Other manifestations: Cardiomyopathy, arthropathy (2nd/3rd MCP), hypogonadism
  • Typically presents middle age (women later due to menstrual iron loss)
Diagnosis
  • Screen: Transferrin saturation (>45%) and ferritin
  • Confirm: HFE gene testing (C282Y homozygosity most common)
  • Liver biopsy: For staging if suspected cirrhosis or ferritin >1000
  • MRI: Can quantify hepatic iron
Treatment
  • Phlebotomy: Weekly until ferritin <50-100, then maintenance (every 2-4 months)
  • Screen first-degree relatives
  • Avoid: Vitamin C supplements (increases iron absorption), alcohol
  • HCC surveillance if cirrhosis

Wilson Disease

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Genetics: Autosomal recessive; ATP7B gene mutation
  • Age of onset: Usually 5-35 years
  • Liver: Hepatitis, cirrhosis, acute liver failure
  • Neuropsychiatric: Tremor, dysarthria, dystonia, parkinsonism, psychiatric symptoms
  • Eyes: Kayser-Fleischer rings (copper in Descemet's membrane) ‚Äî not always present
Diagnosis
  • Low serum ceruloplasmin (<20 mg/dL)
  • Elevated 24-hour urine copper (>100 Œºg)
  • Kayser-Fleischer rings on slit-lamp exam
  • Liver biopsy: Elevated hepatic copper content
  • Genetic testing if uncertain
üö® WILSON'S WITH ACUTE LIVER FAILURE
Look for: Coombs-negative hemolytic anemia, low ALP, ALP:bilirubin ratio <4, AST:ALT >2.2
High mortality without transplant
Treatment
  • Chelation: D-penicillamine or trientine (removes copper)
  • Zinc: Blocks intestinal copper absorption (maintenance or mild disease)
  • Lifelong treatment required
  • Screen first-degree relatives

üü§ Cirrhosis & Complications

üö®

Cirrhosis Diagnosis & Staging

HIGH YIELD
Diagnosis
  • Clinical + imaging: Often sufficient (nodular liver, splenomegaly, varices)
  • Labs: Low platelets, albumin; elevated INR, bilirubin
  • Elastography: FibroScan or MRE ‚Äî non-invasive fibrosis assessment
  • Liver biopsy: Gold standard but not always needed
üìä CHILD-PUGH SCORE

Assess: Ascites, Encephalopathy, Bilirubin, Albumin, INR (each 1-3 points)

  • Class A (5-6): Compensated, good prognosis
  • Class B (7-9): Significant functional compromise
  • Class C (10-15): Decompensated, poor prognosis
üìä MELD SCORE

Uses: Bilirubin, INR, Creatinine, Sodium (MELD-Na)

  • Predicts 90-day mortality
  • Used for liver transplant allocation
  • MELD ‚â•15: Consider transplant referral

Ascites

HIGH YIELD
Diagnostic Paracentesis
  • Perform on ALL new-onset ascites and hospital admissions with ascites
  • SAAG (Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient):
    • ‚â•1.1 g/dL: Portal hypertension (cirrhosis, heart failure)
    • <1.1 g/dL: Non-portal hypertension (malignancy, TB, nephrotic)
  • Total protein: Low (<2.5) = cirrhosis; High = cardiac ascites, malignancy
üìò AASLD Ascites Guidelines
Treatment:
  • Sodium restriction: 2g/day (88 mEq)
  • Diuretics: Spironolactone 100mg + furosemide 40mg (100:40 ratio); titrate to max 400:160
  • Goal: Weight loss 0.5 kg/day (without edema) or 1 kg/day (with edema)
  • Large volume paracentesis (>5L): Give albumin 6-8 g/L removed
üö® REFRACTORY ASCITES
  • Diuretic-resistant or diuretic-intractable
  • Options: Serial large-volume paracentesis, TIPS, liver transplant
  • Avoid NSAIDs (cause sodium retention and AKI)

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

HIGH YIELD
Diagnosis
  • Ascitic fluid PMN ‚â•250 cells/mm¬≥ ‚Äî diagnostic threshold
  • Culture often negative (~40% positive)
  • Symptoms may be subtle: Fever, abdominal pain, encephalopathy, renal dysfunction
  • Low threshold to tap any cirrhotic patient with clinical deterioration
üìò SBP Treatment
  • Empiric antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily (or cefotaxime) √ó 5 days
  • Alternative: Fluoroquinolone if no prior FQ prophylaxis
  • IV Albumin: 1.5 g/kg on day 1 + 1 g/kg on day 3 (reduces mortality and HRS)
  • Repeat paracentesis at 48h if not improving clinically
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: SBP Prophylaxis
Primary prophylaxis:
  • GI bleed: Ceftriaxone 1g √ó 7 days (then norfloxacin/cipro)
  • Low ascitic fluid protein (<1.5) with liver failure or renal dysfunction
Secondary prophylaxis: Lifelong after SBP episode (norfloxacin 400mg daily or TMP-SMX DS daily)

Hepatic Encephalopathy

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Spectrum: Minimal (subtle cognitive deficits) ‚Üí Overt (confusion, asterixis, coma)
  • Precipitants: Infection, GI bleed, constipation, dehydration, sedatives, hypokalemia, high protein intake, TIPS
  • Asterixis: "Flapping tremor" ‚Äî not specific for HE
  • Ammonia level: Correlates poorly with severity; don't follow serially
üìò Treatment
  • Identify and treat precipitants (most important!)
  • Lactulose: Titrate to 2-3 soft bowel movements/day (trap ammonia as NH4+)
  • Rifaximin: Add for recurrent HE (reduces recurrence by 50%)
  • Protein restriction: NOT recommended (leads to malnutrition)
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: Cirrhotic patient presents confused with asterixis after large-volume paracentesis without albumin replacement.

Answer: Hepatic encephalopathy likely precipitated by paracentesis-induced circulatory dysfunction. Treat with lactulose, ensure albumin given for future LVPs.

Hepatorenal Syndrome

HIGH YIELD
üö® HEPATORENAL SYNDROME
Functional renal failure in advanced cirrhosis due to splanchnic vasodilation and renal vasoconstriction
Diagnostic Criteria
  • Cirrhosis with ascites
  • AKI per KDIGO criteria (Cr increase ‚â•0.3 in 48h or ‚â•50% in 7 days)
  • No improvement after 2 days of diuretic withdrawal + albumin challenge (1 g/kg/day)
  • Absence of shock, nephrotoxic drugs, structural kidney disease
Treatment
  • Albumin + vasoconstrictor:
    • Terlipressin (if available) ‚Äî preferred
    • Norepinephrine (requires ICU)
    • Midodrine + octreotide (less effective)
  • Definitive treatment: Liver transplantation
  • TIPS: May be considered in select patients

Hepatocellular Carcinoma Surveillance

HIGH YIELD
üìò AASLD HCC Surveillance
Who to screen:
  • All patients with cirrhosis (any etiology)
  • Chronic HBV carriers (certain populations regardless of cirrhosis)
How: Ultrasound ¬± AFP every 6 months
If abnormal: Diagnostic CT or MRI with contrast
Diagnostic Imaging
  • LI-RADS criteria: Standardized reporting for CT/MRI
  • Hallmark features: Arterial phase hyperenhancement + washout + capsule
  • LI-RADS 5: Definite HCC (biopsy often not needed)

Hepatitis C

HIGH YIELD
Screening & Diagnosis
  • USPSTF recommends: One-time screening for all adults 18-79
  • Screening test: Anti-HCV antibody
  • If positive: Confirm with HCV RNA (viral load)
  • Anti-HCV+ but RNA negative: Cleared infection (spontaneous or treated)
üìò AASLD/IDSA HCV Guidelines
Treatment ‚Äî Direct-Acting Antivirals (DAAs):
  • ALL patients with chronic HCV should be treated (regardless of fibrosis stage)
  • Pangenotypic regimens: Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir, glecaprevir/pibrentasvir
  • Duration: Usually 8-12 weeks
  • Cure rates (SVR12): >95%
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Post-SVR Management
  • SVR = Cure: Undetectable HCV RNA 12 weeks after treatment completion
  • If NO cirrhosis: No further HCV-related follow-up needed
  • If cirrhosis: Continue HCC surveillance indefinitely (risk persists)
  • Reinfection possible: Counsel on risk behaviors

üíö Biliary Disease

üíä

Cholelithiasis & Cholecystitis

HIGH YIELD
Biliary Colic
  • Presentation: Episodic RUQ/epigastric pain, often postprandial, lasting 30 min to several hours
  • Diagnosis: RUQ ultrasound (gallstones + GB wall normal)
  • Treatment: Elective cholecystectomy if symptomatic
Acute Cholecystitis
  • Presentation: Persistent RUQ pain (>6 hours), fever, Murphy's sign
  • Labs: Leukocytosis; may have mild transaminase/bilirubin elevation
  • Imaging: US (stones, GB wall thickening >4mm, pericholecystic fluid, sonographic Murphy's sign)
  • HIDA scan: If US inconclusive; non-filling of GB = cystic duct obstruction
üìò Treatment
  • NPO, IV fluids, pain control, antibiotics
  • Cholecystectomy: Preferably within 72 hours (early surgery preferred)
  • Poor surgical candidate: Percutaneous cholecystostomy tube
üö® COMPLICATIONS OF CHOLECYSTITIS
  • Gangrenous cholecystitis: Necrosis of GB wall; may be painless in elderly/diabetics
  • Emphysematous cholecystitis: Gas-forming organisms; diabetics; emergent surgery
  • Perforation: Localized or free perforation

Choledocholithiasis & Cholangitis

HIGH YIELD
Choledocholithiasis (CBD Stones)
  • Presentation: RUQ pain, jaundice, elevated LFTs (cholestatic pattern)
  • Diagnosis: MRCP or EUS (non-invasive); ERCP if high probability or therapeutic intent
Acute Cholangitis
  • Charcot's triad: Fever, RUQ pain, jaundice (50-70% of cases)
  • Reynolds' pentad: + Hypotension + Altered mental status (severe/suppurative)
  • Pathogens: E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus, Bacteroides
üìò Cholangitis Management
  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum (pip-tazo, ampicillin-sulbactam, or FQ + metronidazole)
  • Biliary drainage: ERCP within 24-48 hours (emergent if severe/septic)
  • Alternatives: Percutaneous transhepatic drainage or surgical drainage if ERCP fails
üìù CHARCOT'S TRIAD
Fever + RUQ Pain + Jaundice = Cholangitis

Gallstone Pancreatitis

HIGH YIELD
Key Points

  • Suspect if: ALT >3√ó ULN, dilated CBD, visible CBD stone
  • ERCP: Indicated if cholangitis or persistent biliary obstruction
  • Cholecystectomy: During same admission for mild gallstone pancreatitis (reduces recurrence)

üü° Pancreatic Disorders

üíä

Acute Pancreatitis

HIGH YIELD
Diagnosis (2 of 3 criteria)
  • Characteristic abdominal pain (epigastric, radiating to back)
  • Lipase (or amylase) ‚â•3√ó upper limit of normal
  • Imaging findings of pancreatitis
üìù CAUSES OF ACUTE PANCREATITIS ("I GET SMASHED")
Idiopathic, Gallstones, EtOH, Trauma, Steroids, Mumps, Autoimmune, Scorpion stings, Hyperlipidemia/Hypercalcemia, ERCP, Drugs
üìò ACG Acute Pancreatitis Guidelines
Management:
  • Aggressive IV fluids: Lactated Ringer's preferred; goal-directed (watch for fluid overload)
  • Pain control
  • Nutrition: Early oral feeding when tolerated (within 24-48h if mild); enteral preferred over TPN
  • Antibiotics: NOT indicated prophylactically (even in severe/necrotic pancreatitis)
üìä SEVERITY ASSESSMENT
  • BISAP score: BUN >25, Impaired mental status, SIRS, Age >60, Pleural effusion
  • ‚â•3 points: Higher mortality risk
  • Ranson's criteria: At admission and 48h (‚â•3 = severe)
  • Atlanta classification: Mild (no organ failure), Moderate (transient OF), Severe (persistent OF)

  • Acute peripancreatic fluid collection: <4 weeks, no wall
  • Pseudocyst: >4 weeks, encapsulated, no necrosis
  • Acute necrotic collection: <4 weeks with necrosis
  • Walled-off necrosis (WON): >4 weeks, encapsulated necrosis
  • Infected necrosis: Gas on CT, clinical deterioration; may need drainage/necrosectomy

Chronic Pancreatitis

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Etiology: Alcohol (most common in US), smoking, genetic (PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR), autoimmune
  • Symptoms: Recurrent/chronic abdominal pain, steatorrhea, weight loss, diabetes
  • Exocrine insufficiency: Usually after >90% gland destruction
Diagnosis
  • CT/MRCP: Calcifications, ductal dilation, atrophy
  • EUS: Most sensitive for early changes
  • Fecal elastase: <200 Œºg/g indicates exocrine insufficiency
Management
  • Pain: Abstinence from alcohol/tobacco, analgesics, PERT, celiac plexus block, surgery
  • Exocrine insufficiency: Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) with meals
  • Endocrine insufficiency: Manage diabetes (brittle, prone to hypoglycemia)
  • Nutrition: Small frequent meals, fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)

‚ö° GI Motility Disorders

üíä

Gastroparesis

HIGH YIELD
Etiology
  • Diabetes: Most common identifiable cause
  • Idiopathic: Often post-viral
  • Post-surgical: Vagal nerve injury
  • Medications: Opioids, anticholinergics, GLP-1 agonists
Diagnosis
  • EGD: Rule out mechanical obstruction (required before diagnosis)
  • Gastric emptying scintigraphy (GES): Gold standard; >10% retention at 4 hours
  • Hold medications that affect motility before testing
üìò Treatment
  • Dietary: Small, frequent, low-fat, low-fiber meals
  • Glycemic control (hyperglycemia worsens emptying)
  • Prokinetics: Metoclopramide (FDA black box for tardive dyskinesia ‚Äî limit to 12 weeks), domperidone (not FDA approved), erythromycin
  • Anti-emetics: Ondansetron, prochlorperazine
  • Refractory: Gastric electrical stimulation, pyloromyotomy, jejunostomy tube

Chronic Constipation

CORE
Evaluation
  • Rome IV criteria for functional constipation
  • Alarm features: Hematochezia, weight loss, family history CRC, age >50 without screening
  • Refractory cases: Anorectal manometry, balloon expulsion test, colonic transit study, defecography

  1. Fiber + fluids
  2. Osmotic laxatives: PEG (MiraLAX), lactulose
  3. Stimulant laxatives: Bisacodyl, senna (not for daily long-term use)
  4. Secretagogues: Linaclotide, lubiprostone, plecanatide (prescription)
  5. Prokinetics: Prucalopride (5-HT4 agonist)
  6. Dyssynergic defecation: Biofeedback therapy (first-line)

üçé GI Nutrition

üíä

Enteral vs Parenteral Nutrition

HIGH YIELD
üìò ASPEN Guidelines
"If the gut works, use it"
  • Enteral nutrition preferred ‚Äî maintains gut integrity, reduces infections, cheaper
  • TPN indications: Non-functioning GI tract, severe malabsorption, GI obstruction, high-output fistula, short bowel syndrome (initially)
Enteral Access
  • NG tube: Short-term (<4 weeks), intact mental status/gag
  • PEG (gastrostomy): Long-term, functional stomach, aspiration risk acceptable
  • PEJ (jejunostomy): High aspiration risk, gastroparesis, gastric outlet obstruction
üö® REFEEDING SYNDROME
  • Risk: Severely malnourished patients started on nutrition
  • Mechanism: Insulin surge ‚Üí intracellular shift of phosphorus, potassium, magnesium
  • Complications: Hypophosphatemia (most important), arrhythmias, heart failure, respiratory failure
  • Prevention: Start low, advance slowly; replete electrolytes; give thiamine before feeding

Short Bowel Syndrome

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Definition: Malabsorption due to loss of small bowel (usually <200 cm remaining)
  • Causes: Surgical resection (Crohn's, mesenteric ischemia, trauma), radiation enteritis
  • Symptoms: Diarrhea, steatorrhea, dehydration, malnutrition, micronutrient deficiencies
Management
  • Initial: TPN for fluid/nutrition while gut adapts
  • Oral/enteral: Transition as tolerated; frequent small meals, complex carbs, moderate fat
  • Anti-diarrheal: Loperamide, codeine
  • Acid suppression: PPI (reduces gastric hypersecretion)
  • Teduglutide: GLP-2 analog ‚Äî promotes intestinal adaptation, reduces TPN dependence
  • Intestinal transplant: Refractory cases with TPN complications
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Ileal Resection Consequences
  • <100 cm resected: Bile acid diarrhea (watery) ‚Äî treat with cholestyramine
  • >100 cm resected: Fat malabsorption/steatorrhea (bile acid pool depleted) ‚Äî reduce fat, PERT
  • Also: B12 deficiency, gallstones (cholesterol), kidney stones (oxalate)

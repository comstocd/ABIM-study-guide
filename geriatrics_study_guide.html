Screening

‚úì Annual Falls Screening (CDC STEADI)
  1. Have you fallen in the past year?
  2. Do you feel unsteady when standing or walking?
  3. Do you worry about falling?

Fall Risk Assessment

  • Timed Up and Go (TUG): Rise from chair, walk 3m, return, sit. ‚â•12 seconds = increased risk
  • 30-Second Chair Stand: Number of stands in 30 sec. <8 = increased risk
  • 4-Stage Balance Test: Tandem, semi-tandem, side-by-side, one-leg stand
  • Orthostatic vitals
  • Vision screening
  • Medication review
  • Footwear assessment

3. Falls ‚Äî Prevention & Management

‚ñº

Evidence-Based Interventions

‚úì Proven Fall Prevention Strategies
  • Exercise: Balance and strength training (Tai Chi, physical therapy)
  • Vitamin D: 800-1000 IU daily if deficient or at risk
  • Medication review: Reduce/stop fall-risk medications (see Beers)
  • Vision correction
  • Home safety assessment: Remove hazards, improve lighting, grab bars
  • Footwear: Non-slip, low-heeled, well-fitting
  • Assistive devices: Cane, walker as appropriate

‚ö†Ô∏è Board Pearl

Multifactorial interventions are most effective for fall prevention. Single interventions (except exercise) are less effective than combined approaches.

4. Delirium ‚Äî Diagnosis

‚ñº

Definition

Acute, fluctuating disturbance in attention and awareness, with cognitive change, not explained by preexisting dementia.

Types

TypeFeaturesNotes

HypoactiveLethargy, decreased activity, withdrawalOften missed; worse prognosis

Screening Tools

üìã Confusion Assessment Method (CAM)

Delirium = 1 + 2 + (3 or 4)

  1. Acute onset and fluctuating course
  2. Inattention (difficulty focusing, easily distracted)
  3. Disorganized thinking OR
  4. Altered level of consciousness

CAM-ICU

Modified version for intubated/non-verbal patients

Delirium vs Dementia

FeatureDeliriumDementia
OnsetAcute (hours to days)Insidious (months to years)

ConsciousnessAlteredUsually normal

üö® Key Point

Dementia is a major risk factor for delirium. Delirium superimposed on dementia is common and associated with worse outcomes.

5. Delirium ‚Äî Causes & Management

‚ñº

Common Causes ‚Äî "I WATCH DEATH"

üîç Mnemonic
  • Infection (UTI, pneumonia, sepsis)
  • Withdrawal (alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids)
  • Acute metabolic (electrolytes, glucose, renal/hepatic failure)
  • Trauma (head injury, pain, surgery)
  • CNS pathology (stroke, seizure, meningitis)
  • Hypoxia
  • Deficiencies (thiamine, B12)
  • Endocrine (thyroid, adrenal)
  • Acute vascular (MI, PE, CHF)
  • Toxins/drugs (anticholinergics, opioids, benzos, steroids)
  • Heavy metals (rarely)

High-Risk Medications for Delirium

  • Anticholinergics: Diphenhydramine, oxybutynin, TCAs, antipsychotics
  • Benzodiazepines
  • Opioids (especially meperidine)
  • Corticosteroids
  • H2 blockers (cimetidine, ranitidine)
  • Fluoroquinolones

Workup

  • Complete metabolic panel, glucose
  • CBC
  • Urinalysis, urine culture
  • Chest X-ray
  • Blood cultures if febrile
  • Medication review
  • Consider: TSH, B12, ammonia, drug levels, CT head, LP if indicated

Non-Pharmacologic Management (FIRST LINE)

‚úì HELP Protocol / Multicomponent Interventions
  • Reorientation: Clocks, calendars, familiar objects, family presence
  • Sleep promotion: Reduce nighttime interruptions, daytime activity
  • Sensory optimization: Glasses, hearing aids
  • Early mobilization
  • Hydration and nutrition
  • Avoid physical restraints (worsen agitation)
  • Minimize lines, catheters
  • Treat pain

Pharmacologic Management

üö´ Medications Are Second-Line

Use only when patient is a danger to self or others and non-pharmacologic measures have failed.

  • Haloperidol: 0.5-1mg PO/IM; avoid if QTc prolonged, Parkinson's, DLB
  • Atypical antipsychotics: Quetiapine 12.5-25mg, risperidone 0.25-0.5mg
  • Avoid benzodiazepines except for alcohol/benzo withdrawal or seizures

Prevention

Up to 40% of delirium is preventable with multicomponent interventions (HELP program)

Reversible Causes ‚Äî Rule Out

‚ö†Ô∏è "DEMENTIA" Mnemonic

  • Emotional (depression = pseudodementia)
  • Metabolic (thyroid, B12, folate, electrolytes)
  • Eyes/Ears (sensory impairment)
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus
  • Tumor/Trauma/Toxins
  • Infection (syphilis, HIV, prion)
  • Alcohol

Workup

  • CBC, CMP, TSH, B12, folate
  • Consider: HIV, syphilis, heavy metals if indicated
  • Brain imaging (CT or MRI) ‚Äî at least once to rule out structural causes
  • Depression screening

7. Dementia ‚Äî Management

‚ñº

Pharmacologic Treatment ‚Äî Cognitive Symptoms

ClassAgentsIndicationSide Effects
Cholinesterase Inhibitors Donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine Mild-moderate Alzheimer's, DLB Nausea, diarrhea, bradycardia
NMDA Antagonist Memantine Moderate-severe Alzheimer's Dizziness, confusion, headache
‚ö†Ô∏è Realistic Expectations

Medications provide modest symptomatic benefit at best. They do not cure or significantly slow disease progression. Benefits typically last 6-12 months.

Behavioral & Psychological Symptoms (BPSD)

  • Agitation, aggression
  • Psychosis (hallucinations, delusions)
  • Depression, anxiety
  • Wandering, sleep disturbance

Non-Pharmacologic Approaches (FIRST LINE)

‚úì Behavioral Interventions
  • Identify and address triggers (pain, infection, constipation)
  • Structured routine
  • Music therapy, aromatherapy
  • Redirection, validation
  • Caregiver education
  • Safe environment

Pharmacologic ‚Äî Use Sparingly

üö´ Black Box Warning

Antipsychotics increase mortality in elderly with dementia (1.6-1.7√ó risk). Use only for severe symptoms when non-pharmacologic approaches fail.

  • Depression: SSRIs (sertraline, citalopram) ‚Äî avoid TCAs
  • Agitation: Attempt non-pharmacologic first; low-dose antipsychotic if danger to self/others
  • Sleep: Sleep hygiene; melatonin; avoid benzodiazepines

Driving

  • Mild dementia does not automatically preclude driving
  • Assess individually; consider driving evaluation
  • Document discussions and recommendations
  • Know state reporting requirements

Caregiver Support

  • Assess caregiver burnout
  • Respite care
  • Support groups (Alzheimer's Association)
  • Community resources

üö´ Potentially Inappropriate Medications in Elderly

Avoid in Most Older Adults

Drug ClassExamplesConcern

Anticholinergic Burden

High anticholinergic burden = cognitive impairment, falls, delirium

Common anticholinergic medications:

  • Diphenhydramine
  • Oxybutynin, tolterodine
  • TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
  • First-generation antipsychotics
  • Paroxetine (more than other SSRIs)

Deprescribing

‚úì Deprescribing Approach
  1. Review all medications (including OTC, supplements)
  2. Identify potentially inappropriate medications
  3. Assess each medication: Is there still an indication?
  4. Prioritize which to stop based on harm/benefit
  5. Taper when appropriate (don't abruptly stop)
  6. Monitor for withdrawal or recurrence

9. Pressure Injuries

‚ñº

Staging

StageDescription
Stage 1Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin
Stage 2Partial-thickness loss (dermis); blister or shallow ulcer
Stage 3Full-thickness loss; subcutaneous fat visible; no bone/tendon
Stage 4Full-thickness with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle
UnstageableObscured by slough or eschar
Deep Tissue InjuryPurple/maroon discoloration; suspected deep tissue damage

Risk Factors

  • Immobility
  • Malnutrition
  • Incontinence
  • Decreased sensation
  • Vascular disease

Prevention

  • Reposition every 2 hours
  • Pressure-redistributing surfaces
  • Keep skin clean and dry
  • Optimize nutrition
  • Use Braden Scale for risk assessment

Treatment

  • Offload pressure
  • Debride necrotic tissue (except stable heel eschar)
  • Keep wound moist (hydrocolloid, foam, alginate dressings)
  • Treat infection if present
  • Optimize nutrition (protein, vitamin C, zinc)
  • Consider negative pressure wound therapy for deep wounds

‚ñº

Types

TypeMechanismFeatures
Stress Weak pelvic floor/sphincter Leakage with cough, sneeze, exertion; more common in women

  • Infection (UTI)
  • Atrophic vaginitis
  • Pharmaceuticals (diuretics, anticholinergics, sedatives)
  • Psychological (depression)
  • Excessive urine output (hyperglycemia, CHF, hypercalcemia)
  • Restricted mobility
  • Stool impaction

üö´ Avoid in Elderly

Anticholinergic medications (oxybutynin, tolterodine) ‚Äî high risk of confusion, falls. If needed, use lower doses or consider mirabegron (beta-3 agonist).

11. Goals of Care & Advance Care Planning

‚ñº

Advance Care Planning Components

  • Advance directive: Written document of healthcare preferences
  • Healthcare proxy/POA: Designated decision-maker
  • POLST/MOLST: Actionable physician orders for seriously ill
  • Goals of care discussion: Ongoing conversation about values and preferences

Prognostic Indicators for Life-Limiting Illness

  • Multiple hospitalizations in past year
  • Functional decline
  • Unintentional weight loss >10%
  • "Would you be surprised if this patient died in the next year?"
  • Increasing symptom burden

Goals of Care Conversation Framework

üìã SPIKES Protocol (for serious news)
  1. Setting ‚Äî private, uninterrupted
  2. Perception ‚Äî assess what patient/family understands
  3. Invitation ‚Äî ask how much they want to know
  4. Knowledge ‚Äî share information clearly
  5. Empathy ‚Äî acknowledge emotions
  6. Strategy ‚Äî discuss plan, offer support

Key Questions to Ask

  • "What is your understanding of your illness?"
  • "What matters most to you?"
  • "What are you hoping for?"
  • "What are you worried about?"
  • "If you became sicker, what would be most important to you?"

SymptomManagement

‚úì Key Principle

Morphine for dyspnea is appropriate and does NOT hasten death when used properly. Opioids reduce sensation of breathlessness.

13. Elder Abuse

‚ñº

Types

  • Physical: Bruises, fractures, burns, restraint marks
  • Emotional/Psychological: Humiliation, threats, isolation
  • Sexual: Non-consensual contact
  • Financial: Exploitation, theft, coercion
  • Neglect: Failure to provide care, nutrition, hygiene
  • Self-neglect: Patient fails to care for self

Risk Factors

  • Cognitive impairment
  • Functional dependence
  • Social isolation
  • Caregiver stress/burnout
  • Caregiver substance abuse or mental illness
  • Shared living situation

Red Flags on Exam

  • Unexplained injuries
  • Injuries in various stages of healing
  • Delay in seeking care
  • Inconsistent history
  • Poor hygiene, malnutrition
  • Fearful behavior around caregiver
  • Caregiver speaks for patient

Management

üö® Mandatory Reporting

Most states require mandatory reporting of suspected elder abuse to Adult Protective Services (APS). Document carefully and report even if not certain.

  • Interview patient alone
  • Document injuries with photographs
  • Assess safety ‚Äî is patient safe to go home?
  • Report to APS
  • Involve social work

14. Nutrition & Malnutrition

‚ñº

Screening

  • MNA (Mini Nutritional Assessment): Validated for older adults
  • MUST (Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool)
  • Weight loss >5% in 1 month or >10% in 6 months = significant

Causes of Malnutrition ‚Äî "MEALS ON WHEELS"

  • Medications
  • Emotional (depression)
  • Anorexia (late-life)
  • Late-life paranoia
  • Swallowing problems
  • Oral/dental problems
  • No money (poverty)
  • Wandering (dementia)
  • Hyperthyroidism/disease
  • Entry problems (malabsorption)
  • Eating problems (inability to self-feed)
  • Low-salt, low-cholesterol diets
  • Shopping/food preparation difficulties

Management

  • Treat underlying cause
  • Liberalize diet (avoid overly restrictive diets in elderly)
  • Fortify foods
  • Oral nutritional supplements
  • Address dental issues
  • Social eating, pleasant environment
‚ö†Ô∏è Tube Feeding in Advanced Dementia

Tube feeding does NOT improve survival or quality of life in advanced dementia. Associated with aspiration, infections, restraints. Careful hand-feeding is preferred.

16. High-Yield Board Pearls

‚ñº

Geriatric Assessment Pearls

  • üíé Functional status (ADLs/IADLs) is the best predictor of outcomes in elderly
  • üíé Frailty = weight loss, exhaustion, weakness, slow gait, low activity (‚â•3 of 5)
  • üíé Stop cancer screening when life expectancy <10 years

Falls Pearls

  • üíé Exercise (balance + strength) is the most effective fall prevention intervention
  • üíé Vitamin D 800-1000 IU reduces falls in deficient/at-risk patients
  • üíé Timed Up and Go ‚â•12 seconds = increased fall risk
  • üíé Multifactorial interventions are most effective

Delirium Pearls

  • üíé CAM criteria: Acute + fluctuating + inattention + (disorganized thinking OR altered consciousness)
  • üíé Hypoactive delirium is often missed and has worse prognosis
  • üíé Non-pharmacologic interventions are FIRST-LINE
  • üíé Avoid benzodiazepines except for alcohol/benzo withdrawal
  • üíé Dementia is the #1 risk factor for delirium

Dementia Pearls

  • üíé Rule out reversible causes: B12, TSH, depression, medications
  • üíé DLB: Visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, fluctuations, RBD, sensitive to antipsychotics
  • üíé NPH: "Wet, wacky, wobbly" ‚Äî gait first, then dementia, then incontinence
  • üíé Antipsychotics increase mortality in dementia patients (black box warning)

Polypharmacy Pearls

  • üíé Beers Criteria: Avoid benzos, anticholinergics, long-acting sulfonylureas, chronic NSAIDs, PPIs without indication
  • üíé Diphenhydramine is strongly anticholinergic ‚Äî avoid in elderly
  • üíé Deprescribe when indication no longer exists or harm outweighs benefit

End-of-Life Pearls

  • üíé Morphine for dyspnea is appropriate and effective in terminal illness
  • üíé Tube feeding in advanced dementia does NOT improve outcomes
  • üíé Hospice eligibility: Prognosis <6 months if disease runs its usual course
  • üíé Palliative care ‚â† hospice ‚Äî palliative care can be concurrent with curative treatment

Other Pearls

  • üíé Elder abuse: Mandatory reporting to Adult Protective Services in most states
  • üíé Stress incontinence: Pelvic floor exercises first
  • üíé Pressure injury prevention: Reposition every 2 hours
  • üíé Hearing loss is associated with cognitive decline

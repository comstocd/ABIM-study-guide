1. Stroke‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Acute Ischemic Stroke
Time Windows

IV alteplase is indicated within 4.5 hours of symptom onset or last known well time.

Exclusions for the 3 to 4.5 hour window include age over 80, severe stroke with NIHSS greater than 25, diabetes with prior stroke, and oral anticoagulant use.

Blood pressure must be below 185/110 before and below 180/105 for 24 hours after thrombolysis.

Thrombectomy can extend the window up to 24 hours in selected patients with salvageable penumbra on perfusion imaging.

Contraindications to tPA

Absolute contraindications include recent head trauma or stroke within 3 months, intracranial hemorrhage, intracranial neoplasm, and active bleeding.

Blood glucose must be above 50. Platelets must be above 100,000. INR must be below 1.7.

Secondary Prevention
Antiplatelet Therapy

Aspirin 160 to 325 milligrams should be started within 24 to 48 hours.

Dual antiplatelet therapy with aspirin plus clopidogrel for 21 days is indicated for minor stroke or high-risk TIA based on the POINT and CHANCE trials.

Long-term therapy is aspirin or clopidogrel monotherapy.

Atrial Fibrillation

Stroke from atrial fibrillation requires anticoagulation, not antiplatelet therapy.

DOACs are preferred over warfarin for non-valvular AF.

Timing of anticoagulation after stroke depends on infarct size. Small strokes can start at 1 to 3 days, large strokes at 7 to 14 days.

Hemorrhagic Stroke
Management

Intracerebral hemorrhage requires blood pressure control. Target systolic 140 to 160 for most patients.

Reverse anticoagulation immediately. 4-factor PCC for warfarin, idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet for Xa inhibitors.

Neurosurgical consultation for large hemorrhages or cerebellar hemorrhage greater than 3 centimeters.

2. Seizures & Epilepsy‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Status Epilepticus
Management Algorithm

Status epilepticus is seizure lasting more than 5 minutes or recurrent seizures without return to baseline.

First-line treatment is IV benzodiazepine: lorazepam 4 milligrams or diazepam 10 milligrams or midazolam IM if no IV access.

Second-line is IV antiepileptic: fosphenytoin, valproate, or levetiracetam.

Refractory status requires continuous infusion of midazolam, propofol, or pentobarbital with EEG monitoring.

Epilepsy Treatment
Drug Selection

For focal seizures, options include levetiracetam, lamotrigine, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, and lacosamide.

For generalized seizures, options include valproate, levetiracetam, and lamotrigine.

Avoid carbamazepine and phenytoin in generalized epilepsy as they can worsen absence and myoclonic seizures.

Lamotrigine and levetiracetam are preferred in women of childbearing age. Valproate is teratogenic.

3. Headache‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Migraine
Treatment

Acute treatment includes NSAIDs, acetaminophen, triptans, and antiemetics. Triptans are contraindicated in coronary artery disease.

Prophylaxis is indicated for frequent migraines. Options include beta-blockers, topiramate, valproate, and amitriptyline.

CGRP monoclonal antibodies like erenumab and fremanezumab are newer prophylactic options for refractory cases.

Secondary Headaches
Red Flags

Thunderclap headache with sudden severe onset requires evaluation for subarachnoid hemorrhage. CT head then LP if CT negative.

New headache over age 50 raises concern for giant cell arteritis or mass lesion.

Headache with fever and neck stiffness suggests meningitis. Papilledema suggests elevated intracranial pressure.

Headache worse with Valsalva or positional changes may indicate mass lesion or CSF leak.

4. Movement Disorders‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Parkinson Disease
Diagnosis and Treatment

PD presents with bradykinesia plus resting tremor or rigidity. Asymmetric onset is typical.

Carbidopa-levodopa is the most effective treatment for motor symptoms.

Dopamine agonists like pramipexole and ropinirole are alternatives, especially in younger patients to delay levodopa.

MAO-B inhibitors selegiline and rasagiline can be used as monotherapy or adjuncts.

Deep brain stimulation is considered for motor fluctuations refractory to medication optimization.

Essential Tremor
Key Features

Essential tremor is an action tremor, present with movement and posture, not at rest.

Often bilateral and symmetric, affecting hands, head, and voice.

Improves with alcohol, which is a helpful diagnostic clue.

Treatment is propranolol or primidone.

5. Dementia‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Dementia Types
Key Differences

Alzheimer disease is the most common dementia. Gradual onset with prominent memory impairment early. Hippocampal atrophy on MRI.

Vascular dementia has stepwise progression often with focal neurologic signs. Risk factors are stroke and cardiovascular disease.

Lewy body dementia has visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, and fluctuating cognition. Avoid antipsychotics due to severe sensitivity.

Frontotemporal dementia presents with personality changes and behavioral disinhibition. Onset typically before age 65.

Treatment
Medications

Cholinesterase inhibitors donepezil, rivastigmine, and galantamine are used for mild to moderate Alzheimer disease.

Memantine, an NMDA antagonist, is added for moderate to severe disease.

These medications provide modest symptomatic benefit but do not halt disease progression.

6. Neuromuscular Disorders‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Myasthenia Gravis
Key Points

MG presents with fatigable weakness, worse with use and better with rest. Ptosis, diplopia, and bulbar symptoms are common.

Anti-AChR antibodies are positive in 85 percent. Anti-MuSK antibodies in some seronegative cases.

Ice pack test: ptosis improves with cooling.

Treatment is pyridostigmine for symptomatic relief. Immunosuppression with steroids and steroid-sparing agents for most patients.

Thymectomy is indicated for thymoma and considered for generalized MG even without thymoma.

Myasthenic Crisis

Myasthenic crisis is respiratory failure from MG. Check FVC every 4 hours. Intubate if FVC falls below 15 to 20 mL per kg.

Treatment is IVIG or plasmapheresis.

Avoid aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, and beta-blockers as they can worsen MG.

Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome
Key Points

GBS is acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy presenting with ascending weakness and areflexia.

Often follows infection, classically Campylobacter jejuni.

CSF shows albuminocytologic dissociation: elevated protein with normal cell count.

Treatment is IVIG or plasmapheresis. Steroids are not effective.

Monitor for respiratory failure. FVC less than 20 mL per kg or declining trend warrants ICU monitoring.

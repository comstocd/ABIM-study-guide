Acute Management

IV Alteplase (tPA)

  • Window: Within 4.5 hours of symptom onset
  • Dose: 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg); 10% bolus, 90% over 60 min
  • BP goal: <185/110 before, <180/105 after
tPA Contraindications: Recent surgery/trauma (14 days), prior ICH, BP >185/110 uncontrolled, platelets <100K, INR >1.7, glucose <50 or >400, large infarct (>1/3 MCA territory)

Mechanical Thrombectomy

  • Window: Up to 24 hours with favorable imaging (DAWN/DEFUSE-3)
  • Indication: Large vessel occlusion (ICA, M1, basilar)
  • Requirements: NIHSS ‚â•6, mismatch on perfusion imaging
Thrombectomy window extended to 24 hours if CT perfusion shows salvageable tissue (core-penumbra mismatch). Don't exclude based on time alone!

3. Subarachnoid Hemorrhage Emergency

Etiology

  • Aneurysmal (85%): Berry aneurysm rupture; locations: ACA/AComm (30%), ICA/PComm (25%), MCA (20%)
  • Non-aneurysmal: Perimesencephalic, trauma, AVM

Clinical Features

  • "Thunderclap headache": Worst headache of life, maximal at onset
  • Nausea, vomiting, photophobia
  • Meningismus
  • Altered consciousness, focal deficits (depending on location)
  • "Sentinel headache": Warning leak days before major rupture

Diagnosis

  • Non-contrast CT: >95% sensitive within 6 hours (decreases over time)
  • Lumbar puncture: If CT negative and high suspicion
    • Xanthochromia (yellow CSF from bilirubin)
    • Elevated RBCs that don't clear
  • CTA or DSA: To identify aneurysm

Complications

ComplicationTimingPrevention/Treatment
RebleedingFirst 24 hoursEarly aneurysm repair (clip or coil)
VasospasmDays 3-14 (peak day 7)Nimodipine, "Triple-H" therapy, angioplasty
HydrocephalusAcute or delayedEVD, VP shunt
HyponatremiaDays 2-10SIADH or cerebral salt wasting
SeizuresAny timeProphylaxis controversial (short-term)
Nimodipine 60mg Q4h √ó 21 days: Improves outcomes after SAH (reduces vasospasm-related infarcts). Give to ALL aneurysmal SAH patients.

5. Seizures & Epilepsy Common

Classification

TypeFeatures
Focal (partial)Begins in one hemisphere; aware or impaired awareness
GeneralizedInvolves both hemispheres from onset; tonic-clonic, absence, myoclonic, atonic
Focal to bilateral tonic-clonicStarts focal, then generalizes

Epilepsy Definition

  • ‚â•2 unprovoked seizures >24 hours apart, OR
  • 1 seizure + high recurrence risk (>60%), OR
  • Diagnosis of epilepsy syndrome

Provoked Seizure Causes

VITAMINS: Vascular (stroke), Infection, Trauma, Autoimmune, Metabolic (Na, glucose, uremia), Idiopathic/Ingestion (drugs, alcohol withdrawal), Neoplasm, Sleep deprivation

Workup

  • Labs: BMP (Na, glucose, Ca), tox screen
  • EEG: Interictal abnormalities, classify seizure type
  • MRI brain: Structural lesions
  • LP: If infection suspected

Antiseizure Medications

DrugUseKey Toxicities
LevetiracetamBroad spectrum, first-lineMood changes, irritability
LamotrigineFocal, generalized, pregnancy-safeSJS/TEN (slow titration)
ValproateGeneralized (esp. JME)Teratogenic, hepatotoxicity, weight gain, tremor
CarbamazepineFocal seizuresHyponatremia, aplastic anemia, SJS (HLA-B*1502)
PhenytoinFocal, status epilepticusGingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, osteoporosis, zero-order kinetics

Valproate is teratogenic (neural tube defects). Avoid in women of childbearing potential. Lamotrigine and levetiracetam are safer in pregnancy.

When NOT to Treat After First Seizure

  • Provoked seizure with reversible cause
  • Normal EEG and MRI
  • Low recurrence risk

6. Status Epilepticus Emergency

Definition

  • Seizure lasting >5 minutes, OR
  • ‚â•2 seizures without return to baseline

Treatment Protocol

TimeTreatment
0-5 minABCs, O2, IV access, glucose, thiamine if indicated
5-20 min (emergent)Benzodiazepine: Lorazepam 4mg IV (repeat √ó 1) OR Midazolam 10mg IM
20-40 min (urgent)2nd line AED: Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg, Valproate 40 mg/kg, OR Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg
>40 min (refractory)Anesthetic: Propofol, midazolam, or pentobarbital drip + intubation + continuous EEG
Lorazepam IV is first-line for status epilepticus. If no IV access, give midazolam IM (RAMPART trial showed non-inferiority).

7. Primary Headache Disorders Very Common

Migraine

Diagnostic Criteria

  • ‚â•5 attacks lasting 4-72 hours
  • At least 2 of: Unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, aggravated by activity
  • At least 1 of: Nausea/vomiting, photophobia + phonophobia
  • Aura: Visual (scintillating scotoma), sensory, or speech; develops over 5-60 min

Treatment

AcutePreventive (if ‚â•4 headache days/month)
NSAIDs, acetaminophenPropranolol, topiramate, valproate
Triptans (5-HT1B/1D agonists)Amitriptyline
Gepants (CGRP antagonists)CGRP mAbs (erenumab, fremanezumab)
Antiemetics (metoclopramide)Botox (chronic migraine)
Triptans contraindicated in: CAD, uncontrolled HTN, prior stroke, hemiplegic migraine, basilar migraine. Avoid within 24h of ergots.

Tension-Type Headache

  • Bilateral, pressing/tightening, mild-moderate
  • No nausea, no more than one of photo/phonophobia
  • Treatment: NSAIDs, acetaminophen; preventive with amitriptyline

Cluster Headache

  • Unilateral severe orbital/temporal pain, 15-180 minutes
  • Autonomic features: Lacrimation, rhinorrhea, ptosis, miosis, eyelid edema
  • Occurs in "clusters" (weeks-months) with remission periods
  • Acute treatment: High-flow O2 (100%), sumatriptan SC
  • Preventive: Verapamil, lithium, prednisone bridge
Cluster headache: Male predominance, circadian pattern (often same time nightly), patient paces/agitates (vs migraine patients prefer stillness).

10. Parkinson's Disease Common

Cardinal Features

TRAP: Tremor (rest), Rigidity (cogwheel), Akinesia/bradykinesia, Postural instability

Clinical Features

  • Tremor: "Pill-rolling," 4-6 Hz, worse at rest, improves with action
  • Rigidity: Cogwheel (ratchety), lead-pipe
  • Bradykinesia: Slow movements, hypomimia, micrographia, shuffling gait
  • Postural instability: Late finding, falls
  • Non-motor: REM sleep behavior disorder, anosmia, constipation, depression

Pathophysiology

  • Loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra pars compacta
  • Lewy bodies (Œ±-synuclein aggregates)

Treatment

Drug ClassExamplesNotes
Levodopa/carbidopaSinemetMost effective; motor fluctuations with long-term use
Dopamine agonistsPramipexole, ropiniroleYounger patients; impulse control disorders
MAO-B inhibitorsSelegiline, rasagilineMild effect; early disease or adjunct
COMT inhibitorsEntacaponeExtend levodopa effect
AnticholinergicsTrihexyphenidylTremor; avoid in elderly (confusion)
Amantadine‚ÄîDyskinesias

Motor Complications

  • Wearing off: End-of-dose deterioration
  • On-off fluctuations: Unpredictable motor state changes
  • Dyskinesias: Involuntary movements at peak dose
REM sleep behavior disorder (acting out dreams) often precedes motor symptoms by years. Consider early PD/synucleinopathy.

14. Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome Emergency

Clinical Features

  • Acute ascending symmetric weakness
  • Areflexia or hyporeflexia
  • Often preceded by infection (Campylobacter, CMV, EBV, influenza, COVID)
  • Nadir by 4 weeks (usually 2-3 weeks)
  • Respiratory failure: Monitor FVC (intubate if <20 mL/kg or declining)
  • Autonomic: BP fluctuations, arrhythmias

Variants

VariantFeatures
AIDPClassic demyelinating form (most common)
AMANAxonal, motor, associated with Campylobacter
Miller FisherAtaxia, ophthalmoplegia, areflexia; anti-GQ1b antibodies

Diagnosis

  • CSF: Albuminocytologic dissociation (high protein, normal WBC)
  • NCS: Demyelinating features (prolonged distal latency, conduction block)

Treatment

  • IVIG OR Plasmapheresis (equally effective)
  • Corticosteroids NOT effective in GBS
  • ICU for respiratory and autonomic monitoring
  • VTE prophylaxis, PT/OT
Monitor FVC every 4-6 hours. Intubate if FVC <20 mL/kg, NIF <-30 cmH2O, or >30% decline. Don't wait for hypoxia.

15. Myasthenia Gravis High-Yield

Pathophysiology

  • Autoantibodies against acetylcholine receptors (AChR) at neuromuscular junction
  • Associated with thymic abnormalities (thymoma 10-15%, hyperplasia 65%)

Clinical Features

  • Fatigable weakness: Worsens with use, improves with rest
  • Ocular: Ptosis, diplopia (most common initial symptoms)
  • Bulbar: Dysarthria, dysphagia, facial weakness
  • Limb: Proximal > distal
  • Diurnal variation: Worse later in day

Diagnosis

  • AChR antibodies: Positive in 85% generalized, 50% ocular
  • Anti-MuSK antibodies: In seronegative patients (more bulbar/respiratory)
  • Ice pack test: Improves ptosis (cooling improves NMJ function)
  • Repetitive nerve stimulation: Decremental response >10%
  • Single-fiber EMG: Most sensitive
  • CT chest: Evaluate for thymoma

Treatment

CategoryTreatment
SymptomaticPyridostigmine (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor)
ImmunotherapyPrednisone, azathioprine, mycophenolate
ThymectomyThymoma or AChR-positive generalized MG
Rapid actionIVIG, plasmapheresis
NewerEculizumab, efgartigimod (FcRn inhibitor)

Myasthenic Crisis

  • Respiratory failure from weakness
  • Triggers: Infection, surgery, medication changes, drugs
  • Treatment: ICU, intubation if needed, IVIG or PLEX
Drugs that worsen MG: Aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers, magnesium, neuromuscular blockers, penicillamine.

18. Viral Encephalitis Emergency

HSV Encephalitis

  • Most common sporadic viral encephalitis
  • Features: Fever, altered mental status, seizures, focal deficits
  • Temporal lobe predilection: Personality changes, aphasia, olfactory hallucinations
  • MRI: T2/FLAIR hyperintensity in temporal lobes (often hemorrhagic)
  • CSF: Lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, RBCs, HSV PCR positive

Treatment

  • Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h √ó 14-21 days
  • Start empirically if HSV encephalitis suspected ‚Äî don't wait for PCR
HSV encephalitis is fatal without treatment. Start acyclovir empirically in any patient with encephalitis and temporal lobe findings.

45. High-Yield Board Pearls

Stroke

  • üíé tPA window 4.5 hours; thrombectomy up to 24h with favorable imaging
  • üíé Dual antiplatelet (ASA + clopidogrel) √ó 21 days after minor stroke/TIA
  • üíé Normal or rising PaCO2 in acute asthma = impending respiratory failure
  • üíé Nimodipine for ALL SAH patients √ó 21 days (vasospasm prevention)

Seizures

  • üíé Lorazepam IV first-line for status epilepticus
  • üíé Valproate teratogenic ‚Äî avoid in pregnancy
  • üíé Carbamazepine: Check HLA-B*1502 (SJS risk in Asians)

Headache

  • üíé Thunderclap headache = SAH until proven otherwise
  • üíé Cluster headache: High-flow O2 + sumatriptan SC; verapamil for prevention
  • üíé New headache >50 years = consider temporal arteritis

Multiple Sclerosis

  • üíé INO (impaired adduction with nystagmus in abducting eye) = MLF lesion, classic for MS
  • üíé Oligoclonal bands in CSF in >90% of MS
  • üíé Natalizumab: Check JCV antibody (PML risk)

Movement Disorders

  • üíé PD tremor: Rest, 4-6 Hz, asymmetric, pill-rolling
  • üíé Essential tremor: Action, higher frequency, bilateral, improves with alcohol
  • üíé Early falls + vertical gaze palsy = PSP

Neuromuscular

  • üíé GBS: Ascending weakness + areflexia + albuminocytologic dissociation; steroids DON'T work
  • üíé Myasthenia: Fatigable weakness, AChR antibodies, thymoma association
  • üíé Aminoglycosides and fluoroquinolones worsen MG

Dementia

  • üíé NPH triad: Wet, wobbly, wacky (incontinence, gait, dementia) ‚Äî treatable!
  • üíé DLB: Visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, fluctuations; sensitive to antipsychotics

CNS Infections

  • üíé Don't delay antibiotics for LP in suspected meningitis
  • üíé Dexamethasone before/with antibiotics in bacterial meningitis
  • üíé HSV encephalitis: Temporal lobe, start acyclovir empirically

1. Diabetes Mellitus‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Diagnosis
Diagnostic Criteria

Diabetes is diagnosed by fasting glucose 126 or higher, 2-hour glucose 200 or higher during OGTT, HbA1c 6.5 percent or higher, or random glucose 200 or higher with symptoms.

Two abnormal tests are required for diagnosis unless random glucose with symptoms.

Prediabetes is fasting glucose 100 to 125, HbA1c 5.7 to 6.4, or 2-hour glucose 140 to 199.

Treatment Goals
Glycemic Targets

General A1c goal is less than 7 percent for most adults.

More stringent goal of less than 6.5 is appropriate for younger patients without hypoglycemia risk.

Less stringent goal of less than 8 is appropriate for elderly, limited life expectancy, or hypoglycemia-prone patients.

Medication Selection

Metformin remains first-line unless contraindicated.

For patients with ASCVD, heart failure, or CKD, add SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 receptor agonist regardless of A1c.

SGLT2 inhibitors reduce heart failure hospitalizations and CKD progression. Examples are empagliflozin and dapagliflozin.

GLP-1 agonists reduce cardiovascular events and promote weight loss. Examples are semaglutide, liraglutide, and dulaglutide.

2. DKA & HHS‚ñ∂Ô∏è
DKA Management
Key Points

DKA is defined by glucose over 250, pH less than 7.3 or bicarb less than 18, and positive ketones with anion gap acidosis.

Treatment priorities are fluids first, then insulin, then potassium.

Start with IV normal saline 1 to 1.5 liters per hour initially, then adjust based on hemodynamics.

Hold insulin if potassium is less than 3.3. Replete potassium first.

Continue IV insulin until anion gap closes, not just until glucose normalizes. Add dextrose to fluids when glucose falls below 200.

Overlap subcutaneous insulin with IV insulin by 1 to 2 hours before stopping the drip.

HHS
Distinguishing Features

HHS presents with glucose over 600, osmolality over 320, minimal ketones, and pH above 7.3.

Patients are profoundly dehydrated, often with 8 to 10 liter deficits.

Fluid resuscitation is even more critical in HHS than DKA. Start with 1 to 2 liters per hour initially.

3. Thyroid Disorders‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Hypothyroidism
Diagnosis and Treatment

Primary hypothyroidism shows elevated TSH with low free T4. Most common cause is Hashimoto's thyroiditis.

Treat with levothyroxine. Full replacement dose is approximately 1.6 micrograms per kilogram.

In elderly and cardiac patients, start with lower doses and titrate slowly.

Goal TSH is generally 0.5 to 2.5 for most patients.

Hyperthyroidism
Causes and Workup

Graves' disease is the most common cause. It presents with diffuse goiter and positive TSI antibodies.

Toxic multinodular goiter and toxic adenoma show increased uptake on thyroid scan.

Thyroiditis and exogenous thyroid hormone show low or absent uptake on scan.

Treatment options for Graves' include antithyroid drugs methimazole or PTU, radioactive iodine, or surgery.

PTU is preferred in first trimester pregnancy due to methimazole teratogenicity.

Thyroid Storm
Emergency Management

Thyroid storm is a life-threatening complication of hyperthyroidism with fever, tachycardia, altered mental status, and multiorgan dysfunction.

Treatment requires PTU to block synthesis, iodine given 1 hour after PTU to block release, beta-blocker for rate control, and steroids.

Give iodine only after PTU to prevent using iodine as substrate for more hormone synthesis.

4. Adrenal Disorders‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Adrenal Insufficiency
Diagnosis

Primary adrenal insufficiency, or Addison's disease, presents with fatigue, hypotension, hyperpigmentation, hyponatremia, and hyperkalemia.

Secondary adrenal insufficiency from pituitary disease lacks hyperpigmentation and hyperkalemia.

Morning cortisol less than 3 confirms diagnosis. Cortisol above 18 excludes it. Intermediate values need ACTH stimulation test.

Cosyntropin stimulation test: give 250 micrograms IV, measure cortisol at 30 and 60 minutes. Peak cortisol above 18 is normal.

Treatment

Replacement is hydrocortisone 15 to 25 milligrams daily in divided doses.

Primary AI also requires fludrocortisone for mineralocorticoid replacement.

Stress dosing: double or triple dose for illness. Give IV hydrocortisone 100 milligrams for surgery or adrenal crisis.

Cushing Syndrome
Diagnosis

Clinical features include central obesity, moon facies, buffalo hump, striae, thin skin, proximal weakness, and hypertension.

Screening tests: 24-hour urine free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, or 1 milligram overnight dexamethasone suppression test.

If screening positive, measure ACTH. ACTH-dependent causes are pituitary adenoma or ectopic ACTH. ACTH-independent causes are adrenal tumors.

5. Pituitary & Calcium‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Hypercalcemia
Causes and Management

Most common causes are primary hyperparathyroidism and malignancy.

PTH will be elevated or inappropriately normal in hyperparathyroidism, and suppressed in malignancy.

Treatment of acute symptomatic hypercalcemia: IV saline for volume expansion, then loop diuretics. Calcitonin for rapid but short-lived effect. Bisphosphonates for sustained effect but take 2 to 4 days.

Denosumab is an alternative to bisphosphonates, especially in renal impairment.

Pituitary Adenomas
Management

Prolactinoma is the most common pituitary tumor. Treat medically with dopamine agonists cabergoline or bromocriptine.

Growth hormone-secreting adenomas cause acromegaly. Surgery is first-line.

Nonfunctioning adenomas are monitored if small. Surgery for visual field defects or significant mass effect.

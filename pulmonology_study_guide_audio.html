1. COPD‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Diagnosis and Classification
GOLD Guidelines

COPD is diagnosed by spirometry showing FEV1 to FVC ratio less than 0.70 after bronchodilator.

Severity is classified by FEV1 percent predicted. GOLD 1 is mild with FEV1 80 percent or higher. GOLD 2 is moderate with FEV1 50 to 79 percent.

GOLD 3 is severe with FEV1 30 to 49 percent. GOLD 4 is very severe with FEV1 less than 30 percent.

ABCD assessment combines spirometry with symptoms using CAT or mMRC score and exacerbation history.

Stable COPD Treatment
Pharmacotherapy

For Group A with few symptoms and low exacerbation risk, use a bronchodilator as needed.

For Group B with more symptoms, start a long-acting bronchodilator. Either LAMA or LABA.

For Group E with high exacerbation risk, start LAMA plus LABA. Add inhaled corticosteroid if eosinophils are 300 or higher.

Triple therapy with LAMA plus LABA plus ICS reduces exacerbations in patients with elevated eosinophils.

Board Pearl

Blood eosinophil count predicts ICS response. Eosinophils 300 or higher favor adding ICS. Eosinophils under 100 suggest ICS is unlikely to help.

Roflumilast is a PDE4 inhibitor added for severe COPD with chronic bronchitis phenotype and frequent exacerbations.

Non-Pharmacologic

Smoking cessation is the most important intervention. It is the only thing proven to slow FEV1 decline.

Pulmonary rehabilitation improves exercise capacity and quality of life.

Supplemental oxygen is indicated if PaO2 is 55 or less, or 55 to 59 with cor pulmonale or polycythemia.

COPD Exacerbation
Management

Increase bronchodilators. Give systemic corticosteroids for 5 days. Prednisone 40 milligrams daily is standard.

Antibiotics are indicated if there is increased sputum purulence or volume, or if the patient requires mechanical ventilation.

Typical antibiotics include azithromycin, doxycycline, or a respiratory fluoroquinolone.

Use noninvasive ventilation for respiratory acidosis. NIV reduces intubation rates and mortality.

2. Asthma‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Diagnosis and Control
Diagnosis

Asthma is characterized by variable airflow obstruction that is reversible. Spirometry shows increased FEV1 by 12 percent and 200 mL after bronchodilator.

Symptoms include episodic wheeze, cough, dyspnea, and chest tightness, often worse at night or with triggers.

Methacholine challenge can confirm diagnosis when spirometry is normal. A positive test shows PC20 less than 4 milligrams per mL.

Step Therapy

Step 1 for intermittent asthma is as-needed SABA or as-needed low-dose ICS plus formoterol.

Step 2 is daily low-dose ICS or as-needed ICS-formoterol.

Step 3 is low-dose ICS-LABA.

Step 4 is medium-dose ICS-LABA.

Step 5 is high-dose ICS-LABA plus add-on therapies like LAMA, biologics, or low-dose oral steroids.

Severe Asthma
Biologic Therapies

Omalizumab is anti-IgE for allergic asthma with elevated IgE.

Mepolizumab, benralizumab, and reslizumab target IL-5 or its receptor for eosinophilic asthma with eosinophils 150 or higher.

Dupilumab targets IL-4 and IL-13 for eosinophilic asthma or asthma with elevated FeNO.

Tezepelumab targets TSLP and works across multiple phenotypes.

Acute Asthma Exacerbation
ED Management

Give continuous nebulized albuterol or albuterol MDI with spacer every 20 minutes times 3.

Give systemic corticosteroids early. Prednisone 40 to 60 milligrams or IV methylprednisolone.

Add ipratropium for severe exacerbations.

Magnesium sulfate 2 grams IV for severe exacerbations not responding to initial therapy.

Consider intubation for impending respiratory failure. A normal or rising PCO2 in acute asthma is a danger sign.

3. Interstitial Lung Disease‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Classification
Major Categories

Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis is the most common idiopathic interstitial pneumonia. It has a poor prognosis.

Connective tissue disease-associated ILD occurs with rheumatoid arthritis, scleroderma, myositis, and Sjogren syndrome.

Hypersensitivity pneumonitis is caused by inhaled organic antigens like mold, bird proteins, or bacteria.

Sarcoidosis is a multisystem granulomatous disease most common in young African Americans and Scandinavians.

Drug-induced ILD can be caused by amiodarone, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin, and bleomycin.

Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis
Diagnosis

IPF presents with progressive dyspnea and dry cough in patients typically over 60 years old.

Physical exam reveals bibasilar velcro-like crackles and digital clubbing.

HRCT shows usual interstitial pneumonia pattern with basal-predominant honeycombing, traction bronchiectasis, and minimal ground glass.

If HRCT is definitive for UIP, lung biopsy is not needed.

Treatment

Antifibrotic therapy with pirfenidone or nintedanib slows FVC decline but does not reverse fibrosis.

Corticosteroids are NOT indicated for IPF and may be harmful.

Lung transplant referral for appropriate candidates.

Sarcoidosis
Clinical Features

Sarcoidosis can affect any organ but lungs are involved in over 90 percent of cases.

Classic findings include bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, erythema nodosum, uveitis, and hypercalcemia.

Lofgren syndrome is the combination of bilateral hilar adenopathy, erythema nodosum, and arthralgias. It has excellent prognosis.

Diagnosis requires compatible clinical picture plus noncaseating granulomas on biopsy plus exclusion of other causes.

Treatment

Many patients do not require treatment. Observe if asymptomatic with stable disease.

Treat for symptomatic pulmonary disease, cardiac involvement, neurologic involvement, or hypercalcemia.

Corticosteroids are first-line. Methotrexate or azathioprine for steroid-sparing.

4. Pulmonary Hypertension‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Classification
WHO Groups

Pulmonary hypertension is defined as mean pulmonary artery pressure greater than 20 on right heart catheterization.

Group 1 is pulmonary arterial hypertension, or PAH. This includes idiopathic PAH, connective tissue disease-associated PAH, and drug-induced PAH.

Group 2 is PH due to left heart disease. This is the most common cause of PH overall.

Group 3 is PH due to lung disease or hypoxia, such as COPD or ILD.

Group 4 is chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension, or CTEPH.

Group 5 is PH due to unclear or multifactorial mechanisms.

Diagnosis and Treatment
Workup

Echo is the initial screening test. It estimates PA systolic pressure and assesses RV function.

Right heart catheterization is required to confirm diagnosis and classify type.

V/Q scan is essential to rule out CTEPH. It is more sensitive than CT for chronic PE.

Group 1 PAH Treatment

PAH-specific therapies target three pathways: endothelin, nitric oxide, and prostacyclin.

Endothelin receptor antagonists include ambrisentan and bosentan.

PDE-5 inhibitors include sildenafil and tadalafil. Riociguat is a soluble guanylate cyclase stimulator.

Prostacyclin analogs include epoprostenol, treprostinil, and iloprost. Selexipag is a prostacyclin receptor agonist.

Combination therapy is standard for most PAH patients.

CTEPH

CTEPH is curable with pulmonary thromboendarterectomy surgery in appropriate candidates.

All patients with unexplained PH should have V/Q scan to rule out CTEPH.

5. Pleural Disease‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Pleural Effusion Analysis
Light's Criteria

Light's criteria distinguish exudative from transudative effusions. An effusion is exudative if any one criterion is met.

First, pleural protein divided by serum protein greater than 0.5.

Second, pleural LDH divided by serum LDH greater than 0.6.

Third, pleural LDH greater than two-thirds the upper limit of normal serum LDH.

Transudates are caused by hydrostatic or oncotic pressure changes: heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome.

Exudates are caused by pleural inflammation or impaired drainage: infection, malignancy, PE, connective tissue disease.

Parapneumonic Effusion and Empyema
When to Drain

Uncomplicated parapneumonic effusions may resolve with antibiotics alone.

Complicated parapneumonic effusions require drainage. Features include pH less than 7.2, glucose less than 60, positive Gram stain, loculations, or pus.

Empyema is frank pus in the pleural space and always requires drainage.

Options for drainage include thoracentesis, chest tube with or without fibrinolytics, or surgery for refractory cases.

6. Pulmonary Embolism‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Diagnosis
Diagnostic Approach

Use pretest probability with Wells score or Geneva score to guide testing.

If low pretest probability, check D-dimer. If negative, PE is excluded.

Age-adjusted D-dimer cutoff is age times 10 for patients over 50.

CT pulmonary angiography is the test of choice if D-dimer is positive or pretest probability is intermediate to high.

V/Q scan is an alternative for patients with contrast allergy or renal insufficiency.

Treatment
Anticoagulation

DOACs are first-line for most patients with PE. Options are apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, or dabigatran.

LMWH or fondaparinux is preferred for cancer-associated thrombosis, though DOACs are increasingly used.

Warfarin requires heparin bridge until INR is therapeutic for 2 days.

Duration is 3 months for provoked PE, indefinite for unprovoked PE or recurrent VTE.

Massive PE

Massive PE presents with hypotension, defined as systolic BP less than 90 or drop of 40 or more for 15 minutes.

Submassive PE has RV dysfunction or elevated troponin without hypotension.

Systemic thrombolysis is indicated for massive PE without contraindications.

Catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy are alternatives.

7. Sleep Disorders‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Obstructive Sleep Apnea
Diagnosis

OSA is diagnosed by polysomnography showing apnea-hypopnea index of 5 or more with symptoms, or AHI of 15 or more without symptoms.

Home sleep testing is acceptable for uncomplicated patients with high pretest probability.

Severity is classified as mild with AHI 5 to 15, moderate with AHI 15 to 30, and severe with AHI greater than 30.

Treatment

CPAP is first-line therapy for moderate to severe OSA and reduces cardiovascular events and improves quality of life.

Weight loss, positional therapy, and avoiding alcohol and sedatives are adjunctive measures.

Oral appliances are an option for mild to moderate OSA or CPAP-intolerant patients.

Upper airway surgery may be considered for select patients with anatomic obstruction.

8. ARDS & Mechanical Ventilation‚ñ∂Ô∏è
ARDS Definition
Berlin Criteria

ARDS is defined by acute onset within 1 week of known insult or new/worsening respiratory symptoms.

Bilateral opacities on chest imaging not fully explained by effusion, collapse, or nodules.

Respiratory failure not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload.

Severity is based on PaO2 to FiO2 ratio with PEEP 5 or higher. Mild is 200 to 300. Moderate is 100 to 200. Severe is less than 100.

Ventilator Management
Lung-Protective Ventilation

Low tidal volume ventilation with 6 mL per kilogram of predicted body weight reduces mortality. This is based on the ARMA trial.

Target plateau pressure less than 30 centimeters of water.

Allow permissive hypercapnia if needed to maintain low tidal volumes.

Use adequate PEEP to maintain oxygenation while avoiding overdistension.

Additional Interventions

Prone positioning for 16 or more hours per day improves survival in moderate to severe ARDS. This is based on the PROSEVA trial.

Neuromuscular blockade may be considered early in severe ARDS.

Conservative fluid management after initial resuscitation improves oxygenation and shortens ventilator duration.

ECMO is a salvage therapy for refractory hypoxemia.

Ventilator Liberation
Weaning

Daily spontaneous awakening trials and spontaneous breathing trials reduce time on the ventilator.

Rapid shallow breathing index less than 105 predicts successful extubation.

Assess for cuff leak before extubation if prolonged intubation or difficult airway.

9. Lung Cancer‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Screening and Diagnosis
Screening Guidelines

Low-dose CT screening is recommended for adults 50 to 80 years old with 20 pack-year smoking history who currently smoke or quit within 15 years.

Annual screening reduces lung cancer mortality by 20 percent.

Evaluation of Pulmonary Nodules

Nodule management depends on size, appearance, and patient risk factors.

Solid nodules less than 6 millimeters in low-risk patients need no follow-up.

Ground glass nodules have lower malignancy risk but require longer follow-up.

PET scan is useful for nodules 8 millimeters or larger. SUV greater than 2.5 suggests malignancy.

Types and Treatment
Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer

NSCLC accounts for 85 percent of lung cancers. Subtypes include adenocarcinoma, squamous cell, and large cell.

Early stage is treated with surgical resection plus adjuvant chemotherapy for stage 2 or higher.

Molecular testing is essential. EGFR mutations, ALK rearrangements, and other targets guide therapy.

Immunotherapy with PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors has transformed treatment of advanced NSCLC.

Small Cell Lung Cancer

SCLC is strongly associated with smoking and has aggressive behavior.

Limited stage is confined to one hemithorax. Treat with concurrent chemoradiation.

Extensive stage is treated with chemotherapy plus immunotherapy.

Prophylactic cranial irradiation reduces brain metastases in responders.

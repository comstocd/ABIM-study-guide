üîç Cancer Screening & Prevention
‚ñº
‚úÖ Evidence-Based Screening (USPSTF Grade A/B)
‚úì
Lung Cancer USPSTF A
Annual low-dose CT: Age 50-80, ‚â•20 pack-years, current smoker or quit <15 years. Stop if quit >15 years or limited life expectancy.
‚úì
Colorectal Cancer USPSTF A
Start age 45, stop at 75 (individualize 76-85). Options: Colonoscopy q10y, FIT annually, or Cologuard q3y. Colonoscopy NOT superior to FIT for average-risk screening.
‚úì
Breast Cancer USPSTF B
Mammography every 2 years, ages 40-74. Annual mammography not proven superior to biennial.
‚úì
Cervical Cancer USPSTF A
Age 21-29: Pap q3y. Age 30-65: Pap + HPV q5y (preferred) or Pap alone q3y. Stop at 65 if adequate prior screening.
üö´ Low-Value Screening (Avoid)
‚úó
PSA Screening for Prostate Cancer USPSTF C
Do not routinely screen. Shared decision-making for men 55-69. Do NOT screen men ‚â•70 or with <10-year life expectancy.
‚úó
Ovarian Cancer Screening USPSTF D
Do not screen with CA-125 or transvaginal ultrasound in average-risk women. Causes more harm than benefit.
‚úó
Thyroid Cancer Screening USPSTF D
Do not screen asymptomatic adults with neck palpation or ultrasound.
‚úó
Pancreatic Cancer Screening
Do not screen average-risk adults. No proven effective screening method.
‚úó
Whole-Body CT Screening
Never recommended. High false positive rate, radiation exposure, incidentalomas, unnecessary procedures.
üìå When to STOP Screening
  • Life expectancy <10 years (cancer screening unlikely to provide benefit)
  • Colonoscopy: After age 75 (or 85 max)
  • Pap smear: After age 65 with adequate prior screening
  • Mammography: Individualize after 75; stop if limited life expectancy
üì∑ Diagnostic Imaging
‚ñº
üö´ Avoid Unnecessary Imaging
‚úó
Acute Low Back Pain (<6 weeks) Choosing Wisely
No imaging without red flags (trauma, neurologic deficits, cancer history, fever, IV drug use, osteoporosis, age >50 with new symptoms, prolonged steroid use).
‚úó
Uncomplicated Headache Choosing Wisely
No imaging for stable headache patterns without red flags. Red flags: thunderclap, neurologic signs, papilledema, fever, immunocompromise, cancer, age >50 new onset.
‚úó
Syncope Without High-Risk Features Choosing Wisely
No head CT or carotid imaging for typical vasovagal syncope. Focus on cardiac workup if concerning features.
‚úó
Minor Head Injury Choosing Wisely
Use Canadian CT Head Rule to avoid unnecessary CT. Low-risk patients (GCS 15, no LOC, no amnesia, no vomiting) do not need imaging.
‚úó
Preoperative CXR Choosing Wisely
Not routine. Only if acute cardiopulmonary symptoms or needed for specific surgical planning.
‚úó
Routine Follow-up Imaging After Incidental Findings
Follow Fleischner (pulmonary nodules), Bosniak (renal cysts), ACR (adrenal incidentaloma) guidelines. Many small findings need no follow-up.
‚úÖ Use Clinical Decision Rules
Condition Decision Rule Purpose
Pulmonary Embolism Wells + D-dimer (PERC if very low risk) Avoid CT in low-risk patients
Ankle/Foot Injury Ottawa Ankle Rules Reduce unnecessary X-rays by 30-40%
Knee Injury Ottawa Knee Rules Rule out fracture clinically
C-Spine Injury NEXUS or Canadian C-Spine Rule Avoid CT in low-risk trauma
Head Injury Canadian CT Head Rule Identify who needs CT
üìå Imaging Pearls
  • MRI is NOT always "better" than CT ‚Äî choose based on clinical question
  • Contrast is often unnecessary and adds risk (nephropathy, allergy)
  • PET scans should not be used for cancer screening
  • Repeat imaging rarely needed if prior study was normal and symptoms unchanged
üß™ Laboratory Testing
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Laboratory Tests
‚úó
Routine Daily Labs in Stable Hospitalized Patients Choosing Wisely
Order labs only when results will change management. Daily BMP/CBC not needed in stable patients.
‚úó
Routine Preoperative Testing Choosing Wisely
No routine CBC, BMP, coags, or UA before low-risk surgery in healthy patients. Test only if indicated by history/exam.
‚úó
BNP for Routine Dyspnea Evaluation
BNP is most useful when HF probability is intermediate. Don't use in clear-cut cases (obvious HF or clearly non-cardiac).
‚úó
D-dimer in High-Risk PE Patients
Skip D-dimer if Wells score high ‚Äî go directly to imaging. D-dimer only useful in low/moderate pretest probability.
‚úó
ESR/CRP for Non-specific Symptoms
Don't order as "fishing expeditions." Useful for specific inflammatory conditions (PMR, GCA, RA monitoring), not general workup.
‚úó
ANA Without Clinical Suspicion for CTD Choosing Wisely
ANA is positive in many healthy people. Only order if clinical features suggest SLE, scleroderma, or other CTD.
‚úó
Lyme Serology Without Appropriate Exposure/Symptoms Choosing Wisely
High false positive rate. Only test with compatible clinical syndrome AND endemic exposure.
‚úÖ When Testing Adds Value
‚úì
HbA1c for Diabetes Screening
Screen adults 35-70 who are overweight/obese. More convenient than fasting glucose, one-time test.
‚úì
Lipid Panel for CV Risk Assessment
Non-fasting lipids acceptable for initial screening. Repeat fasting only if triglycerides very high.
‚úì
High-Sensitivity Troponin
Enables faster rule-out of MI with 0/1-hour or 0/3-hour protocols. Reduces length of stay.
ü´Ä Cardiology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Cardiac Testing
‚úó
Stress Testing in Asymptomatic Low-Risk Patients Choosing Wisely
Do not perform routine stress testing in patients without symptoms. Does not improve outcomes.
‚úó
Routine Preoperative Stress Testing ACC/AHA
Only if it will change management. Most patients can proceed to surgery with medical optimization.
‚úó
Annual ECGs in Asymptomatic Adults Choosing Wisely
Not recommended for routine screening. Leads to false positives and unnecessary testing.
‚úó
Echocardiogram for Uncomplicated Hypertension Choosing Wisely
Not routine. Reserve for suspected HF, valvular disease, or hypertensive emergency with LV dysfunction concern.
‚úó
Coronary Calcium Score in Known CAD
No added value if CAD already established. Useful only for risk stratification in intermediate-risk asymptomatic patients.
‚úó
Routine Follow-up Imaging After Stable Stent
No routine stress testing after PCI if asymptomatic. Only if new or worsening symptoms.
‚úÖ High-Value Cardiology Practices
‚úì
Exercise ECG Before Imaging
If patient can exercise and has interpretable ECG, start with exercise ECG (not imaging stress test). Cheaper, no radiation.
‚úì
Generic Statins
Atorvastatin and rosuvastatin are generic, high-potency, and first-line for ASCVD prevention.
‚úì
Rate Control First for Afib
Rate control is non-inferior to rhythm control for most patients (AFFIRM trial). Less costly, fewer side effects.
‚úì
DOACs Over Warfarin for Afib
Non-inferior or superior efficacy, less monitoring, lower ICH risk. Exception: Mechanical valve or severe MS ‚Äî use warfarin.
üìå Stent Duration ‚Äî Current Guidelines
  • Stable CAD with DES: DAPT √ó 6 months (can shorten to 1-3 months if high bleeding risk)
  • ACS with DES: DAPT √ó 12 months (can extend if high ischemic, low bleeding risk)
  • After DAPT: Single antiplatelet indefinitely (aspirin or clopidogrel alone)
ü´Å Pulmonology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Pulmonary Practices
‚úó
CT Pulmonary Angiography in Low-Risk PE Choosing Wisely
Use PERC rule first. If PERC negative, no testing needed. If Wells low + D-dimer negative, no CTPA.
‚úó
Routine Preoperative PFTs ATS
Not routine before non-thoracic surgery. Clinical assessment is sufficient for most patients.
‚úó
Antibiotics for Acute Bronchitis Choosing Wisely
No antibiotics for acute bronchitis in healthy adults. Viral in >90% of cases. Offer supportive care.
‚úó
Chest X-ray for Every COPD Exacerbation
Not routine for typical exacerbations. Only if pneumonia, pneumothorax, or other complication suspected.
‚úó
Continuous Pulse Oximetry in Stable Patients Choosing Wisely
Discontinue when stable. Leads to alarm fatigue and unnecessary interventions.
‚úó
Aggressive Follow-up of Tiny Pulmonary Nodules
Per Fleischner 2017: Solid nodules <6mm in low-risk patients need no follow-up CT.
‚úÖ High-Value Pulmonary Practices
‚úì
NIV for AECOPD
BiPAP reduces intubation and mortality in AECOPD with respiratory acidosis. Cost-effective.
‚úì
Smoking Cessation Counseling
Most cost-effective intervention in medicine. Pharmacotherapy (varenicline, NRT) doubles quit rates.
‚úì
Inhaler Technique Assessment
Check technique before escalating therapy. Up to 90% of patients use inhalers incorrectly.
üìå Supplemental O2 ‚Äî When It's Needed
  • LTOT criteria: PaO2 ‚â§55 or SpO2 ‚â§88% at rest (or 56-59 with cor pulmonale)
  • Ambulatory O2: Only if documented exertional desaturation AND improved with O2
  • Don't prescribe O2 for dyspnea without hypoxemia ‚Äî no benefit
üü† Gastroenterology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value GI Practices
‚úó
Repeat Colonoscopy Too Soon AGA
Follow guidelines: Normal = 10 years. 1-2 small adenomas = 7-10 years. 3-4 small adenomas = 3-5 years. Advanced adenoma = 3 years.
‚úó
EGD for Uncomplicated GERD Choosing Wisely
No EGD for typical GERD symptoms responding to PPI. Only for alarm features (dysphagia, weight loss, anemia, age >60 new onset).
‚úó
Long-term PPI Without Indication Choosing Wisely
Re-evaluate PPI need annually. Step down or discontinue if no clear indication. Use lowest effective dose.
‚úó
CT Abdomen for Typical IBS Symptoms
Clinical diagnosis. Rome IV criteria. Only image if red flags (weight loss, rectal bleeding, anemia, age >50 new onset).
‚úó
Routine Stool Testing for All Diarrhea
Most acute diarrhea is self-limited and viral. Test only if: >7 days, bloody, severe, immunocompromised, recent antibiotics, hospitalized.
‚úÖ High-Value GI Practices
‚úì
FIT for CRC Screening
Annual FIT is an acceptable alternative to colonoscopy for average-risk screening. Patient preference matters.
‚úì
Treat H. pylori to Prevent Ulcer Recurrence
Eradication eliminates need for long-term PPI in H. pylori-related PUD. Cost-effective.
‚úì
Non-invasive Fibrosis Assessment
FIB-4, NAFLD fibrosis score, or elastography before liver biopsy for NAFLD/MASLD staging.
ü¶† Infectious Disease
‚ñº
üö´ Antibiotic Stewardship ‚Äî Avoid
‚úó
Antibiotics for Viral URI Choosing Wisely
No antibiotics for common cold, viral pharyngitis (if Strep rapid test/culture negative), or acute sinusitis <10 days.
‚úó
Antibiotics for Asymptomatic Bacteriuria Choosing Wisely
Do NOT treat except in pregnancy or before urologic procedures. Treating ASB increases antibiotic resistance.
‚úó
Prolonged Antibiotic Courses
Shorter courses are often equivalent: CAP 5 days, cellulitis 5-6 days, UTI 3-5 days. Use shortest effective duration.
‚úó
Fluoroquinolones as First-line for Uncomplicated UTI Choosing Wisely
Reserve for complicated UTI/pyelonephritis. Use nitrofurantoin or TMP-SMX for uncomplicated cystitis.
‚úó
Urine Culture for Typical Cystitis in Young Women
Treat empirically. Culture only if recurrent, complicated, or treatment failure.
‚úó
IV Antibiotics When Oral Equivalent Exists
Oral step-down early. Many infections (including osteomyelitis, endocarditis in select cases) can complete therapy PO.
‚úÖ High-Value ID Practices
‚úì
Procalcitonin-Guided Antibiotic Duration
Can safely shorten antibiotic courses in pneumonia and sepsis. Stop when procalcitonin <0.25 or drops >80%.
‚úì
Generic Antibiotics
Most antibiotics are generic. Brand-name rarely justified. Doxycycline, amoxicillin, TMP-SMX are highly cost-effective.
‚úì
Hepatitis C Screening and Treatment
Universal screening once for adults. Cure prevents cirrhosis and HCC ‚Äî highly cost-effective despite drug cost.
üìå Antibiotic Duration ‚Äî Evidence-Based Short Courses
  • Uncomplicated UTI: 3 days (nitrofurantoin 5 days)
  • Community-acquired pneumonia: 5 days (if afebrile 48-72h and stable)
  • Uncomplicated cellulitis: 5-6 days
  • Intra-abdominal infection (source controlled): 4 days
  • VAP: 7 days
üß† Neurology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Neurology Practices
‚úó
Imaging for Primary Headache Choosing Wisely
No MRI/CT for stable migraine or tension headache without red flags.
‚úó
EEG for Syncope Without Seizure Features Choosing Wisely
Low yield. Only order if clinical features suggest seizure (witnessed tonic-clonic activity, tongue bite, prolonged confusion).
‚úó
Carotid Imaging for Non-hemispheric Symptoms
Carotid disease doesn't cause dizziness, syncope, or confusion. Only image for hemispheric stroke/TIA symptoms.
‚úó
Routine Seizure Prophylaxis After Stroke
Not recommended. Only treat if seizure occurs. Prophylaxis does not prevent late epilepsy.
‚úó
Brand-Name ASMs When Generic Available
Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, carbamazepine, valproate, phenytoin ‚Äî all generic and effective.
‚úÖ High-Value Neurology Practices
‚úì
HINTS Exam for Acute Vestibular Syndrome
More sensitive than early MRI for posterior stroke. Skilled exam can avoid unnecessary imaging.
‚úì
Epley Maneuver for BPPV
Highly effective, costs nothing. Avoids unnecessary medications, imaging, and specialist referrals.
‚úì
Generic Triptans
Sumatriptan is generic and effective. Reserve branded or newer agents for treatment failures.
ü¶¥ Rheumatology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Rheumatology Practices
‚úó
ANA Testing Without Clinical Suspicion Choosing Wisely
ANA is positive in 15-20% of healthy people. Only test with clinical features of CTD.
‚úó
Lyme Testing for Non-specific Symptoms Choosing Wisely
High false positive rate leads to unnecessary treatment. Test only with compatible syndrome + endemic exposure.
‚úó
X-rays for Acute Low Back Pain <6 Weeks
Clinical assessment only unless red flags. Most acute LBP resolves without imaging.
‚úó
MRI for Non-specific Back Pain Without Red Flags
MRI finds "abnormalities" in most asymptomatic adults. Leads to unnecessary interventions.
‚úó
DEXA Before Age 65 in Average-Risk Women
Screen at 65, or younger if risk factors (FRAX). Not routine in younger women.
‚úÖ High-Value Rheumatology Practices
‚úì
Methotrexate as First-Line DMARD
Effective, inexpensive, well-studied. Use before biologics in RA. Anchor drug in many regimens.
‚úì
Generic Allopurinol for Gout
First-line ULT, generic. Febuxostat and pegloticase reserved for allopurinol failures/intolerance.
‚úì
Acetaminophen and Topical NSAIDs for OA
Start with safest options. Topical diclofenac effective for knee OA with fewer systemic effects.
üî¨ Endocrinology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Endocrine Practices
‚úó
Routine Thyroid Screening in Asymptomatic Adults USPSTF I
Insufficient evidence for routine TSH screening. Test if symptomatic or high-risk.
‚úó
Multiple Daily Glucose Checks in Stable T2DM on Oral Agents
Minimal benefit in non-insulin-treated T2DM. HbA1c is sufficient for monitoring.
‚úó
Sliding Scale Insulin Alone Choosing Wisely
Reactive, not proactive. Always add basal insulin for hospitalized patients with diabetes.
‚úó
Vitamin D Screening in Low-Risk Adults
Not recommended routinely. Supplement empirically if high-risk (malabsorption, limited sun, elderly).
‚úó
Testosterone Testing for Fatigue Without Other Signs
Low testosterone common with aging. Only test if clinical hypogonadism suspected (specific symptoms + signs).
‚úÖ High-Value Endocrine Practices
‚úì
Metformin as First-Line for T2DM
Generic, effective, weight-neutral, CV benefits. First-line unless contraindicated.
‚úì
SGLT2i/GLP-1 RA for Cardiorenal Protection
Add early if ASCVD, HF, or CKD ‚Äî benefit beyond glucose lowering. Now considered high-value despite cost.
‚úì
Levothyroxine (Generic)
Brand-name not superior. Generic levothyroxine is effective and far less expensive.
‚úì
Observation for Small Thyroid Nodules
Per ATA guidelines: Nodules <1 cm (or <1.5 cm without suspicious features) ‚Äî observation, not biopsy.
üíß Nephrology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Nephrology Practices
‚úó
Renal Ultrasound for Isolated AKI Without Obstruction Concern
Low yield if no clinical suspicion for obstruction (anuria, known stones, pelvic malignancy). History determines need.
‚úó
Stopping ACEi/ARB Permanently for Small Cr Rise
Up to 30% Cr increase is acceptable when starting ACEi/ARB. Long-term renoprotection outweighs short-term rise.
‚úó
Routine 24-Hour Urine for Proteinuria Quantification
Spot urine protein/Cr ratio is sufficient for most clinical decisions. 24-hour collection cumbersome and often inaccurate.
‚úó
NSAIDs in CKD
Avoid in CKD stage 3+. Nephrotoxic, worsen hypertension, cause hyperkalemia. Use alternatives.
‚úÖ High-Value Nephrology Practices
‚úì
SGLT2 Inhibitors for CKD
Slow CKD progression regardless of diabetes status (DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY). Now standard of care.
‚úì
ACEi/ARB for Proteinuric CKD
Proven to slow progression. Continue unless hyperkalemia or Cr rise >30%.
‚úì
Avoid Contrast Nephropathy Overtreatment
Risk often overstated. Volume expansion helps, but don't delay necessary imaging. Hold metformin only if eGFR <30.
ü©∏ Hematology
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Hematology Practices
‚úó
Liberal Blood Transfusion Choosing Wisely
Restrictive strategy (Hgb <7) is safe and non-inferior in most patients. Liberal (Hgb <8) for ACS, symptomatic anemia.
‚úó
Hypercoagulability Workup After Provoked VTE
Not indicated for VTE with clear provoking factor (surgery, immobility). Won't change management.
‚úó
Routine Preoperative Coagulation Testing Choosing Wisely
PT/INR/PTT not needed unless history of bleeding, liver disease, or anticoagulant use.
‚úó
Iron Studies for Mild Anemia Without Symptoms
Check MCV first. If microcytic, then iron studies. If normocytic, consider other causes before extensive workup.
‚úÖ High-Value Hematology Practices
‚úì
IV Iron for Iron Deficiency in HF/CKD
Improves symptoms and reduces hospitalizations even without anemia. More effective than oral iron.
‚úì
DOACs for VTE Treatment
No monitoring needed, fewer interactions than warfarin. Exception: mechanical valve, antiphospholipid syndrome.
‚úì
Observation for Low-Grade Lymphomas
"Watch and wait" for asymptomatic follicular lymphoma or CLL. Treatment doesn't prolong survival if asymptomatic.
üë¥ Geriatrics & Deprescribing
‚ñº
üö´ Medications to Avoid in Elderly (Beers Criteria)
‚úó
Benzodiazepines & Non-Benzo Hypnotics Choosing Wisely
Avoid for insomnia. Falls, fractures, cognitive impairment. Use sleep hygiene, CBT-I instead.
‚úó
Anticholinergics (Diphenhydramine, Oxybutynin, TCAs)
High anticholinergic burden ‚Üí delirium, falls, cognitive decline. Cumulative effect of multiple agents.
‚úó
Proton Pump Inhibitors Long-Term
Re-evaluate annually. Associated with C. diff, fractures, hypomagnesemia. Use lowest dose, shortest duration.
‚úó
Tight Glycemic Control in Frail Elderly
HbA1c target 7.5-8.5% in limited life expectancy. Hypoglycemia risk outweighs benefit of tight control.
‚úó
Aggressive BP Lowering in Frail Elderly
SPRINT enrolled robust elders. Individualize targets ‚Äî orthostatic hypotension and falls are real risks.
‚úÖ Deprescribing Opportunities
‚úì
Stop Statins if Life Expectancy <1-2 Years
No benefit in terminal illness. Reduces pill burden, interactions, and costs.
‚úì
Stop Bisphosphonates After 5 Years (Oral) or 3 Years (IV)
"Drug holiday" ‚Äî benefits persist, reduces atypical fracture risk.
‚úì
Reassess Aspirin for Primary Prevention
Not recommended for most adults >60 for primary prevention (ASPREE, ARRIVE). Bleeding risk increases with age.
‚úì
Review and Stop Unnecessary Vitamins/Supplements
Most have no proven benefit. Reduce polypharmacy and costs.
üìå Deprescribing Questions to Ask
  • Is there still a valid indication for this medication?
  • Is the benefit likely to be realized given life expectancy?
  • Are there potential harms that outweigh benefits?
  • What are the patient's goals and preferences?
üè• Perioperative Medicine
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Perioperative Testing
‚úó
Routine Preoperative Testing in Healthy Patients Choosing Wisely
No routine CBC, BMP, PT/INR, UA, CXR, ECG for low-risk surgery in healthy patients. Test only if indicated.
‚úó
Stress Testing Before Low/Intermediate-Risk Surgery ACC/AHA
Rarely changes management. For elevated-risk surgery: test only if result will change care (cancel or revascularize).
‚úó
PFTs Before Non-Thoracic Surgery
Clinical assessment is sufficient. PFTs rarely change management. Optimize asthma/COPD clinically.
‚úó
Cardiology "Clearance"
Not a thing. Risk assessment and optimization is the goal, not "clearance." Anesthesia clears for surgery.
‚úÖ High-Value Perioperative Practices
‚úì
Use Functional Capacity Assessment
Can patient climb 2 flights of stairs? If yes (‚â•4 METs), low cardiac risk regardless of comorbidities.
‚úì
Continue Beta-Blockers Perioperatively
Don't stop chronic beta-blockers (withdrawal risk). Don't start de novo before surgery (POISE harm).
‚úì
Appropriate Antibiotic Prophylaxis Timing
Within 60 min of incision. Redose if surgery prolonged. Stop within 24h (48h for cardiac surgery).
üìå When Preoperative ECG IS Indicated
  • Known CAD, arrhythmia, or structural heart disease
  • New cardiac symptoms
  • High-risk surgery (vascular, thoracic) in patients with risk factors
  • NOT routine for low-risk surgery regardless of age
üõèÔ∏è Hospital Medicine
‚ñº
üö´ Low-Value Inpatient Practices
‚úó
Routine Daily Labs Choosing Wisely
Order only when results will change management. Daily phlebotomy causes iatrogenic anemia.
‚úó
Telemetry for Low-Risk Patients Choosing Wisely
Use AHA guidelines. Most patients don't need continuous monitoring. Causes alarm fatigue.
‚úó
Foley Catheters Without Indication Choosing Wisely
Remove as soon as possible. CAUTI is preventable. "Patient convenience" is not an indication.
‚úó
Strict Bed Rest for Most Conditions
Early mobilization improves outcomes. Bed rest causes deconditioning, VTE, delirium, pressure ulcers.
‚úó
Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis in Low-Risk Patients Choosing Wisely
Not routine. Reserve for ICU with risk factors (coagulopathy, mechanical ventilation, burns, TBI). PPI overuse causes C. diff.
‚úÖ High-Value Inpatient Practices
‚úì
VTE Prophylaxis for ALL Hospitalized Patients
Pharmacologic or mechanical depending on bleeding risk. One of most effective preventive measures.
‚úì
Glycemic Control Without Hypoglycemia
Target 140-180 mg/dL in most hospitalized patients. Hypoglycemia is harmful. Avoid sliding scale alone.
‚úì
Discharge Planning Starting Day 1
Reduces length of stay, readmissions. Engage case management early for complex patients.
‚úì
Medication Reconciliation
At admission and discharge. Prevents errors, inappropriate continuation of inpatient-only meds.

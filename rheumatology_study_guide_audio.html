1. Rheumatoid Arthritis‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Diagnosis
Clinical Features

RA presents with symmetric inflammatory polyarthritis affecting small joints of hands and feet.

Classic involvement is MCP and PIP joints with sparing of DIP joints. Morning stiffness lasting more than 1 hour is typical.

Extra-articular manifestations include rheumatoid nodules, interstitial lung disease, Felty syndrome, and vasculitis.

Serology

Rheumatoid factor is sensitive but not specific. It is positive in 70 to 80 percent of RA patients.

Anti-CCP antibodies are more specific and predict erosive disease. They can be positive before clinical symptoms.

Seronegative RA with negative RF and anti-CCP occurs in about 20 percent of cases.

Treatment
DMARD Therapy

Methotrexate is first-line DMARD. Start early to prevent joint destruction.

Give folic acid supplementation to reduce side effects. Monitor CBC and LFTs.

If inadequate response, add or switch to biologic DMARD: TNF inhibitors, abatacept, tocilizumab, or rituximab.

JAK inhibitors tofacitinib and baricitinib are oral alternatives to biologics.

2. SLE‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Diagnosis
ACR/EULAR Criteria

SLE diagnosis requires positive ANA plus clinical and immunologic criteria with total score 10 or more.

Clinical domains include constitutional, hematologic, neuropsychiatric, mucocutaneous, serosal, musculoskeletal, and renal.

Key antibodies are anti-dsDNA which correlates with disease activity and lupus nephritis, and anti-Smith which is highly specific.

Low complements C3 and C4 suggest active disease.

Key Manifestations
Lupus Nephritis

Renal involvement occurs in about 50 percent of SLE patients and is a major cause of morbidity.

Class 3 and 4 proliferative nephritis require aggressive immunosuppression with mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide.

All lupus patients should receive hydroxychloroquine unless contraindicated.

Antiphospholipid Syndrome

APS causes arterial and venous thrombosis plus pregnancy morbidity.

Diagnose with lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin antibodies, or anti-beta-2-glycoprotein antibodies positive on 2 occasions 12 weeks apart.

Treatment is anticoagulation. Warfarin is preferred for arterial events. DOACS are acceptable for venous events.

3. Vasculitis‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Large Vessel Vasculitis
Giant Cell Arteritis

GCA affects patients over 50, typically with new headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, and visual symptoms.

ESR and CRP are markedly elevated. Temporal artery biopsy shows granulomatous inflammation.

Start high-dose steroids immediately if clinical suspicion is high. Do not wait for biopsy. Vision loss can be permanent.

Tocilizumab is steroid-sparing and reduces relapse risk.

ANCA-Associated Vasculitis
GPA, MPA, EGPA

GPA, formerly Wegener's, affects upper airways, lungs, and kidneys. PR3-ANCA, or c-ANCA, is positive in 90 percent.

MPA causes pulmonary-renal syndrome without upper airway involvement. MPO-ANCA, or p-ANCA, is typically positive.

EGPA, formerly Churg-Strauss, presents with asthma, eosinophilia, and vasculitis. MPO-ANCA positive in about 40 percent.

Treatment is rituximab or cyclophosphamide plus steroids for induction. Rituximab or azathioprine for maintenance.

4. Crystal Arthropathies‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Gout
Diagnosis and Treatment

Acute gout presents with rapid onset of severe monoarticular arthritis, classically the first MTP joint or podagra.

Synovial fluid shows negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals.

Acute treatment options are NSAIDs, colchicine, or steroids. Choose based on comorbidities.

Do not start or stop urate-lowering therapy during an acute flare. It can prolong or worsen the attack.

Urate-lowering therapy with allopurinol or febuxostat is indicated for frequent attacks, tophi, or uric acid stones. Target uric acid less than 6.

Pseudogout
CPPD Disease

Calcium pyrophosphate deposition disease presents similarly to gout but often affects the knee or wrist.

Synovial fluid shows positively birefringent rhomboid-shaped crystals.

X-ray shows chondrocalcinosis, which is calcification of cartilage.

Associated with hemochromatosis, hyperparathyroidism, and hypomagnesemia.

Treatment of acute attacks is similar to gout. No urate-lowering equivalent exists for CPPD.

5. Spondyloarthropathies‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Ankylosing Spondylitis
Key Features

AS presents with inflammatory back pain in young adults. Pain is worse at rest, improves with exercise, and causes morning stiffness.

HLA-B27 is positive in over 90 percent of cases.

X-ray findings include sacroiliitis and syndesmophytes leading to bamboo spine in advanced disease.

MRI is more sensitive for early sacroiliitis before X-ray changes.

Treatment is NSAIDs first, then TNF inhibitors or IL-17 inhibitors like secukinumab.

Reactive Arthritis
Classic Triad

Reactive arthritis presents with arthritis, urethritis, and conjunctivitis. Often occurs after GI or GU infection.

Common triggers are Chlamydia, Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, and Yersinia.

Treatment is NSAIDs. Prolonged antibiotics may be used for Chlamydia-triggered cases.

6. Myopathies & Scleroderma‚ñ∂Ô∏è
Inflammatory Myopathies
DM vs PM

Both dermatomyositis and polymyositis present with proximal muscle weakness and elevated CK.

Dermatomyositis has characteristic skin findings: heliotrope rash around eyes, Gottron papules over knuckles, shawl sign, and V-sign.

Dermatomyositis is associated with underlying malignancy. Screen for cancer especially in adults.

Anti-Jo-1 antibody is associated with interstitial lung disease, mechanics hands, and arthritis.

Treatment is high-dose steroids plus steroid-sparing agents like methotrexate or azathioprine.

Systemic Sclerosis
Limited vs Diffuse

Limited cutaneous SSc, formerly CREST, affects skin distal to elbows and knees. Associated with pulmonary hypertension and anti-centromere antibody.

Diffuse cutaneous SSc has widespread skin involvement and higher risk of renal crisis and interstitial lung disease. Associated with anti-Scl-70 antibody.

Scleroderma renal crisis presents with hypertensive emergency and AKI. Treat with ACE inhibitors.
